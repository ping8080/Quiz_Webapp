Question No: 1

A patient with an acute myocardial infarction (MI) shows the following ECG pattern: an atrial rate of 82 (regular), a ventricular rate of 46 (regular), normal P waves and QRS complexes, but no consistent relationship between the P waves and QRS complexes. What dysrhythmia does the nurse identify this as and what treatment is expected?

Options:

  • 1.

    Sinus bradycardia treated with atropine

  • 2.

    Third-degree heart block treated with a pacemaker

  • 3.

    Atrial fibrillation treated with electrical cardioversion

  • 4.

    Type I second-degree AV block treated with observation  

Solution:

  • Third-degree heart block treated with a pacemaker

    • In third-degree (complete) heart block, there is complete dissociation between atrial and ventricular activity—P waves and QRS complexes occur independently. The atrial impulses do not conduct to the ventricles, causing a slower ventricular rate. The definitive treatment is the insertion of a pacemaker to restore proper coordination between atrial and ventricular contractions.

    Incorrect Options:

    • Sinus bradycardia treated with atropine: Sinus bradycardia originates from the sinoatrial node and presents with a slow but regular heart rhythm, maintaining a consistent P wave before each QRS complex. There’s no atrioventricular dissociation, unlike in third-degree heart block.
    • Atrial fibrillation treated with electrical cardioversion: Atrial fibrillation is characterized by an irregularly irregular rhythm without distinct P waves. Instead, there are fibrillatory waves caused by chaotic atrial activity. This doesn’t match the ECG description of regular atrial and ventricular rates with normal P waves.
    • Type I second-degree AV block treated with observation: In Type I second-degree AV block (Mobitz I or Wenckebach), the PR interval progressively lengthens until a QRS complex is dropped, followed by a repeating cycle. In the described scenario, the PR interval is inconsistent without this progressive prolongation, which is characteristic of third-degree heart block.

    Slow breathing (bradypnea) and pale skin (pallor)

  • 3.

  • 4.

Solution:

    • In cases of acute aspirin (salicylate) poisoning, the body’s metabolism accelerates, leading to an elevated body temperature (hyperpyrexia). This rapid, deep breathing helps to expel excess carbon dioxide and temporarily balance the pH levels. The child may also appear flushed due to increased metabolic activity.

    Incorrect Options:

    • Slow breathing (bradypnea) and pale skin (pallor): This option is incorrect because aspirin toxicity typically leads to hyperventilation, not slow breathing. The body attempts to correct metabolic acidosis by increasing respiratory rate. Additionally, pallor is not an early sign; instead, the child may appear flushed due to increased circulation from metabolic stimulation.
    • Low body temperature and bleeding: While aspirin toxicity can lead to bleeding tendencies because of its effect on platelet function, bleeding is not an early symptom. Moreover, the child’s body temperature is expected to rise, not fall, due to increased metabolic activity. Hypothermia is not characteristic of acute aspirin overdose.

Bradycardia is also not typical; in fact, the child is more likely to experience tachycardia (fast heart rate) due to the metabolic stress and acidosis associated with aspirin poisoning.

    Key Takeaway: Aspirin poisoning in young children often presents with signs of metabolic acidosis, including hyperventilation and hyperpyrexia. Early recognition of these symptoms is critical for prompt medical intervention, as aspirin toxicity can progress rapidly, leading to severe complications.

Options:

  • 1.

    Observe the child and offer assistance if needed

  • 2.

    Position the child to lie down

  • 3.

    Call for help and take the child back to the room

  • 4.

    Help the child stand up

Solution:

    • Children with tetralogy of Fallot often instinctively squat during episodes of decreased oxygenation, commonly referred to as "tet spells" or hypercyanotic episodes. Since this is a compensatory mechanism, the nurse should calmly observe the child, provide reassurance, and assist only if the child requires help maintaining the position or experiences increased distress. This response allows the child to manage symptoms effectively without unnecessary intervention.

    Incorrect Options:

    • Position the child to lie down: Lying down would worsen the child’s symptoms by decreasing systemic vascular resistance, leading to increased right-to-left shunting and reduced oxygenation. This position would counteract the benefits of squatting, making the hypoxic episode more severe.
    • Call for help and take the child back to the room: While it’s important to monitor the child closely, this situation does not require an emergency response. The child’s behavior is typical for managing cyanotic episodes in tetralogy of Fallot. Moving the child unnecessarily could increase stress and exacerbate symptoms.
    • Help the child stand up: Standing reduces the systemic vascular resistance that squatting helps to increase, potentially worsening hypoxia. Forcing the child into a standing position would interfere with the body’s natural compensatory mechanism and could lead to further distress.

    Key Takeaway: In children with tetralogy of Fallot, squatting during cyanotic episodes is an instinctive, self-correcting behavior that improves oxygenation. The best approach is to remain calm, observe the child, and intervene only if the episode worsens or if the child requires additional support.

A young adult patient is admitted to the psychiatric unit after becoming extremely withdrawn and discontinuing attendance at college. Options:

  • 1.

  • 2.

    Ask her if she has any questions about the unit or what she is expected to do.

  • 3.

    Hold her hand and guide her around the unit to orient her.

Solution:

    • The patient shows signs of a psychotic episode, potentially involving hallucinations and severe withdrawal. In such cases, the patient may struggle to process detailed information or engage in complex interactions. Providing concise, essential information helps reduce cognitive overload and creates a sense of structure without overwhelming her. Sitting quietly nearby demonstrates a calm, non-threatening presence, fostering a sense of security and building trust, which is crucial in establishing therapeutic rapport with psychotic patients.

    Incorrect Options:

    • Option A) Explain the unit routines to her in detail: A patient in a psychotic state often has impaired concentration and cognitive processing. Providing detailed explanations will likely be ineffective and overwhelming. The focus should be on simplicity and clarity until the patient stabilizes enough to absorb more comprehensive information.
    • Option B) Ask her if she has any questions about the unit or what she is expected to do: Patients experiencing psychosis may not be fully oriented to reality, making it difficult for them to identify or articulate questions. This approach assumes a level of cognitive clarity that the patient currently lacks. A more passive, supportive presence is appropriate initially.
    • Option D) Hold her hand and guide her around the unit to orient her: Physical contact with a psychotic patient can be perceived as intrusive or threatening, potentially escalating agitation or paranoia. It’s important to maintain a respectful distance unless the patient initiates contact or there is a clear therapeutic need. Orientation should be done verbally and with patience, avoiding unnecessary physical touch.

    Key Takeaway: When caring for patients experiencing psychosis, the priority is to create a calm, supportive environment. Brief, simple communication paired with a non-intrusive presence helps build trust and minimizes the risk of increasing the patient’s anxiety or confusion. Physical touch and overwhelming details should be avoided unless clinically indicated.
