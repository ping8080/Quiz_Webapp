Question No: 1

A nurse is reviewing the serum laboratory results of a 7-year-old child who has undergone rehydration following episodes of acute vomiting and diarrhea. Which change in laboratory values should the nurse anticipate as an indicator of successful rehydration?

Options:

  • 1.

    Hemoglobin increased from 9.5 mg/dL to 12.5 mg/dL.

  • 2.

    Hematocrit increased from 32% to 37%.

  • 3.

    Blood urea nitrogen (BUN) decreased from 28 mg/dL to 16 mg/dL.

  • 4.

    Serum creatinine (SCr) decreased from 1.2 mg/dL to 0.9 mg/dL.

Solution:

    • A decrease in BUN to a normal range indicates successful rehydration. For children, normal BUN levels typically range from 4 to 17 mg/dL.
    • Elevated levels of hemoglobin, hematocrit, BUN, and creatinine are common in cases of acute diarrhea due to hemoconcentration, and these values should normalize following rehydration. Normal hemoglobin levels for children are between 11.5 to 15.5 g/dL, while normal hematocrit values range from 35% to 45%.
    • Additionally, a normal SCr for children is typically between 0.3 to 0.7 mg/dL, indicating that the previously elevated SCr may not have fully normalized.
    • Thus, the most significant indicator of effective rehydration is the reduction in BUN.

Question No: 2

When reviewing the laboratory results of an 83-year-old patient, which finding would raise the greatest concern for the nurse?

Options:

  • 1.

    Platelets 150,000/μL

  • 2.

    Serum iron 50 mcg/dL

  • 3.

    Partial thromboplastin time (PTT) 60 seconds

  • 4.

    Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) 35 mm in 1 hour

Solution:

    • Option a. Platelets 150,000/μL: This value is within the normal range for platelet count. In adults, normal platelet levels typically range from 150,000 to 450,000 platelets per microliter of blood. Aging does not significantly affect platelet counts, so this finding is not a cause of concern. A count of 150,000/μL indicates that the patient has an adequate number of platelets for hemostasis.
    • Option b. Serum iron 50 mcg/dL: While this serum iron level is on the lower side, it is not unusual for older adults to have slightly decreased serum iron levels. Factors such as diet, chronic illness, and age can contribute to variations in serum iron. Although low iron may warrant further investigation to determine if it is contributing to anemia or other health issues, it is not an immediate concern compared to abnormal coagulation parameters.
    • Option c. Partial thromboplastin time (PTT) 60 seconds: This finding is the most concerning for the nurse. The normal range for activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT) is usually around 30 to 40 seconds, depending on the laboratory standards. An elevated PTT of 60 seconds indicates a significant delay in the clotting process. This can be indicative of various conditions, such as the presence of anticoagulants (e.g., heparin), liver dysfunction, or bleeding disorders. In an elderly patient, a prolonged PTT increases the risk of bleeding, which can have serious consequences.
    • Option d. Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) 35 mm in 1 hour: The ESR is a non-specific test that measures inflammation in the body. While a rate of 35 mm/hr is elevated (normal is typically less than 20 mm/hr for men and 30 mm/hr for women), it is important to note that the ESR often increases with age and can be influenced by various factors, including chronic diseases or infections. While it may indicate an inflammatory process, it is not as critical as the elevated PTT in terms of immediate risk to the patient.

Question No: 10

A nurse evaluates a patient's arterial blood gas (ABG) results, which are as follows: pH 7.38; PO2 88 mmHg; PCO2 33 mmHg; HCO3 24 mEq/L; and O2 saturation 96%. What is the appropriate interpretation of these results?

Options:

  • 1.

    Metabolic acidosis

  • 2.

    Metabolic alkalosis

  • 3.

    Normal values

  • 4.

    Respiratory alkalosis

Solution:

  • The correct interpretation of these arterial blood gas results is c) Normal values. When analyzing ABG results, the nurse assesses several parameters, including pH, partial pressure of oxygen (PO2), partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PCO2), bicarbonate (HCO3), and oxygen saturation (O2 saturation).

    • pH: The normal range for arterial blood pH is approximately 7.35 to 7.45. A pH of 7.38 indicates a slight decrease but is still within the acceptable range.
    • PO2: A normal PO2 range is 75 to 100 mmHg. The client's PO2 of 88 mmHg is within normal limits, indicating adequate oxygenation.
    • PCO2: The normal range for PCO2 is 35 to 45 mmHg. A PCO2 of 33 mmHg suggests slight hyperventilation, but it remains within a range that does not indicate a significant acid-base imbalance.
    • HCO3: The normal bicarbonate range is 22 to 26 mEq/L. An HCO3 of 24 mEq/L indicates normal metabolic function.
    • O2 Saturation: A normal oxygen saturation level is typically between 95% and 100%. With an O2 saturation of 96%, the client demonstrates adequate oxygenation.

    Summary: All the values presented fall within the normal ranges for arterial blood gases. Although the pH is slightly on the lower end, it does not signify an acid-base disorder when considered alongside the other parameters. Thus, the overall assessment shows that there is no acid-base imbalance present, making option c) Normal values the correct answer.

    The other options are incorrect for the following reasons:

    • Option a) Metabolic acidosis: This condition is characterized by a low pH (typically < 7.35), low HCO3, and potentially elevated PCO2 as a compensatory mechanism. The provided values do not support this diagnosis.
    • Option b) Metabolic alkalosis: This condition is indicated by a high pH (typically > 7.45) and elevated HCO3. Since the pH and HCO3 levels are normal in this case, this interpretation is also incorrect.
    • Option d) Respiratory alkalosis: This would typically show a high pH (> 7.45) and a low PCO2. Here, the pH is slightly low, ruling out this diagnosis.

    In conclusion, the ABG results indicate that the client is within normal limits, which is an essential aspect of clinical assessment in nursing practice.

Question No: 14

How should the nurse interpret laboratory results consisting of a positive Coombs’ test, decreased hematocrit (Hct), increased reticulocyte count, and increased bilirubin? These results are indicative of:

Options:

  • 1.

    Hereditary spherocytosis

  • 2.

    Autoimmune hemolytic anemia

  • 3.

    Sickle cell disease

  • 4.

    Acute lymphocytic leukemia

Solution:

  • The combination of a positive Coombs’ test, decreased Hct, increased reticulocyte count, and increased bilirubin suggests the presence of autoimmune hemolytic anemia (AIHA).

    • Positive Coombs’ Test: This test detects antibodies attached to red blood cells, indicating an immune response against them, which is characteristic of AIHA.
    • Decreased Hct: This reflects a reduction in the number of red blood cells, which is common in hemolytic anemias due to increased destruction of these cells.
    • Increased Reticulocyte Count: This indicates that the bone marrow is responding to anemia by producing more red blood cells, which is a compensatory mechanism in response to hemolysis.
    • Increased Bilirubin: The breakdown of hemoglobin from destroyed red blood cells results in elevated bilirubin levels, further supporting the diagnosis of hemolytic anemia.

    Discussion of Other Options:

    • Option a) Hereditary spherocytosis: While this condition also shows increased fragility of red blood cells and elevated reticulocyte counts, it typically presents with spherocytes (abnormally shaped red blood cells) on a blood smear.     Explanation of Incorrect Answers:

    • Option d) WBC 20,000, segmented neutrophils 58%: Although the WBC count is high, the segmented neutrophil percentage remains within a range that would suggest a less severe infection, as severe bacterial infections typically show a higher percentage of band neutrophils.

Which of the following urinalysis findings would most likely suggest for renal dysfunction?

    Positive leukocyte esterase

  • 2.

    Positive urobilinogen

    Positive protein

  • 4.

Solution:

    Explanation of Incorrect Answers:

    • Positive nitrites: Nitrites in the urine are a common sign of bacterial infection in the urinary tract, specifically with certain bacteria that convert nitrates to nitrites, and do not directly indicate renal dysfunction.
