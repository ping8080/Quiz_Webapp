Question No: 12

What is the antidote of opioid poisoning?

Options:

  • 1.

    Naloxone

  • 2.

    Calcium gluconate

  • 3.

    Activated charcoal

  • 4.

    Sodium bicarbonate

Solution:

    • Naloxone is an opioid antagonist that works by reversing the effects of opioid toxicity. It binds to opioid receptors in the brain (specifically the mu-opioid receptors) and displaces opioids from these receptors. This results in the reversal of opioid-induced respiratory depression, sedation, and other toxic effects. Naloxone is often used in emergency settings to manage opioid overdose. It can be administered intravenously, intramuscularly, or intranasally.

    Distractors:

    • Calcium Gluconate: Calcium gluconate is used to treat calcium channel blocker poisoning, hyperkalemia, or magnesium toxicity, not opioid poisoning. It works by restoring calcium levels and stabilizing the cardiac membrane potential but has no effect on opioid receptors.
    • Activated charcoal is a treatment option for certain types of poisoning (e.g., drugs or toxins that are still in the gastrointestinal tract). It works by absorbing the poison to prevent its absorption into the bloodstream. However, it is not effective for opioid poisoning because opioids are rapidly absorbed into the bloodstream, and activated charcoal does not neutralize their effects on opioid receptors.
    • Sodium bicarbonate is used to treat conditions like metabolic acidosis or drug overdoses involving tricyclic antidepressants or salicylates, but it is not the antidote for opioid toxicity. It works by buffering acid and raising blood pH but does not counteract the toxic effects of opioids.

Question No: 13

What is the antidote of methanol poisoning?

Options:

  • 1. Fomepizole
  • 2. Physostigmine
  • 3. Flumazenil
  • 4. Methylene blue 1%

Solution:

    • Fomepizole is the first-line antidote for methanol poisoning. Methanol is metabolized by alcohol dehydrogenase into formaldehyde and formic acid, which are highly toxic compounds. Fomepizole works by inhibiting the action of alcohol dehydrogenase, thereby preventing the conversion of methanol into its toxic metabolites. By blocking this metabolic pathway, fomepizole helps prevent the harmful effects of methanol toxicity, including metabolic acidosis, central nervous system depression, and visual disturbances.

      Distractors:

    • Physostigmine is a cholinesterase inhibitor that is used to treat anticholinergic toxicity, such as poisoning from drugs like atropine or scopolamine. It is not effective in treating methanol poisoning, which is primarily related to the accumulation of toxic metabolites formed from alcohol dehydrogenase activity, not acetylcholine inhibition.
    • Flumazenil is a benzodiazepine antagonist used to reverse the effects of benzodiazepine overdose, such as sedation or respiratory depression. It is not effective in treating methanol poisoning, as methanol’s toxicity is unrelated to benzodiazepine receptor activity.
    • Methylene blue is used in the treatment of methemoglobinemia, where it acts as a reducing agent to convert methemoglobin back to normal hemoglobin. It is not used to treat methanol poisoning, which involves the toxic effects of methanol metabolites like formaldehyde and formic acid. Methylene blue does not address the underlying pathophysiology of methanol toxicity.

Question No: 18

Which of the following is done to confirm the placement of Endotracheal tube after intubation?

Options:

  • 1. Capnography
  • 2. Chest X-ray
  • 3. Bilateral lung auscultation
  • 4. Ultrasound

Solution:

    • Capnography is the most reliable and immediate method for confirming endotracheal tube (ETT) placement after intubation. This method uses end-tidal CO₂ (ETCO₂) monitoring to confirm that the ETT is in the trachea and that gas exchange is occurring in the lungs. The presence of ETCO₂ is the surest sign that the tube is correctly placed and the patient is ventilating effectively. This technique provides real-time confirmation, making it the preferred choice in emergency and critical care settings.

      Signs of Correct Placement of ETT:

    • Chest Lift: Bilateral and Equal: A correct placement of the tube results in a bilateral chest rise during ventilation. Equal chest expansion confirms the tube is not in the right mainstem bronchus or esophagus, but in the trachea.
    • Mist Formation Inside ETT: The presence of condensation or mist inside the tube indicates humidified exhaled air, confirming that the ETT is in the trachea and air is passing through the lungs.
    • Auscultate: Bilateral Equal Air Entry: Bilateral breath sounds heard during auscultation suggest that the tube is in the trachea, with airflow reaching both lungs. However, this method can be less definitive than capnography, as it may still detect air in the lungs even if the tube is in the right bronchus or esophagus.
    • Capnography (ETCO₂): Surest Sign: Capnography provides real-time feedback on the presence of CO₂ in the exhaled air. If ETCO₂ is detected, it confirms that the tube is in the trachea and that ventilation is effective. This is the gold standard and the most reliable method to confirm ETT placement immediately after intubation.
    • Fibre Optic Bronchoscopy: Gold Standard: Fibre optic bronchoscopy is considered the gold standard for confirming ETT placement, as it allows direct visualization of the tube within the trachea. This method is often reserved for specialized procedures, such as lung surgeries like pneumonectomy or lobectomy, and may not be immediately available in emergency settings.
    • Last CXR: Used in ICU: A Chest X-ray (CXR) is useful for confirming the depth and placement of the ETT in the trachea. It is commonly used in the ICU to ensure the tube is not in the right mainstem bronchus or esophagus, but it is not a real-time tool for confirming placement.

      Distractors:

    • Chest X-ray is useful for confirming the tube’s position in the trachea, but it is not a real-time method. It requires imaging equipment and can cause delays in confirming tube placement, making it is less ideal in critical situations.
    • Auscultation can identify the presence of breath sounds but may not be reliable if the tube is in the right bronchus or esophagus. It can also be influenced by external noise, making it a less definitive method compared to capnography.
    • Ultrasound is not typically used to confirm ETT placement, although it may be employed in certain specialized settings. It lacks the immediacy and ease of use of capnography and is not considered a standard tool for this purpose.
