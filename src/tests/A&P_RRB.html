Question No: 1

Which of the following is the outermost layer of the skin?

Options:

  • 1.

    Dermis

  • 2.

    Epidermis

  • 3.

    Hypodermis

  • 4.

    Stratum basale

Solution:

    • The epidermis is the outermost layer of the skin. It serves as the first line of defense against environmental factors such as pathogens, UV radiation, and water loss. It is made up of five distinct layers, each with a specific function in skin protection and regeneration.
    • Layers of Epidermis-
      • Stratum Basale (Basal Layer): This is the deepest layer of the epidermis, located just above the dermis. It consists of a single layer of columnar or cuboidal cells called keratinocytes, which are actively dividing to produce new cells. This layer also contains melanocytes (cells that produce the pigment melanin) and Merkel cells (responsible for touch sensation).
      • Stratum Spinosum: The cells in this layer are polygonal in shape and connected by desmosomes, which give the layer a "spiny" appearance under the microscope. Keratinocytes in this layer are starting to produce keratin (a tough protein).
      • Stratum Granulosum: This layer is made up of flattened cells. helps to create a barrier to water loss.
      • Stratum Lucidum: This is a thin, translucent layer found only in areas of thick skin (like the palms of the hands and soles of the feet). It consists of dead keratinocytes that have lost their nuclei and organelles and are filled with keratin, providing extra protection.
      • Stratum Corneum: This is the outermost layer of skin. It is made up of several layers of dead keratinocytes that have been flattened and filled with keratin.  
    • The dermis is the layer beneath the epidermis and contains connective tissue, hair follicles, blood vessels, and nerve endings.
    • The hypodermis (also called the subcutaneous layer) is the deepest layer, made up of fat and connective tissue that helps insulate the body and absorb shock.

Question No: 2

The subcutaneous layer primarily consists of:

Options:

  • 1.

    Dense connective tissue

  • 2.

    Adipose tissue

  • 3.

    Keratinized epithelial cells

  • 4.

    Cartilage tissue

Solution:

    • The subcutaneous layer (also called the hypodermis) is the deepest layer of the skin, located beneath the dermis. It primarily consists of adipose tissue (fat cells) and areolar connective tissue. The adipose tissue in this layer serves several functions:
      • Insulation: It helps to insulate the body and maintain body temperature.
      • Cushioning: It acts as a cushion to protect underlying muscles and organs from physical shock.
      • Energy storage: It stores energy in the form of fat, which the body can use when needed.
    • Dense connective tissue is found in the dermis, providing structural support and strength, but it is not the primary component of the hypodermis.
    • Keratinized epithelial cells are found in the epidermis, particularly in the stratum corneum, where they form the outermost layer of dead, flattened skin cells.
    • Cartilage tissue is a type of connective tissue that is found in places such as joints, ears, and the nose, but it is not found in the subcutaneous layer.

Question No: 3

What is the primary effect of vasodilation on blood pressure?

Options:

  • 1.

    Increases blood pressure

  • 2.

    Decreases blood pressure

  • 3.

    Has no effect on blood pressure

  • 4.

    Increases heart rate but does not affect blood pressure

Solution:

    • Vasodilation refers to the widening of blood vessels, particularly the arteries and arterioles. This process is typically triggered by factors like relaxation of smooth muscle in the vessel walls, certain hormones (e.g., nitric oxide), or medications.
    • When blood vessels dilate (widen), the resistance to blood flow decreases, allowing blood to flow more freely and easily through the vessels. This reduction in resistance leads to a decrease in blood pressure because the heart doesn't have to work as hard to pump blood through the wider vessels. Therefore, the primary effect of vasodilation is to decrease blood pressure.  
    • Vasoconstriction is the constriction of blood vessels which will increase resistance and thus raise blood pressure.

Options:

Solution:

It is made up of three key components of lung volume- Tidal volume + Inspiratory reserve volume + Expiratory reserve volume.
    • Lung volume and lung capacities: Lung volume refers to the volume of gas in the lungs at a given time during the respiratory cycle. Lung capacities are derived from a summation of different lung volumes. These are essential measurements used to assess respiratory health and lung function.

    Lung volume

    Lung capacities

    Tidal Volume (TV)

    Amount of air inhaled or exhaled during normal, relaxed breathing

    Total Lung Capacity (TLC)

    Total volume of air the lungs can hold

    TLC = TV + IRV + ERV + RV

    Inspiratory Reserve Volume (IRV)

    Additional amount of air that can be inhaled after a normal inspiration

    Maximum amount of air that can be exhaled after taking a deep breath in

    Additional amount of air that can be exhaled after a normal exhalation

    Maximum amount of air that can be inhaled after a normal exhalation

    Amount of air left in the lungs after a maximal exhalation

    Volume of air remaining in the lungs after a normal exhalation

Which structure in the ear is responsible for converting sound vibrations into electrical signals?

Options:

  • 1.

    Tympanic membrane

  • 2.

    Cochlea

  • 3.

    Auditory canal

  • 4.

    Semi-circular canals

Solution:

    • The cochlea is the structure in the inner ear responsible for converting sound vibrations into electrical signals that can be interpreted by the brain.
    • In the cochlea, the vibrations create waves in the fluid inside, which moves tiny hair cells within the cochlear structure.
    • The movement of these hair cells generates electrical signals that are transmitted via the auditory nerve to the brain for processing and interpretation as sound.
    • Tympanic membrane transmits the vibrations to the ossicles.
    • The auditory canal directs sound waves toward the eardrum, but it does not convert vibrations into electrical signals.
    • Semi-circular canals are part of the vestibular system and are responsible for maintaining balance.

Question No: 7

The correct order of auditory ossicles from tympanic membrane to oval window is: 

Options:

  • 1.

    Malleus → Incus → Stapes

  • 2.

    Incus → Malleus → Stapes

  • 3.

    Malleus → Stapes → Incus

  • 4.

    Stapes → Malleus → Incus

Solution:

    • The auditory ossicles are three small bones in the middle ear that transmit sound vibrations from the tympanic membrane (eardrum) to the inner ear, specifically to the oval window of the cochlea.
      • Malleus (hammer): This bone is directly attached to the tympanic membrane (eardrum), and it receives vibrations from the eardrum.
      • Incus (anvil): The malleus is connected to the incus, and the vibrations are transmitted from the malleus to the incus.
      • Stapes (stirrup): The incus is connected to the stapes, which is the smallest bone in the body. The stapes transmits the vibrations to the oval window, which leads into the inner ear.
    • The vibration travels from tympanic membrane (eardrum) to the malleus, then to the incus, and finally to the stapes, which transmits the vibrations to the oval window of the cochlea.
    • So, the correct order is Malleus → Incus → Stapes.

Options:

  • 1.

    Peripheral vision

  • 2.

    Focusing on close objects

  • 3.

    Sharp and central vision

  • 4.

    Adjusting the pupil size

Solution:

    • The macula is a small, specialized area located in the center of the retina at the back of the eye. It is responsible for sharp, central vision and allows us to see fine details clearly, such as when reading or recognizing faces. The central part of the macula is called the fovea, which contains a high concentration of cone cells, specialized for color vision and detailed perception.   
    • Peripheral vision is primarily provided by the areas of the retina outside the macula, where there are more rod cells, which are better at detecting motion and light in low-contrast environments.
    • Focusing on close objects is managed by the lens of the eye, not the macula. The lens changes shape to focus light on the retina.
    • Adjusting the pupil size is controlled by the iris and the pupillary reflex.

Options:

  • 1.

    To increase the pressure inside the left atrium during systole

  • 2.

    To prevent a drop in pressure in the aorta during diastole

  • 3.

    To prevent backflow from left ventricle to left atrium during systole

  • 4.

    To add additional blood from left atrium to left ventricle during atrial systole

Solution:

    • The mitral valve is a flap of tissue that sits in the left side of the heart, between the left atrium and the left ventricle. It keeps blood flowing in the forward direction, from the atrium into the ventricle.

    • Mitral valve disease can cause blood to flow backward into the left atrium (mitral regurgitation). It may also mean that not enough blood can pass through from the left atrium into the left ventricle (mitral valve stenosis).

    • It is too big a molecule to be absorbed through the plasma membrane: While insulin is a large molecule, this option is less accurate. The main reason it cannot be given orally is the digestive breakdown, not its size.

  • 2.

    Efferent arteriole, glomerulus, afferent arteriole, peritubular capillaries, vasa recta

Solution:

    • Afferent arteriole brings blood into the glomerulus, and then to glomerular capillaries.
    • The glomerulus has unusually high pressure relative to other capillary beds. It is the only capillary bed that has both an efferent arteriole (instead of the expected efferent venule). This high pressure helps drive the continued movement of fluid from the blood, across the filtration membrane, and into Bowman’s capsule.     • After passing through the renal corpuscle, the capillaries form a second arteriole, the efferent arteriole.

    • Peritubular capillaries and vasa recta have a more standard anatomical arrangement, with afferent arterioles and efferent venules. Because of this, they also have a more typical blood pressure, which is substantially lower than the pressure in glomeruli.

Question No: 49

Which of the following is the correct sequence of phases in mitosis? 

Options:

  • 1.

    Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase

  • 2.

    Metaphase → Anaphase → Prophase → Telophase

  • 3.

    Telophase → Anaphase → Metaphase → Prophase

  • 4.

    Prophase → Anaphase → Metaphase → Telophase

Solution:

    • Mitosis is a process of cell division that results in two genetically identical daughter cells. It occurs in four phases:
      1. Prophase: This is the first stage of mitosis, where the chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes. The nuclear envelope begins to break down, and the mitotic spindle (made of microtubules) starts to form. The centrioles move toward opposite poles of the cell.
      2. Metaphase: During this stage, the chromosomes align along the cell's equatorial plane (also called the metaphase plate). The spindle fibers attach to the centromeres of the chromosomes, ensuring that each sister chromatid is attached to a spindle fiber from opposite poles.
      3. Anaphase: In this phase, the sister chromatids are pulled apart toward opposite poles of the cell as the spindle fibers shorten. This separation ensures that each daughter cell will receive an identical set of chromosomes.  
      4. Telophase: The final stage of mitosis, where the chromatids reach the poles of the cell. The nuclear envelope re-forms around each set of chromosomes, which begin to de-condense back into chromatin. The mitotic spindle disintegrates, and the cell prepares for cytokinesis (division of the cytoplasm).

    Fig: Phases of Mitosis
