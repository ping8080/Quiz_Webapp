Question No: 1

Which endocrine gland is situated in the lower part of neck on the thyroid cartilage.

Options:

  • 1.

    Pituitary gland

  • 2.

    Adrenal gland

  • 3.

    Thyroid gland

  • 4.

    Pineal gland

Solution:

    • The thyroid gland is situated in the lower part of the neck, anterior to the trachea, and on the thyroid cartilage.
    • It consists of two lobes connected by an isthmus, positioned on either side of the trachea.
    • It is responsible for producing thyroid hormones (T3 and T4) and calcitonin, which regulate metabolism and calcium levels.

     

    Pituitary gland:

    • The pituitary gland is located in the brain within the sella turcica of the sphenoid bone, not in the neck.
    • It is the master endocrine gland that regulates other glands but does not correspond to the anatomical description in the question.

    Adrenal gland:

    • The adrenal glands (also known as suprarenal glands) are situated on top of the kidneys, in the retroperitoneal space.
    • The adrenal glands have two parts: the cortex and the medulla.
    • The cortex is the outer part of the gland. It produces the hormones cortisol and aldosterone. The medulla, meanwhile, is the inner part of the gland. It produces the hormones adrenaline and noradrenaline.

    Pineal gland:

    • The pineal gland is a small pea-shaped gland located in the epithalamus, near the center of the brain.
    • Regulates sleep-wake cycles and circadian rhythms by producing and releasing melatonin in response to darkness.

Solution:

    • The caecum is the first part of the large intestine, located at the junction of the ileum and the colon.

    • The sigmoid colon is an S-shaped segment of the large intestine, connecting the descending colon to the rectum.

    • The ascending colon is a vertical part of the large intestine, located on the right side of the abdomen.
    • It absorbs water and electrolytes from undigested food, aiding in the formation of solid waste. 

Question No: 3

Skeletal muscles are composed of a large number of:

Options:

  • 1.

    Cartilage

  • 2.

    Elastic fibers

  • 3.

    Inelastic fibers

  • 4.

    Dense fibers

Solution:

    • Skeletal muscles contain elastic fibers that allow them to stretch and recoil, but the primary structure responsible for muscle contraction is the muscle fibers (which consist of myofibrils made from actin and myosin).
    • The elasticity of muscle tissue aids in recoil after contraction, allowing muscles to return to their resting length after being stretched.

    Cartilage:

    • Cartilage is not a component of skeletal muscle; it is found in joints, the ear, nose, and between ribs.
    • It provides structural support but does not play a role in muscle contraction.

    Inelastic fibers:

    • Inelastic fibers, such as collagen, are found in tendons and ligaments but are not a component of skeletal muscle fibers.
    • Skeletal muscles require elasticity for contraction and relaxation, making inelastic fibers unsuitable for this purpose

    Dense fibers:

    • Dense fibers are more related to connective tissue (like in tendons and ligaments) and are not the main structural component of skeletal muscle.
    • Skeletal muscle fibers are primarily made of proteins like actin and myosin, not dense fibers.

Options:

    Glucose

  • 2.

    Fat

  • 3.

    Protein

  • 4.

    Lactic acid

Solution:

  • The primary source of energy for cardiac muscle is fatty acids. Cardiac muscle relies predominantly on aerobic metabolism and uses fatty acids as its main energy source under normal conditions.     • Lactic acid: Lactic acid can be utilized by cardiac muscle as an energy source, especially during anaerobic metabolism or hypoxia. However, it is not the primary source under normal conditions.

Question No: 18

Psycho-somatic illness can be differentiated from hysteria by:

Options:

  • 1.

    Altered sensorium

  • 2.

    Autonomic disturbances

  • 3.

    Involuntary movements

  • 4.

    Skeletal muscle atrophy

Solution:

    • Psycho-somatic illness and hysteria (now referred to as conversion disorder) can present with physical symptoms, but the key difference lies in how these symptoms manifest and are affected by psychological factors.
    • Autonomic disturbances (such as changes in heart rate, blood pressure, sweating, etc.) are more commonly associated with psycho-somatic illnesses.
    • These are physical symptoms caused or exacerbated by psychological factors, such as stress, anxiety, or emotional conflicts that affect the body's autonomic functions (like heart rate or blood pressure). These symptoms are real but have no identifiable medical cause.
    • In hysteria (conversion disorder), the physical symptoms are not typically associated with autonomic disturbances, but rather with motor or sensory symptoms that can be traced to a psychological conflict (like paralysis, blindness, or non-epileptic seizures).
    • Altered sensorium: Altered sensorium (such as confusion or delirium) is not a distinguishing feature between psycho-somatic illness and hysteria. This can occur in both conditions but is not characteristic of either in isolation.
    • Involuntary movements: Involuntary movements are more characteristic of conversion disorder (hysteria), where psychological factors can cause physical symptoms such as abnormal motor function. Psycho-somatic illnesses typically do not include such motor disturbances.
    • Skeletal muscle atrophy: Skeletal muscle atrophy is not a feature of either psycho-somatic illness or hysteria. Atrophy typically occurs in chronic physical conditions or diseases that result in muscle wasting, and is not associated with the psychological origins of these disorders.
