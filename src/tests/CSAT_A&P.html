Question No: 2

Which of the following cells in the cerebellar cortex is excitatory?

Options:

  • 1. Purkinje cells
  • 2. Granule cells
  • 3. Basket cells
  • 4. Stellate cells

Solution:

    • Granule cells are the only excitatory neurons in the cerebellar cortex. They release glutamate, an excitatory neurotransmitter, which stimulates Purkinje cells via their parallel fibers. This excitation is essential for cerebellar function, especially in motor coordination and learning.

      Distractors:

    • Purkinje cells: Purkinje cells are inhibitory. They release gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA), which inhibits the deep cerebellar nuclei. Their role is to modulate and fine-tune motor activity by providing inhibitory output.
    • Basket cells: Basket cells are interneurons that are inhibitory. They also release GABA, inhibiting the Purkinje cells by forming basket-like synapses around their cell bodies.
    • Stellate cells: Stellate cells are inhibitory interneurons located in the molecular layer. They release GABA, targeting the dendrites of Purkinje cells to regulate their activity.

      Key Points to Remember

    • All cells in the cerebellar cortex are inhibitory except granule cells.
    • Granule cells use glutamate (excitatory), while Purkinje, basket, and stellate cells use GABA (inhibitory).
    • This inhibitory and excitatory balance is crucial for cerebellar coordination of voluntary movements.

Question No: 4

The middle cerebellar peduncle primarily carries which fibers?

Options:

  • 1. Pontocerebellar fibers
  • 2. Spinocerebellar fibers
  • 3. Olivocerebellar fibers
  • 4. Vestibulocerebellar fibers

Solution:

    • Pontocerebellar fibers (A): The middle cerebellar peduncle (MCP) carries fibers originating from the pontine nuclei, which receive cortical input via corticopontine pathways. These fibers, known as pontocerebellar fibers, project to the contralateral cerebellar hemisphere. They are critical for integrating motor plans from the cerebral cortex with cerebellar functions, particularly in coordinating voluntary movements.

      Distractors:

    • Spinocerebellar fibers: These fibers convey proprioceptive information from the spinal cord to the cerebellum and are found in the inferior cerebellar peduncle (ICP) and the superior cerebellar peduncle (SCP), not the MCP. Examples include the anterior spinocerebellar tract (in the SCP) and the posterior spinocerebellar tract (in the ICP).
    • Olivocerebellar fibers: These fibers originate in the inferior olivary nucleus and are transmitted via the inferior cerebellar peduncle (ICP). They form climbing fibers, which synapse directly with Purkinje cells to modulate cerebellar output. They are not associated with the MCP.
    • Vestibulocerebellar fibers: These fibers transmit input from the vestibular nuclei and are crucial for balance and eye movement coordination. They travel through the inferior cerebellar peduncle (ICP) to reach the cerebellum. The MCP does not carry vestibulocerebellar fibers.

Question No: 5

The fibers of the superior cerebellar peduncle decussate at which structure?

Options:

  • 1. Medulla
  • 2. Pons
  • 3. Midbrain
  • 4. Thalamus

Solution:

    • The fibers of the superior cerebellar peduncle (SCP) decussate in the midbrain, specifically at the level of the inferior colliculus. These decussated fibers carry cerebellar outputs from the dentate, emboliform, and globose nuclei and project to the red nucleus and thalamus, enabling integration of cerebellar coordination with higher motor centers.

      Distractors:

    • Medulla: The medulla is involved in the decussation of pyramidal tracts (corticospinal tracts), not the SCP fibers. While the medulla plays a role in other cerebellar connections (e.g., inferior cerebellar peduncle), it is not the site for SCP decussation.

      Pons: The pons contains the middle cerebellar peduncle, which transmits pontocerebellar fibers to the cerebellum. However, no decussation of SCP fibers occurs at this level.

    • Thalamus: The SCP fibers terminate in the thalamus (specifically in the ventral lateral nucleus), but decussation has already occurred in the midbrain.

Question No: 13

The activity of the pacemaker in the gastrointestinal (GI) smooth muscles is primarily regulated by:

Options:

  • 1. Cholinergic system
  • 2. Adrenergic system
  • 3. Cajal cells
  • 4. Dopaminergic system

Solution:

    • The Cajal cells (also known as interstitial cells of Cajal) are specialized pacemaker cells located in the walls of the gastrointestinal tract. These cells are responsible for generating the slow waves or basal electrical rhythm (BER) that controls the rhythm of GI smooth muscle contractions. The fluctuation of the resting membrane potential of Cajal cells, typically between -60 mV to -40 mV, sets the pace for coordinated muscle contractions, making them the pacemakers of the GI tract.

      Distractors:

    • Cholinergic system: The cholinergic system can activate GI smooth muscles by releasing acetylcholine, which stimulates contraction. However, it does not directly regulate the pacemaker activity of the GI smooth muscles. The cholinergic system is involved in modulating the contractions initiated by the pacemaker activity of Cajal cells.
    • Adrenergic system: The adrenergic system generally inhibits GI smooth muscle contraction by releasing norepinephrine, which acts on the beta-adrenergic receptors. While this system plays a role in inhibiting motility, it does not regulate the pacemaker cells of the GI tract.
    • Dopaminergic system: The dopaminergic system can modulate GI motility, but it is not the primary regulator of pacemaker activity. It is involved more in the modulation of movement and functions within the nervous system but is not directly responsible for pacemaker activity in the GI smooth muscle.

Which of the following substances is the gold standard for estimating Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR)?

Options:

  • 1. Creatinine
  • 2. Urea
  • 3. Inulin
  • 4. Para-aminohippuric acid (PAH)

Solution:

    • Inulin is the ideal substance for estimating Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR) because it is freely filtered at the glomerulus, not reabsorbed, secreted, or metabolized by the renal tubules. This makes its clearance directly proportional to GFR, making it the gold standard for GFR estimation.

      Distractors:

    • Creatinine: Creatinine is commonly used to estimate GFR, but it is not perfect. It is freely filtered by the glomerulus but is also slightly secreted by the renal tubules, which can lead to an overestimation of GFR, especially in cases of renal dysfunction.
    • Urea: Urea is also filtered by the glomerulus, but it is reabsorbed in the renal tubules. This reabsorption can vary depending on factors such as hydration status and tubular function, which can affect its use for accurate GFR estimation.
    • Para-aminohippuric acid (PAH): PAH is used to estimate Renal Plasma Flow (RPF), not GFR. It is actively secreted by the renal tubules, and its clearance helps assess the renal plasma flow but does not directly estimate GFR.
