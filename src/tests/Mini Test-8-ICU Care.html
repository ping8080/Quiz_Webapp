Question No: 1

An adolescent with severe anorexia nervosa is admitted to an adolescent psychiatric unit after stabilization in intensive care to correct fluid and electrolyte imbalances. When creating the nursing care plan, which intervention should be given the highest priority?

Options:

  • 1. Weighing the patient before and after each meal
  • 2. Monitoring the patient for two hours after each meal
  • 3. Providing education on proper nutrition
  • 4. Documenting the patient’s food intake

Solution:

    • Option a) Weighing the patient before and after each meal: While tracking weight changes is important in the management of anorexia nervosa, weighing the patient before and after each meal is not the top priority. It may also exacerbate anxiety and obsessive behaviors in the patient.
    • Option b) Monitoring the patient for two hours after each meal: This is the highest priority because it helps ensure the patient does not engage in harmful behaviors such as vomiting or excessive exercise to purge calories. This intervention directly addresses behaviors that could compromise recovery.
    • Option c) Providing education on proper nutrition: Teaching about nutrition is valuable for long-term recovery, but it is not the most immediate concern during the early stages of treatment. Stabilizing harmful behaviors takes precedence.
    • Option d) Documenting the patient’s food intake: Recording food intake is necessary to track progress and ensure dietary goals are met. However, it is a supportive task and less urgent than preventing purging behaviors post-meal.

Question No: 2

At the end of an 8-hour shift in the neonatal intensive care unit, a nurse is calculating the total urine output of a preterm infant. The infant had four wet diapers during the shift. The weight of a dry diaper is 15 grams. The wet diapers weighed 30 grams, 24 grams, 21 grams, and 25 grams. What is the total 8-hour urine output for the infant?

Options:

  • 1.

    85 mL

  • 2.

    100 mL

  • 3.

    40 mL

  • 4.

    55 mL

Solution:

  • Explanation of the Calculation:

    Key Information:

    • 1 gram of diaper weight = 1 mL of urine.
    • Dry diaper weight = 15 grams.
    • Wet diaper weights: 30 grams, 24 grams, 21 grams, 25 grams.

    Steps:

    1. Subtract the dry diaper weight (15 grams) from each wet diaper weight:
      • 30 - 15 = 15 grams
      • 24 - 15 = 9 grams
      • 21 - 15 = 6 grams
      • 25 - 15 = 10 grams
    2. Add the urine weights:
      • 15 + 9 + 6 + 10 = 40 grams
    3. Since 1 gram equals 1 mL of urine, the total urine output is 40 mL.

A nurse is caring for a neonate in the NICU with an umbilical artery catheter (UAC) in place. To monitor for and minimize complications associated with the UAC, which interventions should the nurse prioritize?

  1. Check the catheter's position at every shift.
  2. Keep the tubing near the infant's lower limbs.
  3. Observe for redness or discoloration of the abdominal wall.
  4. Palpate femoral, pedal, and tibial pulses every 2 to 4 hours.
  5. Adjust the position of the tubing every hour.
  6. Regularly monitor blood glucose levels.

Options:

  • 1.

    1, 2, 4, and 6 only

  • 2.

    1, 3, 4, and 6 only

  • 3.

    1, 2, 5, and 6 only

  • 4.

    1, 3, 5, and 6 only

Solution:

    • Option 1) Check the catheter's position at every shift
      • Correct: The position of the catheter should be verified at the prescribed marking to ensure it remains secure and properly placed, reducing the risk of complications such as displacement or perforation.
    • Option 2) Keep the tubing near the infant's lower limbs
      • Incorrect: The tubing should be positioned away from the infant’s extremities to prevent accidental displacement caused by entanglement with fingers or toes.
    • Option 3) Observe for redness or discoloration of the abdominal wall
      • Correct: Discoloration or erythema may indicate abdominal complications, such as blood vessel perforation or infection, which are critical to detect early.
    • Option 4) Palpate femoral, pedal, and tibial pulses every 2 to 4 hours
      • Correct: Regular assessment of pulses and distal perfusion ensures that blood flow to the extremities is not compromised due to vascular alterations caused by the catheter.
    • Option 5) Adjust the position of the tubing every hour
      • Incorrect: Frequent manipulation of the catheter increases the risk of infection and should be avoided unless necessary for specific medical interventions.
    • Option 6) Regularly monitor blood glucose levels
      • Correct: Blood glucose monitoring is important as hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia may signal catheter misplacement or complications in glucose infusion, particularly in neonates.

    Summary

    The interventions in option B (1, 3, 4, and 6) prioritize safety by ensuring catheter stability, monitoring for complications, and maintaining proper circulation and glucose levels.

A nurse is accompanying the parents of a newborn to the NICU to visit their infant, who has been diagnosed with a terminal cardiac condition. Which intervention best supports parental attachment?

    Summary

    • Option B (Encourage the parents to actively participate in their newborn’s care) is the best choice as it helps parents establish a connection with their child, which is vital for emotional healing and coping, even in terminal cases.

Question No: 5

A patient is admitted to the ICU following a severe stroke and is receiving a continuous IV insulin infusion, titrated hourly based on blood glucose levels, to manage hyperglycemia. The patient’s spouse asks why insulin is being used when the patient is not diabetic. Which explanations should the nurse provide?

  1. “Stress causes the body to release hormones that raise blood sugar levels.”
  2. “During serious illness, the body becomes less efficient at utilizing glucose.”
  3. “Insulin reduces the risk of brain swelling by stabilizing cell membranes.”
  4. “Using insulin now can lower the chance of developing diabetes later in life.”
  5. “The stroke damaged the brain area responsible for regulating insulin release.”
  6. “The medications given for treatment can cause type 1 diabetes.”

Options:

  • 1.

    1, 3, 4, 5, and 6 only

  • 2.

    1, 2, 3, and 6 only

  • 3.

    1, 4, 5, and 6 only

  • 4.

    1, 2, and 3 only

Solution:

    • Option 1) “Stress causes the body to release hormones that raise blood sugar levels.”: Correct. —Critical illness triggers the release of stress hormones like epinephrine and glucagon, which lead to hyperglycemia. This is a normal physiological response to stress.
    • Option 2) “During serious illness, the body becomes less efficient at utilizing glucose.”: Correct. —Peripheral tissues, such as muscle cells, have reduced insulin sensitivity during severe illness, causing elevated blood glucose levels despite normal or high circulating insulin.
    • Option 3) “Insulin reduces the risk of brain swelling by stabilizing cell membranes.”: Correct. —Hyperglycemia can exacerbate cerebral edema following a stroke. Insulin helps maintain glucose at a therapeutic level, reducing the risk of swelling and stabilizing the blood-brain barrier.
    • Option 4) “Using insulin now can lower the chance of developing diabetes later in life.”: Incorrect. —The use of insulin in critical illness does not affect the long-term risk of developing diabetes. It is solely used to manage hyperglycemia during the acute phase of illness.
    • Option 5) “The stroke damaged the brain area responsible for regulating insulin release.”: Incorrect. —Insulin production and release are regulated by the pancreas, not the brain. A stroke does not directly affect the pancreas’s ability to produce insulin.
    • Option 6) “The medications given for treatment can cause type 1 diabetes.”: Incorrect. —Medications like steroids may temporarily elevate blood glucose levels (steroid-induced hyperglycemia) but do not cause type 1 diabetes, which is an autoimmune condition.

    Summary

    Options 1, 2, and 3 are the correct explanations as they address the physiological mechanisms of hyperglycemia in critical illness and the role of insulin in managing complications like cerebral edema.

A family member asks the nurse why the patient is frequently awakened and assessed for orientation when rest is important. The nurse explains that the frequent checks are necessary because the earliest sign of increased intracranial pressure (ICP) is:

Options:

  • 1.

    Pupillary changes

  • 2.

    A drop in blood pressure

  • 3.

    Altered sensation

  • 4.

    Changes in the level of consciousness

Solution:

    • Option Pupillary changes: While pupillary changes may indicate increased ICP, they are typically a later sign or associated with cranial nerve damage.
    • Option 2. A drop in blood pressure: A drop in blood pressure is not an early sign of increased ICP. Instead, a widening pulse pressure (increased difference between systolic and diastolic pressure) is a late indicator of ICP.
    • Option 3. Altered sensation: Altered sensation is more commonly related to peripheral nerve issues and is not a primary or early sign of increased ICP.
    • Option 4. Changes in the level of consciousness: A change in the level of consciousness is the earliest and most sensitive indicator of increased ICP. It reflects neurological deterioration and warrants immediate attention.

A nurse is evaluating an arterial line in a patient in the ICU. Which assessment finding is most concerning for a possible complication?

Options:

  • 1. Cool extremities on both sides
  • 2. Low blood pressure measurement
  • 3. Capillary refill time of less than 2 seconds in the affected arm
  • 4. Low mean arterial pressure (MAP)

Solution:

    • Option A. Cool extremities on both sides: Incorrect. —While cool extremities can indicate poor perfusion, bilateral coolness suggests a systemic issue rather than a complication specific to the arterial line.

    • Option C. Capillary refill time of less than 2 seconds in the affected arm: Correct. —Capillary refill of less than 2 seconds in the arm with the arterial line suggests a thrombus or compromised blood flow in the microcirculation.     • Option D. Low mean arterial pressure (MAP): Incorrect. —A low MAP reflects systemic blood pressure rather than indicating a complication with the arterial line. The arterial line is primarily used to monitor MAP and blood pressure trends.

    Summary

    The correct answer is C (Capillary refill time of less than 2 seconds in the affected arm) because it directly points to a potential thrombus or impaired blood flow, which could compromise circulation in the affected limb. This finding warrants prompt attention to avoid ischemic injury.

A 24-year-old postpartum patient is transferred to the ICU after developing disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC). While planning care in collaboration with the physician, the nurse recognizes that management of a patient with a clotting disorder typically includes:

Options:

  • 1. Monitoring core body temperature
  • 2. Starting heparin therapy
  • 3. Administering blood transfusions
  • 4. Avoiding dairy products in the diet

Solution:

    • Option B. Starting heparin therapy: Correct. —Heparin is used in DIC to inhibit further abnormal clotting. This helps preserve the remaining clotting factors and prevents the cycle of clotting and bleeding from worsening. Heparin therapy allows the body to restore its natural hemostasis mechanisms.
    • Option C. Administering blood transfusions: Incorrect. —Although blood products may sometimes be used to address severe bleeding in DIC, they are not the primary treatment. The underlying issue in DIC is abnormal clotting, and addressing this with anticoagulants like heparin is more critical.
    • Option D. Avoiding dairy products in the diet: Incorrect. —Dietary restrictions, including those involving dairy, have no relevance in the management of DIC. This option does not apply to the condition’s pathophysiology or treatment.

    Summary

    The correct answer is B (Starting heparin therapy) because DIC involves widespread clotting that depletes clotting factors, and heparin helps interrupt this process. Management focuses on addressing the underlying cause while stabilizing the patient’s clotting and bleeding balance.

  • 2. Chlorothiazide
  • 3. Spironolactone
  • 4. Acetazolamide

    • Option a (Correct): Furosemide: Furosemide is a loop diuretic that works quickly by acting on the loop of Henle to enhance the excretion of sodium and chloride. Its rapid onset makes it the first choice for managing acute pulmonary edema.

    • Option d: Acetazolamide: Acetazolamide is primarily used to manage glaucoma by lowering intraocular pressure, not for conditions like acute pulmonary edema.

Question No: 20

A 5-year-old child is admitted to the pediatric intensive care unit with acute asthma. A blood sample is taken to measure the child’s arterial blood gases. What result would the nurse expect to see?

Options:

  • 1. High oxygen level
  • 2. Elevated pH (alkalinity)
  • 3. Decreased bicarbonate level
  • 4. Increased carbon dioxide level

Solution:

    • Option a: High oxygen level: In acute asthma, airway narrowing causes difficulty in oxygen exchange, leading to a decreased oxygen level in the blood, not an increased one.
    • Option b: Elevated pH (alkalinity): Asthma typically leads to respiratory acidosis due to the retention of carbon dioxide, not alkalosis. The pH would likely be low, reflecting acidosis.
    • Option c: Decreased bicarbonate level: In the acute phase of asthma, bicarbonate levels generally rise as a compensatory mechanism to buffer the acidity caused by increased carbon dioxide. A decrease in bicarbonate would be more characteristic of metabolic acidosis.
    • Option d (Correct): Increased carbon dioxide level: Due to narrowed airways and reduced gas exchange, carbon dioxide is not effectively exhaled, leading to an increase in its concentration in the blood. This results in respiratory acidosis, a common finding in acute asthma.

    Rationale for Correct Option: In acute asthma, impaired ventilation due to bronchoconstriction causes carbon dioxide retention, leading to increased levels of CO2 in the blood. This is a hallmark of respiratory acidosis.
