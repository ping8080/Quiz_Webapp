Question No: 1

A nurse is caring for a 2-year-old child with a fever and cough. The child’s mother asks when she should bring the child to the emergency department. Which of the following signs and symptoms would indicate the need for immediate medical attention?

Options:

  • 1.

    The child’s temperature is 101°F (38.3°C)

  • 2.

    The child is refusing to eat but is drinking fluids

  • 3.

    The child is having difficulty breathing and making a high-pitched sound while breathing

  • 4.

    The child is sleepy but easily awakens

Solution:

  • Difficulty breathing and the presence of a high-pitched sound, like stridor, are signs of respiratory distress or a potential airway obstruction. These symptoms require immediate evaluation and intervention.

    • Option A) A fever of 101°F is common in children and can typically be managed at home unless it persists or increases.
    • Option B) Refusing to eat but still drinking fluids is concerning but not as immediately dangerous as respiratory distress. 
    • Option D) Sleepiness, when easily awakened, is not immediately alarming, but should still be monitored closely if the child becomes more lethargic.

Question No: 9

Which of the following is the primary cause of Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) in preterm infants?

Options:

  • 1.

    Surfactant deficiency

  • 2.

    Pulmonary hypertension

  • 3.

    Meconium aspiration

  • 4.

    Congenital diaphragmatic hernia

Solution:

  • RDS in preterm infant is primarily caused by a deficiency of surfactant, a substance that reduces surface tension in the lungs and prevents alveolar collapse. Without sufficient surfactant, the alveoli collapse, leading to difficulty in breathing.

    • Option b) Pulmonary hypertension: Pulmonary hypertension can be a secondary complication but is not the primary cause of RDS.
    • Option c) Meconium aspiration: This occurs due to the aspiration of meconium into the lungs, which is a different condition (MAS), not the primary cause of RDS.
    • Option d) Congenital diaphragmatic hernia: This is a separate condition affecting the diaphragm, leading to respiratory distress, but not the cause of RDS.
