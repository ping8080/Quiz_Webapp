Solution:

  • STAGES OF PRESSURE ULCER

    • Bedsores can happen when a person is bedridden or otherwise immobile, unconscious or unable to sense pain.
    • Bedsores are ulcers that happen on areas of the skin that are under pressure from lying in bed, sitting in a wheelchair or wearing a cast for a prolonged time.
    • Pressure score occurs if external pressure exceeds 30 mmHg of capillary occlusive pressure.
    • Tissue necrosis with prolonged pressure.
    • It is also known as Bed sores, pressure ulcers, Decubitus ulcers.
    • Incidence: 5% in hospitalized patient.
    • Pressure sores are most common in: Ischium > Greater trochanter > Sacrum in the gluteal region.
    • After Gluteal region the site in order are: Heel > Lateral malleoli > occipital region.

    1

    Non-blanchable erythema without a breach in the epidermis
    •    If the cause is direct pressure then either ensure that the patient’s position is changed more frequently or ‘step-up’ the pressure relief, e.g. move the patient from a static foam mattress to an alternating pressure mattress.
    •  The skin in a grade 1 pressure ulcer is not broken but it requires protection and monitoring.
    •  Wound dressings for a grade 1 pressure ulcer should be simple and offer protection without risking any further skin damage, especially if the patient is sliding down the bed or chair causing the dressing to ‘ruck’.
    •     A film dressing or a thin hydrocolloid would be appropriate to protect the wound area.
    •     The slippery nature of these dressings may reduce further friction or shearing if these factors are involved.

    2

    Partial-thickness skin loss involving the epidermis and dermis
    •     If the pressure ulcer is circular, this is often an indication that direct pressure is the cause. In this case ensure that the patient’s position is changed more frequently or ‘step-up’ the level of pressure relief.
    •     If the pressure ulcer is not uniform and is more widespread, then shearing or friction may be the cause.
    • A grade 2 pressure ulcer is a superficial wound. If the wound has slough on the wound bed, debridement may be necessary.
    •     There are many dressings that aid debridement, for example, hydrogels or hydrocolloids.
    •     If protection is necessary as well as the promotion of a moist wound healing environment, a hydrocolloid or foam may be appropriate.
    •     Dressing some areas may be a challenge like in sacral and heel region. So sacral flap is  used.

    3

    Full-thickness skin loss extending into the subcutaneous tissue but not through underlying fascia
    •     The damaging factors (pressure, shear or friction) are continuing.
    •     The dressings are not managing the exudate levels and the wound is getting bigger or deeper due to the destructive nature of chronic exudate
    •     The pressure ulcer may be infected
    •     The patient has a poor ability to heal, which may be due to poor circulation, inadequate nutrition, low hemoglobin levels or the presence of disease such as diabetes mellitus.
    •     As these pressure ulcers are substantially deep in many cases, the absorbency levels of the dressings may need to be increased.
    •     Alginates and Foams absorbent dressings can be used in combination to increase the overall absorbency.
    •     If this is occurring after the absorbent dressings, increase the dressing absorbency levels or the frequency of dressing changes.
    •     If conventional dressings cannot manage the exudate, topical negative pressure therapy could be considered.

    4

    Full-thickness skin loss through fascia with extensive tissue destruction, may be involving muscles, one, tendon or joint.
    •     Grade 4 pressure ulcers are the deepest. It is essential to establish if or why the wound is deteriorating or if the depth of the wound is due to the initial pressure damage.
    •     The steps outlined under the treatment of grade 3 pressure ulcers should be followed.
    •     If there is bone exposed, consider infection in the bone (osteomyelitis). This can be identified using an X-ray or a magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scans and is usually treatable with antibiotic therapy.

Question No: 14

The leading anatomical part of the fetus closest to the pelvic inlet of the birth canal refers to:

Options:

  • 1.

    Presentation

  • 2.

    Lie

  • 3.

    Denominator

  • 4.

    Position

Solution:

  • Presentation: This refers to the part of the fetus that is closest to the cervix and pelvic inlet during labor. The most common types of presentation are:

    • Cephalic: The head is the presenting part (most common).
    • Breech: The buttocks or feet are presenting.
    • Shoulder: The shoulder is presenting, which is less common.
    • A breech presentation is when the fetus presents buttocks or feet first (rather than head first – a cephalic presentation). It has significant implications in terms of delivery – especially if it occurs at term (>37 weeks’).
    • Breech presentation is defined as a fetus in a longitudinal lie with the buttocks or feet closest to the cervix. This occurs in 3 - 4% of all deliveries.
    • Lie: This describes the relationship of the long axis of the fetus to the long axis of the mother. The two main types of lie are:
    • Longitudinal: The fetus is oriented vertically (head or buttocks up).
    • Transverse: The fetus is oriented horizontally.
    • Denominator: This term usually refers to a specific landmark on the presenting part of the fetus that helps in determining its position. For example, in a cephalic presentation, the occiput (the back of the head) is often used as the denominator.
    • Position: This describes the specific orientation of the presenting part relative to the mother's pelvis. It is usually expressed in terms of the fetal back's position (anterior, posterior, or lateral) in relation to the mother's pelvis.
