Question No: 1

Following a comprehensive assessment, a nurse determines that a client with type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) has been highly successful in managing their blood glucose levels over the past three months. Which result best supports the nurse’s conclusion? (Diabetes mellitus, Endocrine disorders, Adult health nursing)-

Options:

  • 1. Hemoglobin A1c level of 5%
  • 2. No episodes of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)
  • 3. Absence of ketones in the urine
  • 4. Negative oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT)

Solution:

    • The hemoglobin A1c level is the most reliable indicator of long-term blood glucose control in individuals with diabetes. Hemoglobin A1c reflects the average blood glucose levels over a period of approximately 120 days, which corresponds to the lifespan of red blood cells (RBCs). A hemoglobin A1c level of 5% indicates optimal glycemic control, as per the American Diabetes Association (ADA), which recommends an A1c level of 7% or below for individuals with diabetes.
    • Other factors, such as the absence of DKA or ketones in the urine, do not provide a clear indication of blood glucose management in individuals with T2DM, as these patients are more likely to experience hyperosmolar hyperglycemic syndrome (HHS) rather than DKA due to the presence of some insulin production.
    • The OGTT is primarily used for diagnosing diabetes rather than for evaluating ongoing glycemic control.

Question No: 7

Which statement should a nurse include when providing discharge teaching to a 22-year-old patient hospitalized for the treatment of anorexia nervosa? (Eating disorders, Nursing management of behavioural and emotional disorders, Mental health nursing)-

Options:

  • 1. “Now that you’ve learned to eat again, you’ll likely feel hungry often and should give up the urge to eat.”
  • 2. “Expect to feel fat and uncomfortable at times due to distorted thinking, which is characteristic of an eating disorder.”
  • 3. “You should easily gain weight if your parents prepare special meals that you enjoy eating.”
  • 4. “Isn’t it great to feel in control of your life now? Just follow the meal plan and everything will be fine.”

Solution:

    • Patients with anorexia nervosa often experience distorted thinking, which leads them to feel “fat” and uncomfortable, even as they follow a structured meal plan. They must adhere to their meal plan despite potentially feeling full. Encouraging separate or special meals prepared by family members can reinforce unhealthy family dynamics, such as enmeshment, and is not recommended.
    • Providing reassurance that the patient is now “in control” and that everything will be fine is misleading. Anorexia nervosa is a life-threatening condition, and ongoing effort and support are required to disrupt the cycle of disordered eating behaviors.

  • 2. Use the prescribed “as needed” asthma medication
  • 3. Call the physician
  • 4. Go to the emergency department

Solution:

The nurse’s response should be based on: (Drugs used in the postnatal period, Puerperium, Midwifery/Obstetrics and Gynecological nursing)-

Options:

  • 1. Naproxen is the preferred nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) for breastfeeding mothers.
  • 2. No medications should be taken while breastfeeding.
  • 3. Acetaminophen is the only analgesic that should be avoided during breastfeeding.
  • 4. Ibuprofen has minimal transfer into breast milk.

Solution:

    • Among nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), ibuprofen is the preferred choice for breastfeeding mothers because it has minimal transfer into breast milk and has been extensively studied for its safety in infants.     • It is not necessary for breastfeeding mothers to avoid all medications. The transfer of drugs into breast milk depends on factors like the concentration gradient, which allows passive diffusion of non-ionized, non-protein-bound drugs. To reduce infant exposure, medications that are poorly absorbed orally, or prescribing topical treatments when possible, are recommended. Additionally, timing breastfeeding around peak maternal serum concentrations can further limit the infant’s exposure. Acetaminophen is also considered safe for use during breastfeeding.
