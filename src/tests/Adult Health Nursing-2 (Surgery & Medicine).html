A patient in emergency department is diagnosed to have fluid and air present in lung. What is the above-mentioned condition known as?

Options:

  • 1.

    Hemothorax

  • 2.

    Hydropneumothorax

  • 3.

    Hemopneumothorax

  • 4.

    Pneumothorax

Solution:

    • A Hydropneumothorax is the term given to the concurrent presence of a pneumothorax and pleural effusion (i.e. hydrothorax) (i.e. gas and fluid) in the pleural space.
    • Hemothorax: means blood within the chest, is a term usually used to describe a pleural effusion due to accumulation of blood. If a hemothorax occurs concurrently with a pneumothorax it is then termed a
    • Pneumothorax: As air collects in the pleural space it separates the parietal and visceral pleura, with the latter being pushed inwards. Thus there is air density lining the visceral pleura (water density) – this gives rise to a sharp margin of the visceral pleura becoming visible-DEDS/Silhouette sign.
    • Characteristic of tension pneumothorax is accumulation of air within the pleural space such that tissues surrounding the opening into the pleural cavity act as valves, allowing air to enter but not to escape.

Question No: 12

The laboratory screening test suggestive of normal thyroid function is:

Options:

  • 1. TSH
  • 2. Free T4
  • 3. T3
  • 4. Free T3

Solution:

    • The enhanced sensitivity and specificity of TSH assays have greatly improved laboratory assessment of thyroid function.
    • Because TSH levels change dynamically in response to alterations of T4 and T3, a logical approach to thyroid testing is to first determine whether TSH is suppressed, normal, or elevated.
    • With rare exceptions, a normal TSH level excludes a primary abnormality of thyroid function.
    • This strategy depends on the use of immuno-chemiluminometric assays (ICMAs) for TSH that are sensitive enough to discriminate between the lower limit of the reference range and the suppressed values that occur with thyrotoxicosis.
    • Extremely sensitive assays can detect TSH levels 0.004 mU/L, but for practical purposes, assays sensitive to 0.1 mU/L are sufficient.

Question No: 13

Which hormone is secreted in response to high blood glucose?

Options:

  • 1. Insulin
  • 2. Glucagon
  • 3. Cortisol
  • 4. Oxytocin

Solution:

    • Insulin is secreted by the beta cells of the pancreatic islets in response to high blood glucose concentrations and acts to reduce blood glucose concentration.
    •  An insufficient supply or inability to respond to insulin causes hyperglycemia which is a symptom of diabetes mellitus. 
    • Glucagon, produced by pancreatic alpha cells, inhibits the action of insulin and is an antagonist of the major functions of insulin. 
    • Cortisol is a glucocorticoid secreted by the adrenal cortex which affects glucose, protein and fat metabolism. These actions help maintain blood glucose concentrations between meals.
    • Oxytocin has no function in the regulation of blood glucose.

Question No: 17

What is the pathophysiologic mechanism that results in the pulmonary edema of left-sided heart failure?

Options:

  • 1. Increased right ventricular preload
  • 2. Increased pulmonary hydrostatic pressure
  • 3. Impaired alveolar oxygen and carbon dioxide exchange
  • 4. Increased lymphatic flow of pulmonary extravascular fluid

Solution:

    • In left-sided heart failure, blood backs up into the pulmonary veins and capillaries. This increased hydrostatic pressure in the vessels causes fluid to move out of the vessels and into the pulmonary interstitial space.
    • When increased lymphatic flow cannot remove enough fluid from the interstitial space, fluid moves into the alveoli, resulting in pulmonary edema and impaired alveolar oxygen and carbon dioxide exchange.
    • Initially the right side of the heart is not involved.

Question No: 19

The primary reason for rapid continuous rewarming of the area affected by frostbite is to:

Options:

  • 1. Promote movement
  • 2. Lessen the amount of cellular damage
  • 3. Prevent pain and discomfort
  • 4. Prevent the formation of blisters

Solution:

    • Rapid continuous rewarming of frostbite primarily lessens cellular damage. Rapid rewarming is the single most effective therapy for frostbite. Heat conduction and radiation from deeper tissue circulation prevent freezing and ice crystallization until the skin temperature drops below 0°C.
    • It does not prevent the formation of blisters. It does promote movement, but this is not the primary reason for rapid rewarming. It might increase pain for a short period of time as the feeling comes back into the extremity.
    • Before movement can be improved, the cell injury should be resolved first.
    • Blisters contain high amounts of thromboxane and prostaglandins. They contract and dry within 2-3 weeks, forming a dark eschar that sloughs off in 4 weeks.

    Rhythm

    Conduction intervals

  • 3.

    A description of the QRS complexes, ST segments, T-waves

  • 4.

    All of the above

Solution:

    • A normal ECG contains waves, intervals, segments and one complex, as defined below:
      • Wave: A positive or negative deflection from baseline that indicates a specific electrical event. The waves on an ECG include the P wave, Q wave, R wave, S wave, T wave and U wave.
      • Interval: The time between two specific ECG events. The intervals commonly measured on an ECG include the PR interval, QRS interval (also called QRS duration), QT interval and RR interval.

    Complex: The combination of multiple waves grouped together.   The only main complex on an ECG is the QRS complex.

Question No: 45

The principle of hemodialysis in which the water moves from high pressure to an area of lower pressure is:

Options:

  • 1.

    Ultrafiltration

  • 2.

    Osmosis

  • 3.

    Diffusion

  • 4.

    Perfusion

Solution:

    • Hemodialysis is a medical procedure to remove fluid and waste products from the blood and to correct electrolyte imbalances. This is accomplished using a machine and a dialyzer, also known as “artificial kidney”. It is used to treat both acute and chronic kidney failure.

    Figure: Procedure of Dialysis

    • The objectives of hemodialysis are to extract toxic nitrogenous substances from the blood and to remove excess water. In hemodialysis, the blood, loaded with toxins and nitrogenous wastes is diverted from the patient to a dialyzer, in which it’s cleaned and then returned to the patient.
    • Hemodialysis works on the following principles:
      • Ultrafiltration: Water moving under high pressure to an area of lower pressure by negative pressure of a suctioning force to the dialysis membranes.
      • Diffusion: Movement from higher concentration (blood) to lower concentrations (dialysate). The toxins and wastes in the blood are removed.
      • Osmosis: Excess water is removed from the blood by osmosis, in which water moves from an area of higher solute concentration (the blood) to an area of lower solute concentration (the dialysate bath).
      • Perfusion is the passage of fluid through the circulatory system or lymphatic system to an organ or a tissue, usually referring to the delivery of blood to a capillary bed in tissue.

Question No: 51

When pressure is applied to a swollen area, the indentation persists after the release of the pressure, it is called ____________.

Options:

  • 1.

    Refractory edema

  • 2.

    Weeping edema

  • 3.

    Dependent edema

  • 4.

    Pitting edema

Solution:

    • When we apply pressure over a swollen area and indentation remains there after releasing the pressure, it is called pitting edema. There is a criterion to assess grade of pitting edema. It may be rated as 1+, 2+, 3+, 4+ and 5+.
      • 1+: Slight indentation (2 mm), due to interstitial fluid volume 30% above normal.
      • 2+: Deep pit of 4 mm and lasts longer.
      • 3+: Deep pit of 6 mm and remains for several seconds after pressure, skin swelling present.
      • 4+: Deep pit of 8 mm and remains for prolonged time and frank swelling is obvious.
      • 5+: Brawny edema in which fluid is no longer displaced and is due to interstitial fluid accumulation, pitting is not there, tissues will be firm and hard with warm and shiny moist skin surface.
    • Weeping edema is edema when swelling is so severe and fluid leaks out of surface.
    • Dependent edema is a term used if patient develops edema after keeping the part of body in dependent position such as sitting by keeping legs freely in dependent position. This leads to edema as a result of gravity.
    • Refractory edema is peripheral edema that cannot be recovered or subsided by dietary restrictions and it results due to any cardiac or pulmonary condition such as congestive heart failure.
