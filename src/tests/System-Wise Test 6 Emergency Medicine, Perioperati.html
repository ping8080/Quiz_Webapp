Question No: 1

A 19-year-old unmarried college student is admitted to the emergency department unconscious after a car accident. He is experiencing severe internal bleeding. Which of the following statements is accurate regarding obtaining informed consent for his treatment?

Options:

  • 1.

    Emergency treatment can be provided without consent because his condition is life-threatening.

  • 2.

    He can provide his own consent since he is over 18 years old.

  • 3.

    Parental consent is required before initiating any treatment.

  • 4.

    A court order must be obtained before treatment can begin.

Solution:

    • In emergency situations where immediate medical intervention is necessary to save a patient’s life or prevent serious harm, treatment can proceed without informed consent under the legal principle of implied consent. This principle assumes that a reasonable person would consent to life-saving treatment if they were capable of doing so. Since the patient is unconscious and in critical condition, the healthcare team is legally and ethically justified in providing emergency care without delay.

    Incorrect Options:

    • Option B) He can provide his own consent since he is over 18 years old: While it is true that individuals over the age of 18 are legally considered adults and can provide informed consent, this option is not applicable because the patient is unconscious and unable to give consent. In such cases, implied consent applies instead.
    • Option C) Parental consent is required before initiating any treatment: Parental consent is not required for adult patients (18 years or older). Additionally, in life-threatening emergencies, waiting to obtain consent from family members could jeopardize the patient’s life. Emergency treatment should not be delayed while attempting to contact parents or legal guardians.
    • Option D) A court order must be obtained before treatment can begin: Seeking a court order is unnecessary and impractical in emergency situations. Waiting for legal proceedings would cause dangerous delays in providing critical care. The law allows for immediate treatment under the doctrine of implied consent in such cases.

    Key Takeaway: In life-threatening emergencies, healthcare professionals are legally permitted to provide necessary treatment without informed consent if the patient is unable to give it. This ensures that life-saving care is not delayed when time is of the essence.

Question No: 2

Which of the following is the most reliable indicator of cardiac arrest?

Options:

  • 1.

    Loss of consciousness

  • 2.

    Apnea lasting 4 minutes

  • 3.

    Inaudible blood pressure

  • 4.

    Absence of a palpable carotid pulse

Solution:

    • The carotid artery is the closest major artery to the heart that can be easily palpated. During cardiac arrest, the heart ceases to pump blood effectively, leading to the absence of a detectable pulse in the carotid artery. This makes it the most reliable bedside indicator of cardiac arrest. Even in cases of extremely weak cardiac activity, some pulsation can still be detected. However, in complete cardiac arrest, no pulse is present.

    Explanation of the Incorrect Options:  

    • Loss of consciousness (Option a): While loss of consciousness can occur during cardiac arrest, it is not a definitive indicator because it can result from various conditions such as hypoxia, severe hypotension, cerebral ischemia, or neurological disorders. Therefore, it is not a specific or reliable sign of cardiac arrest.
    • Apnea lasting 4 minutes (Option b): Apnea, or cessation of breathing, can occur due to several reasons, including respiratory failure, central nervous system depression, or airway obstruction. Although cardiac arrest can lead to apnea, the reverse is not always true—respiratory arrest may not immediately lead to cardiac arrest. Hence, it is not the most reliable indicator of cardiac arrest.
    • Inaudible blood pressure (Option c): Blood pressure may become inaudible in cases of extreme hypotension or shock, but this does not necessarily mean the heart has stopped beating. In some cases, the blood pressure may be too weak to be heard with a stethoscope but can still be palpated in central arteries. Therefore, this is not a definitive sign of cardiac arrest.
