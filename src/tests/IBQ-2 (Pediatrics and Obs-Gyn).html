Solution:

    • The Leopold manoeuvre shown in the image is the lateral grip. It assesses fetal orientation.
    • Leopold’s manoeuvres help assess the fetal pole, lie, presenting part, engagement, and attitude of the fetal head. They are as follows:

      I. Fundal grip (first Leopold):

    • Assesses the uterine fundus.
    • Helps in identification of fetal lie and determination of fetal pole at the fundus.
    • The breech is felt as a large, irregular mass.
    • The head feels hard and round and is more mobile.

      II.Lateral grip

      (second Leopold):
    • Assesses the fetal orientation.
    • The palms are placed on either side of the maternal abdomen, and gentle but deep pressure is exerted.
    • The back is felt on one side as a hard, resistant structure.
    • The fetal extremities are felt on the other side as numerous small, irregular, mobile parts are felt.
    • Fetal orientation can be determined by noting whether the back is directed anteriorly, transversely, or posteriorly.
    • The position of the anterior shoulder is mainly assessed by this grip.

      III. Pawlik’s grip / 1st pelvic grip (third Leopold):

    • Assesses the presenting part.
    • The thumb and fingers of one hand grasp the lower portion of the maternal abdomen just above the symphysis pubis.
    • If the presenting part is not engaged, a movable mass will be felt, usually the head.

      IV. Deep pelvic grip / 2nd pelvic grip (fourth Leopold):

    • Assesses the engagement and attitude of the fetal head.
    • The examiner faces the mother’s feet, and the fingertips of both hands are positioned on either side of the presenting part.
    • They exert inward pressure and then slide along the axis of the pelvic inlet.

Question No: 7

A G2P1 obese woman was diagnosed with arrest of descent during labour. Identify the following manoeuvre performed for this patient.

Options:

  • 1.

    Rubin manoeuvre

  • 2.

    McRoberts manoeuvre

  • 3.

    Zavanelli manoeuvre

  • 4.

    Wood corkscrew manoeuvre

Solution:

    • The given clinical scenario is suggestive of shoulder dystocia and the manoeuvre shown above is the McRoberts manoeuvre. This is the most effective intervention and should be the first manoeuvre to be performed in shoulder dystocia.

    McRoberts manoeuvre:

    • Flexion and abduction of the maternal hips (positioning the maternal thighs on her abdomen).
    • The pelvic rotation which occurs due to the straightening of the sacrum tends to free the impacted anterior shoulder of the baby.
    • Suprapubic pressure may be used simultaneously.
    • The lateral cutaneous nerve of the thigh is the most common nerve to get injured during this manoeuvre.

    If the first line of management fails, proceed with internal rotational manoeuvres such as Rubin's and Wood corkscrew in McRoberts position.

    Rubin's manoeuvre:

    • Internal rotational manoeuver used to deliver the baby in case of shoulder dystocia.
    • It involves dislodging the easily accessible shoulder towards the anterior aspect of the chest from under the pubic symphysis by 30-45° while staying in the McRoberts' position.
    • Pressure is applied on the scapular aspect (posteriorly) of the shoulder.

    Wood corkscrew manoeuvre:

    • Internal rotational manoeuver used to deliver the baby in case of shoulder dystocia.
    • It involves dislodging the posterior shoulder by 180° in a corkscrew fashion to lie beneath the pubic symphysis while staying in McRobert's position.
    • Pressure is applied on the clavicular aspect (anteriorly) of the shoulder.

A G2P1L1 woman with a previous history of breech delivery underwent an LSCS which was uneventful. Now, she was brought to the ER with an antepartum haemorrhage and was shifted for LSCS during which she lost a lot of blood. An obstetrics hysterectomy had to be carried out. Options:

  • 1.

    Placenta accreta

  • 2.

    Placenta inversion

  • 3.

    Placenta abruption

  • 4.

    Placenta previa

Solution:

    • The given clinical scenario of this woman with previous LSCS, bleeding per vaginum during pregnancy with hysterectomy image showing placenta adherent to the uterine wall suggests that it is a case of placenta accreta.
    • Placenta accreta is a type of morbidly adherent placenta where the placenta is firmly adherent to the uterine wall due to partial or total absence of the decidua basalis and the fibrinoid layer (Nitabuch layer).
    • Nitabuch’s membrane is the fibrinoid deposition in the outer syncytiotrophoblast. It limits the further invasion of the decidua by the trophoblast. Absence of the membrane causes placenta accreta.
    • The main aetiology is defective decidua formation.

    Pathological Findings

    • Absence of decidua basalis
    • Absence of Nitabuch’s fibrinoid layer

    Classification/Variants

    • Placenta accreta – chorionic villi are attached to the superficial myometrium.
    • Placenta increta – villi invade the myometrium.
    • Placenta percreta – villi penetrate the full thickness myometrium up to the serosal layer.

    Risk Factors

    • Placenta previa in present pregnancy (Note: previous placenta previa is not a risk factor)
    • History of operative interference like:
    • Previous cesarean section
    • Previous curettage
    • Previous manual removal
    • Previously treated Ashermann syndrome
    • Synaechiolysis
    • Myomectomy
    • Multiparity
    • Advanced maternal age > 35 years

    Note:  Highest rise of adherent placenta is with previous LSCS.

    • Skin-to-skin positioning of the newborn on the mother's chest.
    • Adequate nutrition through
    • Early discharge and ambulatory care.

    • Time for initiation: 21 years, regardless of the age of first sexual intercourse.
    • Women aged 21-29 years: Every 3 years (earlier it was done annually).
