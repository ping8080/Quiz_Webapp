Question No: 28

Before tonsillectomy of child; the nurse will do most significant review of which laboratory value?

Options:

  • 1. ESR
  • 2. BUN level
  • 3. PT
  • 4. Creatine level

Solution:

  • Before performing a tonsillectomy in a child, a thorough review of the patient’s laboratory values is necessary to assess the risk of complications during the procedure. Tonsillectomy involves surgery with potential for bleeding, so the most important lab value to assess is the Prothrombin Time (PT). Here’s why:
    • PT (Prothrombin Time): PT is a test that measures the time it takes for blood to clot. It specifically assesses the extrinsic and common coagulation pathways and is important in identifying any clotting abnormalities or bleeding disorders. Since tonsillectomy involves significant manipulation in a highly vascular area, assessing PT is crucial to ensure that the child’s blood will clot properly and to minimize the risk of excessive bleeding during or after surgery. A prolonged PT indicates a potential clotting disorder, and it may necessitate further investigation or preoperative correction.
    • ESR (Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate): ESR is a nonspecific marker of inflammation. While it can be useful in diagnosing or monitoring inflammatory conditions, it is not directly related to the bleeding risk or surgical readiness for a tonsillectomy.
    • BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen) level: BUN is a measure of kidney function, reflecting the balance of urea in the blood. While kidney function is always important, BUN levels are not a direct concern for bleeding risk in a tonsillectomy procedure.
    • Creatinine level: Creatinine is another measure of kidney function. Like BUN, it is not directly related to bleeding risk or clotting ability, making it less relevant before a tonsillectomy.

Atrial fibrillation, in particular, is the most common arrhythmia that predisposes individuals to stroke. In AF, the atria beat irregularly and ineffectively, which can lead to the formation of blood clots in the atria. These clots can then travel to the brain, causing an ischemic stroke. While WPW can lead to arrhythmias, it is not as strongly linked to an increased risk of stroke as atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter. However, in some cases, WPW can cause rapid ventricular rates that could lead to complications, but the stroke risk in WPW is generally considered lower compared to AF or atrial flutter.
