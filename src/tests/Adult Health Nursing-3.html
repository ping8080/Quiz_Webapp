Question No: 1

A patient is scheduled for a cholecystectomy. Which of the following preoperative instructions is most important for the nurse to give to the patient?

Options:

  • 1. “Take all your medications as usual, including aspirin.”
  • 2. “You should not eat or drink anything after midnight.”
  • 3. “You will be able to go home the same day as the surgery.”
  • 4. “You will need to avoid physical activity for 6 weeks after surgery.”

Solution:

    • Fasting before surgery is crucial to reduce the risk of aspiration during anesthesia. Typically, patients are instructed not to eat or drink anything after midnight before surgery.

      Rationale

    • Option A) Aspirin should be avoided before surgery due to its anticoagulant effects.
    • Option C) Cholecystectomy may require a hospital stay for observation, depending on the patient’s condition.
    • Option D) Physical activity limitations are not typically as long as 6 weeks after a cholecystectomy; recovery time is usually shorter.

A patient is suspected of having deep vein thrombosis (DVT) in the lower leg. Which of the following findings would support a positive Homan’s sign?

    Redness and warmth over the affected leg

Solution:

    • A positive Homan’s sign is characterized by pain in the calf upon dorsiflexion of the foot. This maneuver can elicit pain if a clot is present in the deep veins of the leg, suggesting a possible deep vein thrombosis (DVT). However, Homan’s sign is not a definitive diagnostic tool and should be followed up with imaging studies, such as ultrasound, to confirm DVT.
    • Option B) Redness and warmth over the affected leg: While redness and warmth can occur with DVT, they are not specific to Homan’s sign. These symptoms can also be seen with other conditions like cellulitis or superficial thrombophlebitis.
    • Option C) Swelling of the entire leg: Swelling is a common sign of DVT, but generalized swelling of the entire leg is not specific to Homan’s sign. It could be due to other conditions, including heart failure or lymphatic obstruction.
    • Option D) Pulsatile mass in the popliteal area: A pulsatile mass in the popliteal area would suggest a possible aneurysm or arterial issue, not a DVT. This finding is not associated with Homan’s sign, which pertains to venous clots.

Question No: 24

A 10-year-old child with asthma is experiencing an acute asthma attack. Which of the following is the most appropriate first-line medication to administer during this acute exacerbation?

Options:

  • 1.

    Inhaled corticosteroids

  • 2.

    Short-acting beta-agonists (SABA)

  • 3.

    Leukotriene modifiers

  • 4.

    Long-acting beta-agonists (LABA)

Solution:

    • During an acute asthma exacerbation, the first-line treatment is a short-acting beta-agonist (SABA), such as albuterol. SABAs work by rapidly relaxing the bronchial smooth muscles, leading to bronchodilation and improved airflow. They act quickly to relieve symptoms like wheezing, coughing, and shortness of breath.
    • Option A) Inhaled corticosteroids: Inhaled corticosteroids are the long-term control medication for asthma, used to reduce inflammation in the airways. However, they are not effective in rapidly relieving acute symptoms and are not appropriate for use during an acute asthma attack.
    • Option C) Leukotriene modifiers: Leukotriene modifiers (e.g., montelukast) are used as part of long-term asthma management and help reduce inflammation and bronchoconstriction. They are not effective in treating acute asthma exacerbations, as their action is not immediate.
    • Option D) Long-acting beta-agonists (LABA): LABAs are used as a maintenance treatment for asthma, often in combination with inhaled corticosteroids, to provide long-term bronchodilation. However, they are not suitable for acute exacerbations because they take longer to act and should never be used alone in asthma management due to the risk of worsening symptoms in the absence of a corticosteroid.

Which of the following best explains the pathophysiological mechanism behind the positive Kernig sign in patients with meningitis?

Options:

  • 1.

    Increased intracranial pressure leading to cranial nerve compression

  • 2.

    Inflammation of the meninges and irritation of the nerve roots

  • 3.

    Spinal cord ischemia resulting from vascular occlusion

  • 4.

    Autoimmune demyelination affecting the peripheral nerves

Solution:

    • The Kernig sign is caused by meningeal irritation. It is observed when the meninges around the spinal cord become inflamed due to infection (such as in bacterial meningitis) or other causes of meningitis. This inflammation leads to the stretching of the nerve roots, particularly when the hip is flexed and the knee is extended. The pain and resistance during this maneuver result from the irritation of the meninges and nerve roots, which is the key mechanism behind a positive Kernig sign.
    • Option A) Increased intracranial pressure leading to cranial nerve compression: While increased intracranial pressure (ICP) is a common feature of conditions like meningitis or brain tumors, it does not directly cause the Kernig sign. ICP can cause symptoms like headache, vomiting, and altered consciousness, but the Kernig sign is specifically due to the irritation of the meninges and nerve roots, not cranial nerve compression.
    • Option C) Spinal cord ischemia resulting from vascular occlusion: Spinal cord ischemia (due to vascular occlusion) may cause neurological deficits, but it does not typically result in a positive Kernig sign. Kernig's sign is associated with meningeal inflammation, not ischemic injury to the spinal cord.
    • Option D) Autoimmune demyelination affecting the peripheral nerves: While conditions like Guillain-Barré syndrome involve autoimmune demyelination of peripheral nerves, they do not cause the meningeal irritation seen in meningitis. The positive Kernig sign is not associated with demyelination or peripheral nerve disorders, but with the inflammation of the meninges.
