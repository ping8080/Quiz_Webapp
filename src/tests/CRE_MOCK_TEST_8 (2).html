Question No: 1

The patient is in the compensatory stage of shock. What manifestations indicate this to the nurse? 

i. Pale and cool

ii. Lower BP than baseline

iii. Tachypnea and tachycardia

iv. Moist crackles in the lungs

Options:

  • 1.

    If i, ii and iii are correct

  • 2.

    If i and iii are correct

  • 3.

    If ii and iv are correct

  • 4.

    If all four (i, ii, iii and iv) are correct

Solution:

    • In the compensatory stage of shock, the patient’s skin will be pale and cool (α-adrenergic stimulation).
    • There may also be a change in level of consciousness but the person will be responsive, the BP will be lower than baseline, bowel sounds will be hypoactive (α-adrenergic stimulation) and tachypnea and tachycardia (β-adrenergic stimulation) will occur.
    • Moist crackles in the lungs occur in the progressive stage of shock.

During airway suctioning, which nursing intervention is most effective in minimizing the risk of hypoxia?

  • 1.

    Initiate suctioning only after the suction catheter is fully inserted.

    Limit the suctioning procedure to a duration of 30 seconds.

Solution:

  • Option a) Initiate suctioning only after the suction catheter is fully inserted. (Correct Answer): Suctioning removes oxygen along with secretions, increasing the risk of hypoxia.     Explanations:      

    Option b) Limit the suctioning procedure to a duration of 30 seconds.

    • Rationale: Prolonged suctioning increases the risk of hypoxia and airway trauma. The procedure should be limited to 10 seconds per attempt, allowing adequate time for oxygenation between attempts.  
    • Why Incorrect: 30 seconds is too long and can worsen hypoxia.

    Option c) Lubricate the suction catheter with saline before insertion.

    • Rationale: Lubricating the catheter minimizes tissue trauma and facilitates smoother insertion.
    • Why Incorrect: While lubrication is important for preventing injury, it does not address hypoxia.

    Option d) Use a sterile suction catheter for every suctioning procedure.

    • Rationale: Using a sterile catheter prevents infection but has no direct impact on oxygenation or hypoxia.
    • Why Incorrect: This measure focuses on infection control, not oxygen conservation during suctioning.

    Key Concept: When performing airway suctioning, the priority is to maintain oxygenation. Suctioning should be brief and initiated only after the catheter is properly inserted to prevent unnecessary oxygen loss.
