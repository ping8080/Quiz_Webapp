Question No: 1

Which of the following is not an intentional tort?

Options:

  • 1. Assault
  • 2. False imprisonment
  • 3. Defamation
  • 4. Negligence

Solution:

    • A tort is an act or omission that gives rise to injury or harm to another and amounts to a civil wrong for which courts imposes liability. Torts are of two types: intentional and unintentional. Intentional torts are wrongs that the person knew would be caused by their actions. For instance, assault, battery, false imprisonment, slander, libel, and breach of privacy or client confidentiality.
    • Unintentional torts occur when the defendant’s actions or inactions were unreasonably unsafe. It can result from acts of commission such as doing something a reasonable nurse would not have done or omission which means failing to do something a reasonable nurse would do.
    • Negligence is an unintentional tort, which is the failure to exercise the ordinary care a nurse or healthcare professional would use in similar circumstances. Hence, option d. is correct.
    • Assault is defined as intentionally putting another person in reasonable apprehension of an imminent harmful or offensive contact.
    • Battery refers to an intentional causation of harmful or offensive contact with another person without that person’s consent.
    • False imprisonment is defined as an act of restraining another person and causing that person to be confined in a bounded area. For example, use of restraints either, physical, chemical, or verbal.

Question No: 2

A physician prescribes one tablet of a medication, but the nurse accidently administers two. After notifying the physician, the nurse monitors the client carefully for any side effects or complications. After a thorough investigation, no side effects or harmful effects were observed. Is the client likely to be successful in suing the nurse for professional negligence?

Options:

  • 1.

    No, the client was not harmed.

  • 2.

    No, the nurse notified the primary care provider.

  • 3.

    Yes, a breach of duty exists.

  • 4.

    Yes, foreseeability is present.

Solution:

    • In order to sue a person for negligence, certain factors or elements must be proven i.e., duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages.
      • Duty means the nurse owed a duty of care to the patient.
      • Breach of duty refers to failing to adhere to the standards of care by the nurse.
      • Causation means the breach of duty directly caused a harm or injury to the patient.
      • Damages means the patient suffered harm or damage as a result of the breach of duty.
    • A nurse is not using ordinary care and is negligent, if the nurse, without intending to do harm, does something (or fails to do something) that she would recognize as creating an unreasonable risk of injury or damage to the patient/client. In this case, the nurse breaches the standard of care by administering an extra dosage. However, no harm or injury occurred to the patient. Therefore, the client/patient will unlikely to be successful in a lawsuit for professional negligence. Hence, option a. is correct.
    • Option b. is incorrect as the nurse duly informed the healthcare provider about her actions.
    • Option c. is incorrect as though breach of duty occurred but it is not alone sufficient to prove negligence.
    • Option d. is also incorrect as foreseeability refers to whether the nurse could predict that her action could result in harm to the patient. This is not enough to constitute negligence without actual harm.

Question No: 4

Options:

  • 1. Assault
  • 2. Battery
  • 3. Invasion of privacy
  • 4. Negligence

Solution:

    • In this case, the nurse could be guilty of battery, an intentional tort referring to the intentional and wrongful physical contact with a person without their consent. Applying a restraint jacket to a patient without obtaining their consent and without a physician’s order is considered an intentional act of physical contact without the patient’s consent. Though the nurse wanted to protect the patient, but the act of restraining without the formal order depicts violating the rights of the patient to autonomy and body integrity. Hence, option b. is correct.
    • Assault is defined as intentionally putting another person in reasonable apprehension of an imminent harmful or offensive contact.
    • Invasion of privacy involves the violation of a person’s right to keep their personal information or body free from public exposure.
    • Negligence is an unintentional tort, which is the failure to exercise the ordinary care a nurse or healthcare professional would use in similar circumstances.

Question No: 5

A nurse is reviewing end-of-life planning with a patient admitted in the oncology ward. The patient wants to ensure his medical preferences are respected if he ever becomes unable to make decisions. Which document should the nurse explain as the one where the patient can list specific treatments he would choose to refuse?

Options:

  • 1. DNR order
  • 2. A living will
  • 3. Durable power of attorney
  • 4. A healthcare proxy

Solution:

    • The legal document that an individual can outline specific medical treatment he/she would want to refuse if they are unable to make decisions for themselves is called “A Living Will”. This legal document ensures that the concerned person’s preferences (Especially medical) are respected, even when they can’t communicate about them to others due to their illness. This document helps in preventing dignity of the patient during the end-of-life care. Hence, option b. is correct.
    • A durable power of attorney for healthcare is a legal document that let a person name someone else to make decisions about their health care in case they are unable to due to illness or incapacity.
    • Healthcare proxy is a healthcare proxy which allows someone else to make medical decisions for the patient if they are incapacitated.
    • DNR or Do not resuscitate is a specific type of directive that provides an information to the healthcare providers about the client’s preference to not to perform resuscitation whenever required.

Question No: 6

Obtaining an informed consent is the responsibility of a:

Options:

  • 1. Physician
  • 2. Nurse manager
  • 3. Nurse
  • 4. Nursing assistant

Solution:

    • Obtaining an informed consent is the responsibility of a physician. An “informed consent” describes the physician’s duty to disclose to the patient the risks and hazards of medical care that would influence the patient’s decision to give or withhold consent to that treatment. The treating physician’s duty to obtain a patient’s informed consent cannot be delegated. Hence, option a. is correct.
    • Hospitals, nurses, surgical assistants, and referring physicians do not owe this duty to their patients.

Question No: 8

A nurse receives a child in the ER following a motor vehicle crash. The child has no brain functioning and his parents refuse to permit withdrawal of life support from the child. Although the nurse believes the child should be allowed to die and organ donation considered, the nurse supports their decision. Which moral principle provides the basis for the nurse’s actions?

Options:

  • 1. Respect for autonomy
  • 2. Non-maleficence
  • 3. Beneficence
  • 4. Justice

Solution:

    • The nurse supporting the parent’s decision shows the principle of “respect for autonomy”. It means acknowledging and supporting an individual’s right to make decisions about their care, even if the nurse or healthcare provider have conflict with the beliefs or opinions of the individual. In this case, the nurse supporting the parent’s decision to refuse withdrawal of life support depicts respecting their autonomy and authority as the child’s caregivers. Hence, option a. is correct.
    • Non-maleficence is the obligation of a physician or other healthcare providers not to harm the patient.
    • Beneficence is the obligation of physician or other healthcare providers to act for the benefit of the patient, defend their rights and remove conditions that may cause harm to the patient.
    • Justice is an ethical principle interpreted as fair, equitable, and appropriate treatment of persons.

Question No: 9

A nursing instructor is teaching a group of nursing students about ethical principles to be followed when providing care to a patient. One of the utmost important principle to be observed while taking care of the patient is “doing no harm”. The principle of ethics the nurse instructor described is:

Options:

  • 1. Justice
  • 2. Autonomy
  • 3. Non-maleficence
  • 4. Beneficence

Solution:

    • There are four main principles of ethics while providing nursing care: autonomy, beneficence, justice, and non-maleficence.
    • The ethical principle of utmost importance to be observed while taking care of the patient is called “non-maleficence or do no harm”. It is the obligation of a physician or other healthcare providers not to harm the patient. We can say that it requires that nurses avoid causing harm to patients. Hence, option c. is correct.
    • Autonomy means each patient has the right to make their own decisions based on their own beliefs and values. For instance, a person has a right to refuse medications, treatment, surgery, or other medical interventions regardless of what benefit may come from it.
    • All patients have a right to be treated fair and equally by others. Justice involves how people are treated when their interest competes with others.
    • Beneficence is the obligation of physician or other healthcare providers to act for the benefit of the patient, defend their rights and remove conditions that may cause harm to the patient. For instance, assisting patients with tasks that they are unable to perform on their own, keeping side rails up for fall precautions, or providing medications in a quick and timely manner.

Question No: 10

A patient suffers an injury requiring longer hospital stay due to administration of wrong medication by a nurse. The patient can sue the hospital under the doctrine of which of the following?

Options:

  • 1. Battery
  • 2. False imprisonment
  • 3. Vicarious liability
  • 4. Statute of limitations

Solution:

    • Vicarious liability is also known as substituted liability, wherein another party is responsible for the negligence or malpractice of another person. The hospital that employed the nurse can be held vicariously liable for the employee’s negligence. Hence, option c. is correct.
    • Battery means touching a person without their consent.
    • False imprisonment occurs when a person is intentionally restricted by another person in a bounded area without legal justification or consent.
    • A statute of limitations is a law that sets the maximum time that parties have to initiate legal proceedings from the date of an alleged offense.

Question No: 12

Nurses are held responsible for the commission of a tort. The nurse understands that a tort is:

Options:

  • 1. The application of force to the body of another by a reasonable individual.
  • 2. An illegal act committed by one person against the property or person of another.
  • 3. Doing something that a reasonable person under ordinary circumstances would not do.
  • 4. An illegal act committed against the public and punishable by the law through the courts.

Solution:

    • A tort is a civil wrong that causes harm to another person by violating a protected right. It requires the presence of four elements required to prove a civil wrong.

      Duty -> Breach of Duty -> Proximate Cause -> Damages

    • The tort is categorized into intentional and unintentional tort. Intentional torts are wrongs that the defendant knew or should have known would have resulted in his/her actions or omissions. Unintentional torts occur when the defendant’s actions or inactions were unreasonably unsafe.
    • In the given case, an illegal act committed by one person against the property or person of another is considered as “tort”. The individual is held legally responsible for actions committed against another individual or an individual’s property. Hence, option b. is correct.
    • The application of force to the body of another by a reasonable individual is called battery, involving physical harm.
    • Doing something that a reasonable person under ordinary circumstances would not do is considered negligence.
    • An illegal act committed against the public and punishable by the law through the courts is considered a crime.

Question No: 19

A client with terminal cancer arrives at the emergency department dead on arrival. After an autopsy is prescribed, the client’s family requests no autopsy be performed. Which response to the family is most appropriate?

Options:

  • 1. The decision is made by the medical examiner.
  • 2. An autopsy is mandatory for any client who is DOA.
  • 3. It is required by federal law. Tell me why you don’t want the autopsy done?
  • 4. I will contact the medical examiner regarding your request.

Solution:

    • Autopsy is required by state law in certain circumstances such as sudden death of a client and a death that occurs under suspicious circumstances. It is requested to find the time, cause, and manner of death, document injuries and disease, or identify the victim, etc. A client may have provided oral or written instructions regarding an autopsy after death. If an autopsy is not required by law, these oral or written requests will be granted. If no oral or written instructions were provided, state law determines who has the authority to consent for an autopsy. Most often, the decision lies with the surviving relative or next of kin.
