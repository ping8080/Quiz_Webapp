Options:

  • 1.

    Patient 1

  • 2.

    Patient 2

  • 3.

    Patient 3

  • 4.

    Patient 4

Solution:

    • The normal duration of the PR interval is  0.12-0.20 seconds (3 to 5 small boxes).

Question No: 2

A nurse admits a patient to a telemetry unit and records the following electrocardiogram (ECG) strip of the patient’s heart rhythm. Based on the ECG findings, which rhythm should the nurse identify?

Options:

  • 1.

    Atrial flutter

  • 2.

    Atrial fibrillation

  • 3.

    Sinus bradycardia

  • 4.

    Sinus rhythm with premature atrial contractions (PACs)

Solution:

  • Sinus bradycardia is characterized by:

    • A regular rhythm
    • A ventricular rate of less than 60 beats per minute (bpm)
    • A normal P wave before each QRS complex
    • A PR interval within the normal range (0.12–0.20 seconds)
    • A normal QRS duration (<0.12 seconds)

    This rhythm originates from the sinoatrial (SA) node but occurs at a slower-than-normal rate. It may be seen in well-conditioned athletes, during sleep, or in patients with vagal stimulation, hypothermia, or certain medications such as beta-blockers.  

    Incorrect Options:  

    • Atrial Flutter (Option A):
      • Identified by a regular or irregular rhythm
      • Presence of multiple "sawtooth" P waves (flutter waves) before each QRS complex
      • Atrial rate usually 250–350 bpm
      • No measurable PR interval
    • Atrial Fibrillation (Option B):
      • Irregularly irregular rhythm
      • No distinct P waves, instead, there are fibrillatory waves
      • No measurable PR interval
      • The ventricular response may be normal, slow, or rapid
    • Sinus Rhythm with Premature Atrial Contractions (PACs) (Option D):
      • Irregular rhythm due to premature beats
      • A ventricular rate typically between 60 and 100 bpm
      • Presence of early, abnormal P waves before premature QRS complexes
      • PR interval can vary but is typically within normal limits (0.12–0.20 seconds)

Options:

  • 1.

  • 2.

  • 3.

    < 0.11 seconds

  • 4.

Solution:

  • The normal duration of the QRS interval is normally 0.1-0.11 seconds (less than 3 small boxes).

    QRS interval represents the duration of ventricular depolarization. When the QRS complex is > 0.11 seconds, it is called a wide QRS complex. It can occur in:

    • Bundle branch blocks (RBBB or LBBB)
    • Depolarizations originating in the ventricles
    • Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome
    • Hyperkalaemia

Question No: 6

A nurse observes the following rhythm on a patient’s cardiac monitor. What should be the most appropriate nursing action?

Options:

  • 1.

    Activate the emergency response system and call a code

  • 2.

    Continue to monitor the patient, as the movement is causing the rhythm disturbance

  • 3.

    Assess the patient’s electrodes, ECG pads, and electrical connections in the room

  • 4.

    Apply the unit’s automated external defibrillator (AED) pads to analyze the rhythm and deliver a shock if indicated

Solution:

  • The ECG strip shows artifact, which is an abnormal distortion of the ECG waveform caused by non-cardiac factors, such as: 

    Loose electrodes or ECG leads

    • Patient movement (e.g., shivering, tremors, or voluntary movements)
    • Electrical interference from surrounding medical equipment

    The nurse should first assess the patient and check the ECG leads and electrical connections before making any clinical decisions. If the leads are loose, they should be repositioned securely on areas with minimal movement to ensure an accurate reading.

    Incorrect Options:

    • Activating the emergency response system (Option A):
      • While calling a code is appropriate for life-threatening arrhythmias (e.g., ventricular fibrillation, asystole, pulseless electrical activity), the irregular, distorted waveform here suggests artifact rather than a true arrhythmia.
      • The nurse should first confirm whether the abnormal tracing is due to a technical issue before initiating a code.
    • Continuing to monitor without intervention (Option B):
      • Although patient movement can cause artifact, the nurse must still assess the patient and ECG leads rather than assuming the distortion is benign.
      • Failing to check the leads may result in missing a true arrhythmia.
    • Applying AED pads and preparing for shock (Option D):
      • AED application is only necessary when a life-threatening arrhythmia is confirmed (e.g., ventricular fibrillation, pulseless ventricular tachycardia).
      • In this case, the distorted waveform is likely due to electrical or lead interference rather than a shockable rhythm.  

    Key Takeaway:

    When ECG artifact is suspected, the nurse’s priority is to assess the patient and check for technical issues such as loose leads or electrical interference before taking further action.

Question No: 8

A nurse observes the following dysrhythmia on a patient’s cardiac monitor. What rhythm should the nurse identify?

Options:

  • 1.

    Atrial flutter

  • 2.

    Atrial fibrillation

  • 3.

    Ventricular fibrillation

  • 4.

    Ventricular tachycardia

Solution:

  • Ventricular fibrillation (VF) is a life-threatening arrhythmia characterized by:

    • Chaotic, disorganized electrical activity in the ventricles
    • No identifiable P waves, QRS complexes, or T waves
    • Irregular and rapid fibrillatory waves (sawtooth-like or coarse baseline irregularity)
    • Absence of a measurable heart rate and pulse

    VF results in immediate loss of cardiac output, leading to cardiac arrest. Immediate defibrillation and CPR are required for survival.  

    Incorrect Options:

    • Atrial Flutter (Option A):
      • Atrial flutter presents with "sawtooth" P waves (flutter waves) due to rapid atrial depolarization (250–350 bpm).
      • Ventricular response can be regular or irregular with a conduction ratio of 2:1, 3:1, or 4:1.
      • Unlike VF, QRS complexes are present and identifiable.
    • Atrial Fibrillation (Option B):
      • Atrial fibrillation (AF) has an atrial rate of 350–600 bpm, with an irregularly irregular ventricular response.
      • No distinct P waves are visible, but QRS complexes remain present.
      • Unlike VF, AF does not result in immediate cardiac arrest unless there is hemodynamic instability.
    • Ventricular Tachycardia (Option D):
      • Ventricular tachycardia (VT) is a wide-complex tachycardia with a rate of 140–200 bpm.
      • QRS complexes are wide and regular but appear abnormal and bizarre.
      • Unlike VF, VT may still generate a pulse (though it can degenerate into pulseless VT or VF).

    Key Takeaway:

    Ventricular fibrillation is a medical emergency requiring immediate CPR and defibrillation. The nurse must recognize this rhythm promptly to prevent cardiac arrest and death.

Question No: 9

A 22-year-old patient is assessed with an ECG during his wellness check-up visit. Calculate the heart rate from the ECG given below.

Options:

  • 1.

    100/min

  • 2.

    90/min

  • 3.

    79/min

  • 4.

    68/min

Solution:

    • The RR interval has 19 small boxes in between, and hence the heart rate is 1500/19~79/min.
    • The relationship between the heart rate and the RR interval is given by: Heart rate = 1500/(number of small boxes in between successive R waves)

     Methods to calculate heart rate from ECG:    

    • Box counting methods: This can be done via the number of small boxes or the large boxes between two successive R waves. If using the large boxes, the equation is Heart rate = 300/(number of large boxes in between successive R waves).
    • QRS counting methods: This is used when the heart rate is irregular. Thus there will be a variability in the RR intervals, making the box-counting method unreliable. Here, the number of QRS intervals in a set interval, for example, 10 seconds, is counted. This number is then multiplied by an appropriate factor to get an estimate for 60 s. In this example, we would multiply the number of QRS complexes in 10s by 6.
    • Tachycardia is defined as a heart rate of more than 100 beats/minute and bradycardia is defined as a heart rate of less than 60 beats/minute.

Question No: 10

A patient is undergoing continuous ECG monitoring while receiving intravenous medication for premature ventricular complexes (PVCs). The following rhythm is observed on the ECG monitor. How should the nurse interpret this rhythm?

Options:

  • 1.

    Atrial flutter

  • 2.

    Atrial fibrillation

  • 3.

    Ventricular fibrillation

  • 4.

    Ventricular tachycardia

Solution:

  • Ventricular tachycardia (VT) is a life-threatening arrhythmia that appears as:

    • A rate of 100 to 250 beats per minute
    • Regular or slightly irregular rhythm
    • Absent or non-discernible P waves
    • Unmeasurable PR interval
    • Wide and distorted QRS complexes

    VT can lead to hemodynamic instability and may degenerate into ventricular fibrillation (VF), requiring immediate intervention with antiarrhythmic drugs, synchronized cardioversion, or defibrillation if pulseless.

    Incorrect Options:

    • Atrial Flutter (Option A):
      • Atrial flutter is characterized by a rapid atrial rate of 250–350 beats per minute.
      • The ventricular rate may be normal or elevated, depending on conduction.
      • P waves appear as “sawtooth” flutter waves due to rapid atrial depolarization.
      • Unlike VT, QRS complexes remain narrow and regular.
    • Atrial Fibrillation (Option B):
      • Atrial fibrillation (AF) features an atrial rate of 350–600 beats per minute.
      • The ventricular response is irregularly irregular with no distinct P waves.
      • QRS complexes are normal in morphology unless an underlying conduction issue exists.  
      • Unlike VT, the ventricular rate is irregular rather than rapid and regular.
    • Ventricular Fibrillation (Option C):
      • Ventricular fibrillation (VF) is a chaotic, disorganized rhythm with no identifiable P waves, QRS complexes, or T waves.
      • The heart loses its ability to pump blood effectively, leading to immediate cardiac arrest.
      • Unlike VT, VF is irregular and lacks structured waveforms.

    Key Takeaway:

    Ventricular tachycardia is a serious cardiac arrhythmia that requires prompt assessment and intervention to prevent hemodynamic collapse or progression to ventricular fibrillation. The nurse must be prepared to administer antiarrhythmic drugs, perform synchronized cardioversion, or initiate defibrillation if pulseless VT occurs.

  • 1.

    Patiromer

  • 2.

    Intravenous calcium gluconate

  • 3.

    Hemodialysis

  • 4.

    Intravenous insulin

Solution:

    • The given clinical scenario along with the ECG showing tall T waves in all the leads and the laboratory investigations showing serum potassium of 7.1 mEq/L are suggestive of The next best step in the management is intravenous calcium gluconate.
    • This patient has acute kidney injury resulting from dehydration. Her BUN: Creatinine ratio is 26.9 which is >20:1 which suggests prerenal azotemia. Acute kidney injury results in impaired excretion of potassium resulting in hyperkalemia.
    • Although ECG changes are not specific and hyperkalemia may occur without significant ECG changes, the typical changes seen in ECG due to hyperkalemia are sequential and include:
      • Peaking of the T waves
      • ST-segment depression
      • Widening of the PR and QRS intervals
      • Sine waves
      • Ventricular fibrillation and asystole

    • The presence of cardiac toxicity is a serious manifestation of hyperkalemia and requires immediate cardiac stabilization with intravenous calcium gluconate. Calcium gluconate should also be initiated in all patients with serum potassium >6.5 mEq/L and patients with muscular weakness.

    • Administer regular insulin with dextrose. The insulin will reduce the serum potassium concentration by causing an intracellular shift. The dextrose will counteract the hypoglycemic effects of insulin.
    • Provide salbutamol nebulization, which will also shift potassium inside the cell.
    • Remove potassium with calcium-based resins, diuretics, or hemodialysis.

Question No: 17

ECG of a patient revealed a QT interval of 0.55 s. Which of the following will not be a differential diagnosis?

Options:

  • 1.

    Hypothermia

  • 2.

    Digitalis toxicity

  • 3.

    Hypocalcemia

  • 4.

    Romano-Ward syndrome

Solution:

  • QT interval of 0.55 s suggests QT prolongation. It is not seen in digitalis toxicity. Rather, a QT shortening with characteristic "scooping" of the ST-T-wave complex, called a digitalis effect, is seen.

    The ECG changes seen in digitalis toxicity are as follows:

    • Prolonged PR interval
    • ST-segment depression
    • Short QT interval

    QT interval represents the time taken for the ventricles to depolarize and repolarize. It is prolonged in the following:

    • Hypocalcemia
    • Hypokalemia
    • Congenital long QT syndromes such as Romano-Ward syndrome
    • Hypothermia
    • Drugs: Class IA and Class III antiarrhythmics
    • Intracranial bleeds: particularly SAH
