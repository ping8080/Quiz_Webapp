Question No: 1

Which of the following is the most common congenital anomaly leading to recurrent pregnancy loss (RPL)?

Options:

  • 1.

    Bicornuate uterus

  • 2.

    Septate uterus

  • 3.

    Cervical incompetence

  • 4.

    Uterine fibroids

Solution:

  • Septate uterus

    • The septate uterus is the most common congenital uterine anomaly associated with RPL. It occurs due to incomplete resorption of the medial septum during embryogenesis.
      • Key Clinical Feature: It is more commonly associated with second-trimester (T2) losses but may also cause first-trimester (T1) abortions due to implantation failure or compromised blood supply in the uterine septum.
      • Diagnosis can be confirmed via imaging techniques like 3D ultrasound, MRI, or hysterosalpingography (HSG).
      • Treatment: Surgical correction using hysteroscopic metroplasty significantly improves pregnancy outcomes.

    Distractors:

    • Bicornuate uterus:
      • Bicornuate uterus is another congenital anomaly, but it is less commonly associated with RPL than the septate uterus. It results from incomplete fusion of the Mullerian ducts.
      • Bicornuate uteri are often linked to preterm labor or second-trimester losses, but the overall prevalence in RPL cases is lower.
    • Cervical incompetence:
      • While cervical incompetence is a leading acquired cause of RPL, it is not a congenital anomaly. It results in painless cervical dilation and second-trimester pregnancy losses (T2 abortions).
      • Management includes cervical cerclage (McDonald or Shirodkar procedure) to prevent further losses.
    • Uterine fibroids:
      • Uterine fibroids are also an acquired anomaly but are less frequently associated with RPL. They usually contribute to implantation issues or pregnancy complications if they distort the uterine cavity.

Which of the following is the most commonly performed type of cerclage for cervical incompetence?

Options:

  • 1.

    Abdominal cerclage

  • 2.

    Shirodkar cerclage

  • 3.

    McDonald cerclage

  • 4.

    Transvaginal cerclage

Solution:

    • McDonald cerclage:
      • McDonald cerclage is the most commonly performed cerclage procedure, primarily because it is technically simpler, minimally invasive, and effective for cervical incompetence.
      • It involves non-absorbable monofilament suture placed in a purse-string pattern at the portio vaginalis (near the external os).
      • It is preferred over Shirodkar cerclage due to its ease of application and fewer procedural complications.
    • Distractors

      • Shirodkar cerclage: While Shirodkar cerclage has a lower failure rate due to the placement of the suture closer to the internal os, it is more challenging to perform and thus less commonly done.
      • Transvaginal cerclage: This term broadly refers to vaginal approaches, which include both McDonald and Shirodkar techniques. The McDonald technique is the most frequently chosen among them.

    A newborn is presented with a soft, fluctuant swelling on the scalp that crosses suture lines and was noted immediately at birth. On examination, the swelling pits on pressure and resolves within a few days. What is the most likely diagnosis?

    Options:

    • 1.

      Cephalohematoma

    • 2.

      Caput succedaneum

    • 3.

      Subgaleal hemorrhage

    • 4.

      Skull fracture

    Solution:

    • Caput succedaneum is the correct diagnosis based on the following:

      • Collection of fluid above the periosteum causes a soft, pitting swelling.
      • Crossing of suture lines is a hallmark feature because the fluid is superficial.
      • The swelling appears at birth due to prolonged pressure during labor.
      • Resolution within 2–3 days is consistent with the transient nature of caput succedaneum.

      Distractors:

      Cephalhematoma:

        • Cephalhematoma is a collection of blood beneath the periosteum, which means it does not cross suture lines.
        • It appears hours after birth and resolves over 2–3 weeks, unlike the immediate and transient nature of caput succedaneum.

      Subgaleal hemorrhage:

        • Subgaleal hemorrhage is a severe condition involving blood accumulation beneath the aponeurosis.
        • It presents as a diffuse swelling that also crosses suture lines but is associated with systemic symptoms like pallor and hypovolemic shock.

      Skull fracture:

        • Skull fractures typically present with bony deformity or crepitus, not a soft, pitting swelling.
        • They are often accompanied by neurological signs or a history of significant trauma, which are absent in this scenario.

      Key Points for Learners:

      • Caput succedaneum: Fluid, above the periosteum, crosses sutures, pits on pressure, present at birth, resolves within 2–3 days.
      • Cephalhematoma: Blood, below the periosteum, does not cross sutures, appears hours after birth, resolves in 2–3 weeks.
      • Subgaleal hemorrhage: Blood beneath aponeurosis, diffuse swelling, crosses sutures, associated with systemic symptoms.
      • Skull fracture: Bony injury, no soft swelling, accompanied by neurological signs.
