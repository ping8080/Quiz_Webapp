Question No: 1

Which is the research method that combines both a longitudinal design and a cross-sectional design?

Options:

  • 1. Exploratory design
  • 2. Divergence design
  • 3. Explanatory design
  • 4. Convergence design

Solution:

    • Convergence design is the research method that combines both a longitudinal design and a cross-sectional design. In the convergent design, a researcher should have two separate sets of data (let’s say, observations and consultation records as a qualitative strand, and quantitative self-report questionnaires as a quantitative strand) of equal importance, collected independently, analyzed independently, and then results from both strands are merged during the interpretation phase.  These two strands should not be connected or influence each other at the time of data collection or analyses.
    • An exploratory design is a qualitative research design, in this study researcher explores new facts. This is not a mixed-method study.
    • Divergence design is a research study as the name indicates researcher start study but it becomes divert toward quantitative or qualitative study during data collection.
    • Explanatory design is a research study in which a researcher explains any problem which is under-researched and this is also not a mixed-method study.

Question No: 6

Applying an unfair amount of pressure on subjects to complete the responses quickly is called:

Options:

  • 1. Selection bias
  • 2. Procedural bias
  • 3. Measurement bias
  • 4. Inclusive bias

Solution:

    • Procedural bias is when pressure is applied on the subjects, forcing them to complete their responses quickly. For example, employees asked to fill out a questionnaire during their break period are likely to rush, rather than reading the questions properly.
    • Selection bias is the bias introduced by the selection of individuals, groups, or data for analysis in such a way that proper randomization is not achieved, thereby ensuring that the sample obtained is not representative of the population intended to be analyzed. It is sometimes referred to as the selection effect.
    • Measurement bias is the bias if it systematically overstates or understates the true value of the measurement.
    • Inclusive bias occurs when samples are selected for convenience.

Question No: 19

Options:

    Pilot study

Question No: 38

Independent variable is also known as____________________.

Options:

  • 1.

    Cause

  • 2.

    Free variable

  • 3.

    Effect

  • 4.

    Final variable

Solution:

  • In research, an independent variable is typically considered the cause because it is the variable that is manipulated or varied to observe its effect on another variable, which is the dependent variable (the effect). Here's an explanation for each option:

    • Cause: The independent variable is often referred to as the cause because it is the variable that researchers manipulate or change in an experiment to see if it causes any effect on the dependent variable. For example, if you're studying how different amounts of exercise (independent variable) affect weight loss (dependent variable), exercise would be the cause.
    • Free variable: While the independent variable is sometimes described as "free" because its value is not dependent on other variables in the study, this term is not as commonly used as "cause" in research terminology.
    • Effect: This refers to the dependent variable, not the independent variable. The dependent variable is the outcome or effect that is measured after manipulating the independent variable.
    • Final variable: This term is not typically used to describe the independent variable. It might be confused with the dependent variable, as the final outcome is what researchers observe or measure.

Question No: 48

In research process, a hypothesis usually identifies:

Options:

  • 1.

    Variables to be used

  • 2.

    Measuring tools

  • 3.

    Sample to be studied

  • 4.

    All of the above

Solution:

  • A hypothesis is essentially a testable statement or prediction about the relationship between variables. In the research process, it primarily identifies the variables that will be examined or measured. Here’s why:

    • Variables to be used: A hypothesis typically defines what the researcher is studying by identifying key variables. For example, a hypothesis might predict how changes in one variable (e.g., hours of study) affect another variable (e.g., exam performance). The variables are at the heart of the hypothesis.
    • Measuring tools: The specific tools or instruments for measurement (e.g., surveys, tests, scales) are determined after the hypothesis is formulated, during the study design phase. The hypothesis doesn't directly specify which tools to use, though the type of variable may influence this decision.
    • Sample to be studied: The sample, or the group of participants in the research, is not directly outlined in the hypothesis. Instead, the sample is typically chosen based on the research question and the variables involved, but this is part of the research design, not the hypothesis itself.

Question No: 49

Which is the most appropriate method of gathering information?

Options:

  • 1.

    Survey

  • 2.

    Review of literature

  • 3.

    Expert’s opinion

  • 4.

    Television

Solution:

    • Survey: This is one of the most common and versatile methods for gathering data. Surveys allow researchers to collect information directly from participants using structured questionnaires or interviews. It’s useful for obtaining quantitative data and is especially effective when you need information from a large or diverse group of people. It can gather responses about attitudes, behaviors, or experiences.
    • Review of literature: This is an important method for gathering existing information and understanding the current state of knowledge on a topic. However, it doesn’t collect new data directly from participants. Instead, it synthesizes and analyzes findings from previous studies, which is crucial in the early stages of research but doesn’t serve as the primary method for gathering new, firsthand information.
    • Expert’s opinion: Expert opinion is valuable when specialized knowledge or insight is needed. However, this method may not always be representative of the broader population, and it relies on subjective judgment, which can introduce bias.
    • Television: While television can provide information, it’s generally not a systematic or rigorous method for gathering research data. Information from television is often intended for entertainment or general public consumption, and may not be reliable or comprehensive for research purposes.

Question No: 50

Survey approach of research primarily focuses on:

Options:

  • 1.

    Past

  • 2.

    Future

  • 3.

    Present

  • 4.

    All

Solution:

  • Surveys are primarily used to collect data about the present. They focus on understanding current attitudes, behaviors, opinions, or conditions at a specific point in time. Here’s why:

    • Present: Surveys typically ask questions about current experiences, beliefs, or behaviors. For example, a survey might ask individuals about their current level of satisfaction with a product, their current lifestyle habits, or their opinions on a current event. The goal is to gather data that reflects the situation or opinions of the respondents at the moment the survey is conducted.
    • Past: While some surveys may inquire about past behaviors or experiences (e.g., "How often did you exercise last month?"), the focus is generally still on how those past experiences are relevant to understanding the present context.
    • Future: Some surveys do include questions about future intentions or predictions (e.g., "Do you plan to buy this product in the future?"), but the primary purpose of surveys is not to predict the future. It’s more about capturing a snapshot of the present.
