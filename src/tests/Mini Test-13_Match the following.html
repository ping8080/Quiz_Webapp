Match the following diseases with their respective mode of inheritance.

Column-A

Column-B

1)    Cystic Fibrosis

2)    Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy

3)    Optic Neuritis

4)    Marfan Syndrome

A.   Mitochondrial Inheritance

B.    Autosomal Recessive

C.   Autosomal dominant

D.   X-Linked Recessive

Options:

  • 1.

    1-D, 2-C, 3-B, 4-A

  • 2.

    1-B 2-D, 3-A, 4-C

  • 3.

    1-B, 2-C, 3-A, 4-D

  • 4.

    1-D, 2-C, 3-B, 4-A

Solution:

  • Mitochondrial Inheritance

    • Mother transmits disorder (maternal inheritance) to all her children but females may show signs of disease.
    • Variable expression in a population or even within a family is due to heteroplasmy.
    • Heteroplasmy: Phenomenon where cells have variable proportion of normal and mutant mtDNA in their organelle is known as heteroplasmy.

    Fig: Mitochondrial inheritance pedigree 

     

    Examples:

    • Most common mitochondrial inheritance is MELAS syndrome (mitochondrial encephalopathy, lactic acidosis, and stroke like syndrome).
    • Leber herediatary optic neuropathy
    • NARP (neurogenic weakness, ataxia and retinitis pigmentosa)
    • Leigh’s syndrome
    • CPEO (Chronic progressive external opthalmoplegia)
    • Kearns-Sayre syndrome

              X linked Dominant

    • Rare pattern of inheritance
    • Affected male will transmit it 100% to their daughters and none of their sons.
    • Affected (diseased) female will transmit the disease 50% to their sons and daughters.
    • Examples:
      • “Red Rose for all children”
      • Red: X-linked hypophosphatemic Rickets
      • Rose: Rett’s syndrome
      • For: Fragile X syndrome (XR > XD)
      • All: Alport syndrome
      • Children : Charcot–Marie–Tooth disease

     

     

      Autosomal dominant inheritance

    • Most common pattern of Mendelian inheritance.
    • They manifest disease in the heterozygous state, so at least one parent of index case is usually affected.
    • Most commonly due to defects in structural genes. 
    • Many generations, both males and females are affected.
    • Examples:
      • Achondroplasia
      • Autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease
      • Familial adenomatous polyposis
      • Familial hypercholesterolemia
      • Hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia
      • Hereditary spherocytosis
      • Huntington disease
      • Li-Fraumeni syndrome
      • Marfan syndrome
      • Multiple endocrine neoplasia
      • Neurofibromatosis type 1 (von Recklinghausen disease)
      • Neurofibromatosis type 2
      • Tuberous sclerosis,
      • Von Hippel-Lindau disease

                                       

     

            X linked Recessive (XLR)

    • Most common pattern of sex-linked disorders is X-linked recessive.
    • Commonly males are affected; because mutant genes on the X-chromosome do not have a Y-chromosome counterpart (affected males are hemizygous for the X-linked mutant gene).
    • Heterozygote, males are not affected usually due to a paired normal Xallele.
    • Female can be affected when there is random X inactivation in cells population.
    • Example: -                                    
      • Ornithine transcarbamylase deficiency
      • Fabry disease
      • Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome
      • Ocular albinism
      • G6PD deficiency
      • Hunter syndrome
      • Bruton agammaglobulinemia
      • Hemophilia A and B
      • Lesch-Nyhan syndrome
      • Duchenne (and Becker) muscular dystrophy.

    Often Give Her Boys Her Linked Disorders

     

     

     

     

     

     

     

     

     

     

          Autosomal recessive

    • Most of the inborn errors of metabolism are autosomal recessive.       
    • Usually seen in only 1 generation.  
    • With 2 carrier (heterozygous) parents, on average:
      • ¼ of children will be affected (homozygous),
      • ½ of children will be carriers,
      • ¼ of children will be neither affected nor carriers.
    • Examples:
      • Albinism
      • Autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease (ARPKD)
      • Cystic fibrosis
      • Glycogen storage diseases
      • Hemochromatosis
      • Kartagener syndrome
      • Mucopolysaccharidoses (except Hunter syndrome)
      • Phenylketonuria
      • Sickle cell anemia
      • Sphingolipidoses (except Fabry disease)
      • Thalassemia
      • Wilson disease

Question No: 3

Match the acid-base imbalances with their mechanisms.

Column-A

Column-B

A.   Increased carbonic acid (H2CO3)

B.    Decreased carbonic acid (H2CO3)

C.    Increased base bicarbonate (HCO3–)

D.   Decreased base bicarbonate (HCO3–)

1)    Metabolic acidosis

2)    Metabolic alkalosis

3)    Respiratory acidosis

4)    Respiratory alkalosis

Options:

  • 1.

    A-4, B-3, C-2, D-1

  • 2.

    A-3, B-4, C-2, D-1

  • 3.

    A-3, B-4, C-1, D-2

  • 4.

    A-4, B-3, C-1, D-2

Solution:

    • Increased carbonic acid - H₂CO₃: This corresponds to respiratory acidosis, where impaired lung function leads to carbon dioxide retention, causing an increase in carbonic acid levels.
    • Decreased carbonic acid - H₂CO₃: This describes respiratory alkalosis, which occurs when excessive breathing (hyperventilation) reduces carbon dioxide levels, leading to decreased carbonic acid.
    • Increased bicarbonate - HCO₃⁻: This is associated with metabolic alkalosis, where elevated bicarbonate levels are often due to excessive loss of acids (e.g., through vomiting) or bicarbonate retention.
    • Decreased bicarbonate - HCO₃⁻: This represents metabolic acidosis, where bicarbonate is depleted, often due to conditions like diarrhea, renal failure, or lactic acid accumulation.

    Key Insight:
    Respiratory imbalances involve changes in carbonic acid (related to CO₂ levels), while metabolic imbalances involve changes in bicarbonate levels.

Question No: 6

Match the methods of local anesthetic administration with their descriptions.

Column-A

Column-B

A.   Injection of agent into subarachnoid space

B.    Injection of anesthetic agent directly into tissues

C.    Injection of a specific nerve with an anesthetic agent

D.   Injection of anesthetic agent into space around the vertebrae

E.    Injection of agent into veins of extremity after limb is exsanguinated

1)    Nerve block

2)    IV nerve block

3)    Spinal block

4)    Epidural block

5)    Local infiltration

Options:

  • 1.

    A-3, B-5, C-1, D-4, E-2

  • 2.

    A-3, B-4, C-5, D-1, E-2

  • 3.

    A-5, B-3, C-1, D-4, E-2

  • 4.

    A-4, B-5, C-1, D-3, E-2

Solution:

    • Injection into the subarachnoid space: This describes a spinal block, where the anesthetic is injected into the cerebrospinal fluid in the subarachnoid space, providing profound anesthesia for surgeries involving the lower abdomen, pelvis, or lower limbs.
    • Injection directly into tissues: This method is known as local infiltration, commonly used for minor procedures to numb a specific area by injecting anesthetic into the surrounding tissues.
    • Injection into a specific nerve: This is a nerve block, where the anesthetic is administered near a specific nerve or nerve group to block sensation in the area supplied by the nerve.
    • Injection into space around the vertebrae: This refers to an epidural block, where the anesthetic is delivered into the epidural space outside the dura mater to provide anesthesia or pain relief, commonly used in labor and surgeries.
    • Injection into veins after limb exsanguination: This is an IV nerve block (also called a Bier block), used for surgeries on extremities. The limb is exsanguinated (drained of blood) using a tourniquet, and anesthetic is injected into the veins to provide regional anesthesia.

    • Bartonella bacilliformis is the only species in the Bartonella genus that invades erythrocytes.
    • Bartonella bacilliformis is a Gram-negative facultative intracellular bacterium that causes Carrion's disease, a biphasic illness endemic to certain regions of South America, particularly in high-altitude areas of Peru, Colombia, and Ecuador.
    • Spread by sandflies (Lutzomyia spp.).
    • Causes:

    Acute Phase (Oroya Fever)

    Chronic Phase (Verruga Peruana)

    ·        High fever, malaise, severe headache, muscle pain, and abdominal pain.

    ·        Acute hemolytic anemia due to massive invasion of erythrocytes by the bacteria.

    ·        Treatment: ciprofloxacin, chloramphenicol, or ceftriaxone.

    ·        Characterized by vascular skin lesions that appear as nodules, papules, or miliary lesions (commonly referred to as Peruvian warts).

    ·        Lesions are highly vascularized and may bleed easily.

    ·        Treatment: Azithromycin or rifampin is effective for treating skin lesions.

    Treponema carateum

    • The causative agent for Pinta (carate, mal del pinto) is Treponema carateum.
    • The primary lesion is a papule which does not ulcerate but develops into lichenoid or psoriaform patch.
