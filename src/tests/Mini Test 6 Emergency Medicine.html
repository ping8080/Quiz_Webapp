Question No: 1

A nurse in the emergency department of a military hospital receives a call regarding an unknown number of person who have been exposed to a nerve agent during a terrorist attack. Which medication should the nurse prepare in sufficient quantities to treat the patients? (Anticholinergic, Drugs used in treatment of Cardiovascular system and blood disorders, Pharmacology)

Options:

  • 1. Atropine sulfate
  • 2. Labetalol
  • 3. Dopamine
  • 4. Phentolamine

Solution:

  • Nerve agents inhibit acetylcholinesterase, leading to overstimulation of nerve endings. This results in symptoms similar to cholinergic crisis, including bradycardia, excessive secretions, and disturbanemic shock occurs when decreased intraces in the respiratory, neurological, gastrointestinal, urinary, and visual systems. Atropine works by increasing the heart rate and reducing secretions, making it the appropriate treatment in this case.
  • Labetalol, a beta-blocker, lowers the heart rate, while dopamine is a vasopressor used to improve cardiac output and raise blood pressure.
  • Phentolamine, an alpha-blocker, causes hypotension and is used subcutaneously to treat dopamine extravasation.

    • Question No: 2

    Which of the following is incorrect with respect to the preload, systemic vascular resistance, and cardiac output respectively in different types of shock? (Shock, Nursing Care of Patients with Common Signs, Symptoms and Management, Adult health nursing)

    Options:

    • 1.

      Hypovolemic shock - increase, increase, decrease

    • 2.

      Cardiogenic shock - increase, decrease, decrease

    • 3.

      Obstructive shock - decrease, increase, decrease

    • 4.

      Distributive shock - decrease, decrease, increase

    Solution:

    • Obstructive shock:

      • It is due to a decrease in venous return or cardiac compliance due to an increased left ventricular outflow obstruction or marked preload decrease. 
      • Cardiac tamponade and tension pneumothorax are common causes.

       Other Options:

       Option A: Hypovolemic shock

      • Hypovolemic shock occurs when decreased intravascular fluid or decreased blood volume causes decreased preload, stroke volume, and CO. 
      • Severe blood loss (hemorrhage) can cause decreased myocardial oxygenation, which decreases contractility and CO. 
      • This action may lead to an autonomic increase in the SVR.

      Option B: Cardiogenic shock

      • In cardiogenic shock, the left ventricle fails to deliver oxygenated blood to peripheral tissues due to variances in contractility, as well as preload and afterload.
      • Myocardial infarction is the most common cause of cardiogenic shock. Dysrhythmias are another common cause.

      Option D: In distributive shock

      • There is relative intravascular volume depletion due to marked systemic vasodilation. This is most commonly seen in septic shock. 
      • Compensatory responses to decreased SVR may include increased CO (increased contractility and heart rate) and tachycardia.
      • The concurrent decreased SVR results in a decreased preload and may hinder CO.

    During a mass casualty disaster, which injured patient should be prioritized by the triage nurse in the emergency department? (Disaster preparedness, Disaster nursing, Adult health nursing)

    Options:

    • 1.

      An unresponsive patient with a penetrating head injury.

    • 2.

      A partially responsive patient with a sucking chest wound.

    • 3.

      A patient with a maxilla fracture and facial wounds without airway compromise.

    • 4.

      A patient with third-degree burns covering 65% of the body surface area.

    Solution:

      • A sucking chest wound is a life-threatening condition but can be treated successfully, making it the highest priority (Priority 1, red tag) in the triage system.
      • Option-a and c: The unresponsive patient with a severe head injury and the patient with extensive third-degree burns have a lower chance of survival even with immediate care and are classified as Priority 4 (black tag).
      • Option-d: The patient with facial wounds and a maxilla fracture, who does not have airway compromise, is considered Priority 2 (yellow tag), as their injuries are serious but not immediately life-threatening and can wait for treatment without a significant risk.

    Question No: 4

    A 6-month-old infant is brought to the emergency department with symptoms of lethargy, irritability, and poor feeding. Upon examination, the infant appears dehydrated, with sunken fontanelles and dry mucous membranes. Laboratory tests reveal hypernatremia. Which of the following consequences of hypernatremia poses the most devastating risk to this infant? (Fluid and electrolyte disturbances, Fluid and electrolyte imbalances, Child health nursing)

    Options:

    • 1.

      Renal failure

    • 2.

      Cardiac arrhythmia

    • 3.

      Pulmonary edema

    • 4.

      Brain hemorrhage

    Solution:

      • In infants with hypernatremia, the most devastating consequence is the shrinkage of brain cells due to increased sodium levels in the interstitial fluid. This osmotic shift causes fluid to exude from brain cells, leading to shearing forces on blood vessels and the potential for brain hemorrhage, including subarachnoid, dural, or parenchymal hemorrhage.

      Incorrect Options:

      • Option A - Renal failure:Renal failure is a potential consequence of severe dehydration but is not the most devastating risk associated with hypernatremia.
      • Option B - Cardiac arrhythmias:Cardiac arrhythmias can occur in cases of severe electrolyte imbalances, including hypernatremia, but they are less common and typically less immediately life-threatening than brain hemorrhage.
      • OptionC - Pulmonary edema: Pulmonary edema is not a typical consequence of hypernatremia; rather, it is more commonly associated with conditions such as heart failure or excessive fluid resuscitation.

      Learning objective

      Most devastating consequences of hypernatremia in children

      • ↑ Na in interstitial fluid → fluid exudes osmotically from brain cells → shrinking of brain cells → shearing force exerted on the blood vessels → pulled apart → brain hemorrhage (subarachnoid/dural/parenchymal hemorrhage).

    • 2. Contact the mental health unit to arrange inpatient treatment.

    • 4. Stay with the client to prevent self-harm until relieved.

    A 60-year-old patient came with burning micturition for 2 weeks. Outside investigations are suggestive of a urinary tract infection. On presentation, BP is 80/40 mmHg, PR is 134/m, spo2 is 97%, and Respiratory rate is 24/m. TLC count is 24000/mm3. You suspect septic shock. Which of the following therapy is not recommended in the treatment of a patient with septic shock? (Shock, Nursing Care of Patients with Common Signs, Symptoms And Management, Adult health nursing)

    Options:

    • 1. IV fluids
    • 2. Vasopressors
    • 3. Steroids
    • 4. Sodium bicarbonate

    Solution:

  • Sodium bicarbonate is not indicated in the management of shock except when a patient has severe metabolic acidosis (pH<7.1). Even evidence for that is limited.

     Other Options:

  • Option A: IV fluids is the first line of treatment in any type of shock. It has a definitive role in hypovolemic and septic shock. Even in cardiogenic shock and obstructive shock, a small fluid challenge can be used to see a response in the form of rise in blood pressure.
  • Option B: Vasopressors is the second line of treatment used in the fluid refractory shock. Use of vasopressors is encouraged early in the management of cardiogenic shock.
  • Option C: Steroids is used in refractory septic shock.

She also reports a fear of closed spaces and a sense of “impending doom.” The nurse suspects she is having a panic attack. What should the nurse do first? (Anxiety disorders, Nursing management of somatoform and neurotic disorders, Mental health nursing)

  • Similarly, physical activity may heighten her agitation and should be avoided during a panic attack.

    Question No: 11

    A multiparous woman at 42 weeks’ gestation arrives at the emergency department experiencing contractions. Upon assessment, the nurse determines she is in the second stage of labor. What is the nurse’s most important action? (Second stage of labor, Labor and its management, Midwifery/Obstetrics and Gynecological nursing)

    Options:

    • 1. Transfer the woman to the delivery room.
    • 2. Time the frequency of contractions.
    • 3. Notify the healthcare provider and prepare for delivery.
    • 4. Check the fetal heart rate.

    Solution:

  • In the second stage of labor, the cervix is fully dilated, and the infant begins to descend into the birth canal, possibly crowning. The nurse’s priority is to notify the healthcare provider and prepare for delivery, as birth may be imminent.
  • Transferring the woman at this stage is not advisable, as delivery could occur quickly. While timing contractions and checking the fetal heart rate are important, they are secondary to ensure readiness for the delivery.
  • The primary responsibility at this stage is to support the infant’s head and apply gentle pressure to control the delivery.

    A 28-year-old male presents to the emergency department after a motor vehicle accident. He exhibits a weak thready pulse, flat neck veins, tachycardia, dyspnea, and a unilateral absence of breath sounds on auscultation. Percussion reveals dullness on the affected side. What is the most likely cause of his condition, and what is the initial treatment of choice? (Management of trauma, Trauma and its management, Adult health nursing)

    Options:

    • 1.

      Spontaneous pneumothorax; Needle decompression

    • 2.

      Tension pneumothorax; Needle decompression

    • 3.

      Hemothorax; Needle aspiration

    • 4.

      Hemothorax; Intercostal drainage insertion and tube thoracostomy

    Solution:

      • The patient's symptoms and signs are consistent with a hemothorax, most commonly caused by trauma leading to injury to the chest wall or lung laceration. In cases of blunt trauma, massive hemothorax is often due to torn intercostal vessels from rib fractures. The initial treatment of choice for hemothorax is the insertion of an intercostal drainage (ICD) and tube thoracostomy to drain the blood from the pleural space and re-expand the lung.

      Incorrect Options:

      • Options A and B:The patient has signs of hypovolemic shock, dullness on percussion, and fluid accumulation (not air) in the pleural space.
      • Option C - Hemothorax; Needle aspiration:The treatment of choice in cases of hemothorax is intercostal drainage (ICD) and tube thoracostomy.

      Learning objective

      Hemothorax

      • Blood accumulates in the pleural space secondary to trauma of chest wall / lung laceration.
      • The most common cause of hemothorax is trauma, which causes injury to the chest wall and lung laceration.
      • Other causes include TB and tumor.
      • In patients of blunt trauma, the most common cause of massive hemothorax is torn intercostal vessels (due to rib fracture).
      • Clinical features
        • Hypovolemic shock (due to loss of blood)
        • Flat neck veins
        • Dyspnea (because of collection of fluid in the pleural space)
        • Unilateral absence of breath sounds (on auscultation)
        • Dullness on percussion
      • Investigations
        • Diagnosis is made by - Needle aspiration of pleural space
        • CXR can be performed - To assess the presence and extent of pleural cavity collections.
      • Treatment
        • TOC - ICD insertion + Tube Thoracostomy.

    A new nurse is caring for an adolescent in the pediatric unit who sustained injuries from a motor vehicle accident and has started a blood transfusion. After 30 minutes, the patient reports nausea and a headache. What should the observing nurse determine, was the new nurse’s first appropriate action? (Blood transfusion, Administration of medications, Nursing Foundations)

    Options:

    • 1. Obtained a set of vital signs
    • 2. Called the primary healthcare provider
    • 3. Flushed the infusion tubing with normal saline and restarted the blood
    • 4. Stopped the transfusion

    Solution:

  • The first action the nurse should take when a transfusion reaction is suspected is to stop the transfusion immediately to prevent further complications. Symptoms such as nausea and headache could indicate a transfusion reaction, and allowing more blood to infuse could worsen the reaction.
  • After stopping the transfusion, the nurse should take a set of vital signs to assess the patient’s condition.
  • Calling the healthcare provider comes after these initial steps. The transfusion line should never be flushed because it could introduce more blood, potentially worsening the reaction.

    Which child admitted to the emergency department with signs of shock should the nurse immediately place on latex allergy precautions? (Shock, Nursing Care of Patients with Common Signs and Symptoms and Management, Adult health nursing, Child health nursing)

    Options:

    • 1. A child with spina bifida
    • 2. A child with cardiac anomalies
    • 3. A child with other known allergies
    • 4. A child with a parent who has a latex allergy

    Solution:

  • Children with spina bifida are at a significantly higher risk of developing latex allergies due to repeated exposure to latex products during multiple surgeries and catheterizations. Studies have indicated that up to 80% of children with spina bifida may have a latex allergy. Symptoms can range from mild reactions, such as redness or blistering, to severe anaphylaxis.
  • Children with other conditions, like cardiac anomalies, are only at risk if they have undergone frequent surgeries involving latex products. While there is a known cross-reaction between latex and certain food allergies (e.g., banana, kiwi, avocado), these food allergies need to be identified before placing a child on latex precautions. A parent’s latex allergy does not necessarily indicate a latex allergy in the child.

    A nurse is performing cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) on a neonate. Which action by the nurse indicates a need for further instruction on neonatal CPR? (Neonatal resuscitation, Neonatology, Child health nursing)

    Options:

    • 1. Performs chest compressions with two thumbs while encircling the chest with the fingers
    • 2. Delivers chest compressions on the lower third of the sternum
    • 3. Completes 90 compressions and 30 breaths per minute
    • 4. Lifts the thumbs an inch from the chest between compressions

    Solution:

  • The nurse should not lift the thumbs from the chest between compressions. The thumbs must remain in contact with the chest to ensure proper positioning and effective compressions. All other actions are correct.
  • Chest compressions for a neonate can be done using the two-thumb technique, where the fingers encircle the chest for support, or with two fingers. Compressions should be delivered to the lower third of the sternum, to a depth of about one-third of the chest’s anterior-posterior diameter.
  • A 3:1 ratio of compressions to ventilations is recommended, with 90 compressions and 30 breaths per minute, totalling around 120 events per minute to optimize ventilation and circulation.
