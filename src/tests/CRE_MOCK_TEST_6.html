Question No: 2

During a regular check-up of an 8-year-old child, you note a loud first heart sound with a fixed and widely split second heart sound at the upper left sternal border that does not change with respirations. The patient is otherwise active and healthy. Which of the following heart lesions most likely explains these findings?

Options:

  • 1. Atrial septal defect (ASD)
  • 2. Ventricular septal defect (VSD)
  • 3. Isolated tricuspid regurgitation
  • 4. Tetralogy of Fallot

Solution:

    • Most children with an ASD are asymptomatic; the lesion is found during a routine examination. In older children, exercise intolerance can be noted if the lesion is of significant size. On examination, the pulses are normal, a right ventricular systolic lift at the left sternal border is palpable, and a fixed splitting of the second heart sound is audible. For lesser degrees of ASD, surgical treatment is controversial.
    • VSD commonly presents as a harsh or blowing holosystolic murmur best heard along the left lower sternum, often with radiation throughout the precordium. The auscultatory finding of tricuspid regurgitation is typically a blowing holosystolic murmur, augmented during inspiration, and best heard at the left lower sternal border; a mid-diastolic rumble may also be heard. A history of birth asphyxia or findings of other cardiac lesions may be present.
    • Tetralogy of Fallot is a common form of congenital heart disease. The four abnormalities include right ventricular outflow obstruction, VSD, dextroposition of the aorta, and right ventricular hypertrophy; the cyanosis presents in infants and in young children.
    • Mitral valve prolapse occurs with the billowing into the atria of one or both mitral valve leaflets at the end of systole. It is a congenital abnormality that frequently manifests only during adolescence or later. It is more common in girls than in boys and seems to be inherited in an autosomal dominant fashion. On clinical examination, an apical murmur is noted late in systole, which can be preceded by a midsystolic click. The diagnosis is confirmed with an echocardiogram that shows prolapse of the mitral leaflets during mid to late systole.

  • 3.

    Inverted P wave of ECG

  • 4.

    Restlessness

Solution:

    • Hyperkalemia is defined as a serum potassium level greater than 5 mmol/L. Potassium is essential for regulating the electrical excitability of cells, particularly cardiac myocytes. In hyperkalemia, excess potassium reduces the difference between resting and threshold potentials, making it easier for cells to depolarize, which disrupts normal heart function.

    Cardiac Arrhythmias Associated with Hyperkalemia:

    Hyperkalemia can cause the following cardiac arrhythmias:

    • Sinus bradycardia: Slow heart rate due to increased potassium affecting the SA node.
    • Sinus arrest: Complete failure of the SA node to generate impulses.
    • Slow idioventricular rhythms: A very slow heart rate generated from the ventricles when normal conduction is impaired.
    • Ventricular tachycardia (VT): Fast, life-threatening rhythm originating in the ventricles.
    • Ventricular fibrillation (VF): Chaotic, uncoordinated ventricular activity, leading to a lack of effective cardiac output.
    • Asystole: Complete cessation of electrical activity in the heart, a state of cardiac arrest.

    ECG Changes Based on Serum Potassium Levels:

    • 5-6.5 mmol/L → Tall Peaked T Waves: Early sign of hyperkalemia. The T waves become sharp, narrow, and peaked due to faster repolarization.
    • 5-7.5 mmol/L → Loss of P Wave: The P wave disappears, and atrial conduction is impaired, leading to atrial standstill. Conduction through the AV node slows, causing further ECG abnormalities.
    • 7-8 mmol/L → Wide QRS Complex, PR Prolongation: The QRS complex widens, and the PR interval prolongs as conduction through the ventricles becomes more sluggish, indicating severe hyperkalemia. The risk of ventricular arrhythmias, such as ventricular tachycardia, significantly increases at this stage.
    • >8 mmol/L → Sine Wave Pattern: The QRS complexes merge with T waves, forming a sine wave pattern on the ECG. This represents near-complete loss of organized electrical activity and is a pre-terminal event, often followed by ventricular fibrillation or asystole.

    ECG Changes by Potassium Levels:

    Serum Potassium (mEq/L)

    ECG Changes

    5.5-6.5

    Tall peaked T waves

    6.5-7.5

    Loss of P wave, prolonged PR interval

    7-8

    Wide QRS complex

    >8

    Clinical Significance:

    • Serum potassium levels >6.5 mmol/L with ECG changes represent a medical emergency. The patient is at high risk for sudden cardiac death, and immediate treatment to lower potassium is essential.
    • >8 mmol/L: At this point, the patient is in imminent danger of cardiac arrest due to the severe widening of the QRS complex and potential progression to a sine wave pattern, which often precedes ventricular fibrillation or asystole.

    Treatment Priorities in Hyperkalemia:

    • Stabilization of the myocardium with calcium gluconate to reduce the risk of arrhythmias.
    • Shifting potassium intracellularly with insulin and glucose, beta-agonists, or sodium bicarbonate.
    • Removing potassium from the body via diuretics, resins (e.g., sodium polystyrene sulfonate), or dialysis in severe cases.
