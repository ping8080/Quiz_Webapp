Question No: 1

Which of the following is not found in urine?

Options:

  • 1. Only glucose
  • 2. Only protein
  • 3. Only Red blood cells
  • 4. All of the above

Solution:

    • Glucose is usually not found in urine unless blood glucose levels are elevated (as in diabetes), causing the renal threshold to be exceeded. Under normal circumstances, glucose should not be present in urine.
    • Protein is typically absent or found in very minimal amounts in urine. If there is an excess of protein in the urine (proteinuria), it could indicate kidney disease or damage.
    • Red blood cells (RBCs) are not normally present in urine. If RBCs are found, it may indicate a urinary tract infection (UTI), kidney stones, or trauma to the urinary tract.

Question No: 2

Out of the following, which medicines are used for intestinal evacuation?

Options:

  • 1. Cathartics
  • 2. Carminatives
  • 3. Astringents
  • 4. Antimycotics

Solution:

    • Cathartics are medications that promote bowel movements by stimulating the intestines to evacuate their contents. They are used to treat constipation and are commonly referred to as laxatives or purgatives.
    • Carminatives are substances that help relieve gas and bloating in the digestive tract. They do not promote evacuation of the intestines but rather reduce discomfort due to gas.
    • Astringents are substances that cause contraction of tissues and can be used to reduce secretions, inflammation, or bleeding. They are not used to evacuate the intestines.
    • Antimycotics are antifungal medications used to treat fungal infections, not for intestinal evacuation.

Question No: 3

An agent which kills pathogenic bacteria is called:

Options:

  • 1. Asepsis
  • 2. Disinfectant
  • 3. Fomite
  • 4. Bacteriostat

Solution:

    • Disinfectants are agents that are used to kill or inhibit the growth of pathogenic microorganisms, particularly bacteria, on non-living surfaces. They are designed to reduce the risk of infection by eliminating harmful pathogens.
    • Asepsis refers to the practice of maintaining sterility to prevent the introduction of pathogens, but it does not specifically involve killing bacteria—it’s more about preventing contamination.
    • Fomite refers to inanimate objects or surfaces that can carry infectious agents, but it is not a term for an agent that kills bacteria.
    • Bacteriostat refers to substances that inhibit the growth and reproduction of bacteria, but they do not necessarily kill the bacteria.

Question No: 4

The reason why lipstick and nail polish are removed before surgery is:

Options:

  • 1. Surgery becomes easier
  • 2. Surgery becomes more successful
  • 3. Psychological benefits to the patient
  • 4. Cyanosis is checked

Solution:

    • Lipstick and nail polish are removed before surgery primarily so that the medical team can easily assess the patient’s circulation during the procedure. Specifically, the color of the lips and nail beds helps in identifying signs of cyanosis (a bluish discoloration that indicates a lack of oxygen in the blood). If nail polish or lipstick is present, it can obscure these visual indicators, making it harder to assess oxygenation levels.
    • While the other options may have some indirect relevance, the primary clinical reason is to ensure proper monitoring of the patient’s oxygenation status during surgery.

Question No: 5

What should be the temperature of water used for cold sponging?

Options:

  • 1. 98° F
  • 2. 105° F
  • 3. 70° F
  • 4. 0° F

Solution:

    • Cold sponging is typically done to help reduce a fever, and the water used for this purpose should be cool, but not too cold. A temperature of around 70°F (21°C) is considered appropriate because it’s cool enough to help lower body temperature without causing discomfort or shock to the body.
    • 98°F is close to body temperature, so it wouldn’t have a cooling effect.
    • 105°F is actually warm and could increase body temperature rather than reduce it.
    • 0°F is too cold and could cause harm, such as triggering shivering or even hypothermia.

    KMnO4

Solution:

Question No: 7

In children, calculation of drug dose is done on the basis of:

Options:

  • 1. Age
  • 2. Height
  • 3. Weight
  • 4. Length

Solution:

    • In children, drug dosage is typically calculated based on their weight because it provides a more accurate estimation of the child’s body mass and the appropriate amount of medication needed. Weight-based dosing ensures that the child receives an appropriate amount of the drug, adjusted for their size.
    • Age is a factor in pediatric dosing (especially for certain age-related variations), it is less precise than weight when calculating the correct dose.
    • Height is important for assessing growth and development, weight is the primary determinant for drug dosing in children.
    • Length is used for infants and very young children, but it’s still generally tied to weight in terms of medication dosing.

Question No: 9

The most effective method of well disinfection is done by:

Options:

  • 1.

    Chlorine tablets

  • 2.

    KMnO4

  • 3.

    Per chlorine

  • 4.

    CaOCl2 (Calcium oxychloride)

Solution:

    • CaOCl2 (Calcium oxychloride), also known as bleaching powder, is a commonly used and highly effective disinfectant for well water.     • Chlorine tablets (a) are also effective but typically come in a form that's more suitable for small-scale disinfection or for emergency use. However, CaOCl2 is more commonly used for large-scale well disinfection.
    • KMnO4 (Potassium permanganate) (b) is used for some water treatments, particularly for oxidizing impurities, but it’s not as effective as CaOCl2 for overall disinfection.
    • Per chlorine (c) is also an oxidizing agent but is less commonly used in well disinfection compared to CaOCl2.
