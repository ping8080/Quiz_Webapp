Question No: 1

What is the normal systolic pressure?

Options:

  • 1. 180 mm of Hg
  • 2. 80 mm of Hg
  • 3. 120 mm of Hg
  • 4. 130 mm of Hg

Solution:

    • 120 mm of Hg is the normal systolic pressure. Normal blood pressure is 120/80 mm of Hg. In this 120 mm of Hg is the systolic pressure and 80 mm of Hg is the diastolic pressure. Here systolic blood pressure means blood pressure measured when left ventricles are in the systolic phase i.e. when the left ventricle is in the contraction phase. In this phase, blood is pumped with the highest pressure in vessels. Diastolic pressure means when the left ventricle is in the diastolic phase i.e., the left ventricle is in a relaxed phase this time blood pressure is lowest in vessels.

Question No: 2

Difference between systolic and diastolic blood pressure is known as:

Options:

  • 1. High blood pressure
  • 2. Low blood pressure
  • 3. Pulse pressure
  • 4. Hydrostatic pressure

Solution:

    • The difference between systolic and diastolic blood pressure is known as Pulse pressure. Pulse pressure is the pressure felt during a heartbeat or we can say it is the measurement of rising of blood pressure due to one systole. It can be measured by calculating the difference between systolic and diastolic pressure. Diastolic pressure is the blood pressure when the left ventricle is dilated or when it is at resting state and systolic pressure is the pressure when left ventricle contracts and due to this contraction blood pressure is increased. A pulse is formed due to this rise of systolic pressure and we can feel it by palpation of an artery. For example, if one patient is having blood pressure 140/80 mm Hg. Then pulse pressure will be the difference between these two pressures that are 60 mm Hg.
    • High blood pressure or hypertension is defined as when blood pressure is increased.
    • Low blood pressure or hypotension is defined as when blood pressure is decreased.
    • Hydrostatic pressure is the pressure exerted by the fluid on its wall.

Options:

  • 3. Job satisfaction theory
  • 4. Social cognitive theory

Solution:

    • Merle H. Mishel began her research into uncertainty, the concept had not been applied in the health and illness context. Her original Uncertainty in Illness Theory (Mishel, 1988) drew from existing information-processing models (Warburton, 1979) and personality research (Budner, 1962) from psychology that characterized uncertainty as a cognitive state resulting from insufficient cues with which to form a cognitive schema or internal representation of a situation or event. Mishel attributes the underlying stress-appraisal-coping-adaptation framework in the original theory to the work of Lazarus and Folkman (1984).     • Maslow’s hierarchy of needs is a motivational theory in psychology comprising a five-tier model of human needs, often depicted as hierarchical levels within a pyramid.     • Job satisfaction theory is a theory that suggests that people have innate dispositions that cause them to have tendencies toward a certain level of satisfaction, regardless of one’s job. This dispositional approach suggests that job satisfaction is closely related to personality.
    • Social cognitive theory, used in psychology, education, and communication, holds that portions of an individual’s knowledge acquisition can be directly related to observing others within the context of social interactions, experiences, and outside media influences.

Question No: 40

The immediate action taken by a first aider to rescue a victim of heat stroke:

Options:

  • 1.

    Give oral fluids

  • 2.

    Fanning of victim

  • 3.

    Remove victim from hot environment

  • 4.

    Spraying water over the victim

Solution:

    • The immediate action for a heat stroke victim is to remove them from the hot environment as the first priority. Heat stroke is a life-threatening emergency where the body's temperature regulation system fails, and the body temperature rises to dangerous levels (usually above 104°F or 40°C). Immediate cooling and intervention are critical to prevent organ damage and death.
    • Here’s the sequence of actions in the management of heat stroke:
      • Remove the victim from the hot environment: Get the person to a cooler, shaded area as soon as possible to stop further heat exposure.
      • Cool the body rapidly: This can be done by fanning the person or applying cold packs to the armpits, neck, and groin area, or even spraying water and using ice for rapid cooling.
      • Give oral fluids (only if the person is conscious and able to swallow): Hydration is important, but the priority is to cool the body first and foremost.
      • Call for emergency medical help immediately: Heat stroke is a medical emergency that requires prompt professional treatment, including possible intravenous fluids, and other medical interventions.
    • Why the other options are less appropriate as initial actions:
      • Give oral fluids: This can be helpful if the person is conscious and alert, but the first action should be to remove them from the hot environment and begin cooling. If the person is confused, unconscious, or having difficulty swallowing, giving fluids could pose a risk of choking.
      • Fanning of victim: Fanning is an effective method of cooling, but it should be done after removing the victim from the hot environment. It should not be the first action.
      • Spraying water over the victim: While spraying water and applying cool compresses can be helpful for cooling, removing the victim from the heat is the most important initial step.

    Thus, the immediate action is to remove the victim from the hot environment to stop further heat exposure and begin the cooling process.

Question No: 45

Physiological response to heat application is:

Options:

  • 1.

    Vasoconstriction

  • 2.

    Vasodilation

  • 3.

    Shivering

  • 4.

    Local anesthesia

Solution:

  • Heat creates higher tissue temperature, which produces vasodilation (widening of blood vessels, relaxing smooth muscle cells) that increases the supply of oxygen and nutrients and the elimination of carbon dioxide and metabolic waste.

    • Vasoconstriction is narrowing or constriction of the blood vessels due to loss of body heat in cold temperatures.
    • Shivering is the random involuntary contraction of superficial muscle fibers, which does not limit heat loss but rather increases heat production.
    • Local anesthesia provides reversible blockade of nerves, leading to loss of sensation of pain. Cold compress can cause numbness.

Question No: 47

When the patient lies on his back with one pillow under the head is called:

Options:

  • 1. Trendelenburg position
  • 2. Genupectoral position
  • 3. Lithotomy position
  • 4. Supine position

Solution:

    • Supine position should be the answer as in this position patient has to lie on his back. This position is used to assess the thoracic, pericardial, head, neck and extremities.

    • Trendelenburg position is position where head of the patient is down and feet are elevated. It improves the venous return to the heart and improves vital organ perfusion.
    • Genupectoral position is also known as Knee-chest position. In this position patient has to be to her/his knees and chest and arms crossed above the head. This position is employed in conditions like cord prolapse, flushing of the intestinal canal and dislodgement of the impacted fetal head.

Question No: 48

The endotracheal tube is put into the:

Options:

  • 1.

    Oesophagus

  • 2.

    Stomach

  • 3.

    Trachea

  • 4.

    Nasal cavity

Solution:

    • An endotracheal tube (ETT) is inserted into the trachea during medical procedures, particularly in cases of respiratory distress or when mechanical ventilation is required.
    • The purpose of the ETT is to maintain an open airway, allowing air to flow into the lungs, ensuring the patient receives adequate oxygen.
    • Oesophagus: The oesophagus is the tube that carries food from the mouth to the stomach. Inserting an endotracheal tube into the oesophagus would result in improper placement and could cause serious complications.
    • Stomach: The stomach is not the target for the endotracheal tube. Its role is in digestion, not in respiration.
    • Nasal cavity: The nasal cavity is part of the upper respiratory tract but is not the site for the placement of an endotracheal tube. Although there are nasal airways used for ventilation, the ETT specifically goes into the trachea.
