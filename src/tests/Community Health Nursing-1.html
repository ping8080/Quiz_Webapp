Question No: 1

Which committee is known as the Health Survey and Planning Committee?

Options:

  • 1. Kartar Committee
  • 2. Bhore Committee
  • 3. Shrivastava Committee
  • 4. Mudaliar Committee

Solution:

    • The Health Survey and Planning Committee is also known as Mudaliar Committee. This committee was established during the second five-year plan in 1959 to survey the progress made in the field of health sector since submissions of the Bhore Committee report and to make recommendations for future development and expansion of health services. This committee was headed by Dr. AL Mudaliar. This committee emphasized that a primary health center (PHC) should not be made to cater to more than 40,000 populations and that the curative, preventive, and primitive services should be all provided at the PHC. The Mudaliar Committee also recommended that an All India Health service should be created to replace the erstwhile Indian Medical service.
    • Kartar Committee: This committee is also known as a committee on multipurpose workers under health and family planning.
    • Bhore Committee: This committee is also known as health survey and development committee
    • Shrivastava Committee: This committee is also known as a group on medical education and support manpower.

Question No: 6

The number of new cases occurring in a defined population during a specified period of time is called:

Options:

  • 1. Point prevalence
  • 2. Incidence
  • 3. Morbidity
  • 4. Prevalence

Solution:

    • Incidence is defined as the number of new cases in a given population during a specific period of time. An incidence rate is the number of new cases of a disease divided by the number of persons at risk for the disease. For example, if in data findings of one year, five women are diagnosed with breast cancer, out of a total female study population of 200, then the investigator would say the incidence of breast cancer in this population was 0.025. (or 2,500 per 1,00,000 women-years of study).
    • Point prevalence is the number of persons with the disease in a time interval (e.g. 1 year) divided by the number of persons in the population.
    • Morbidity is referred to as the rate of disease in a population.
    • Prevalence in epidemiology is the proportion of a particular population found to be affected by a medical condition.

Question No: 22

Secondary prevention is:

Options:

  • 1.

    Safe water supply, vector and animal reservoir control

  • 2.

    Early detection (diagnosis) of disease and prompt treatment

  • 3.

    Good living and working condition

  • 4.

    Nutritional counseling

Solution:

    • Secondary prevention refers to early detection (diagnosis) of disease and prompt treatment.
    • There are three levels of prevention:
      • Primary prevention: Primary prevention aims to prevent disease or injury before it occurs. This is done by preventing exposures to hazards that cause disease or injury, altering unhealthy or unsafe behaviors that can lead to disease or injury, and increasing resistance to disease or injury should exposure occur.
      • Secondary prevention: Secondary prevention aims to reduce the impact of a disease or injury that has already occurred. This is done by detecting and treating disease or injury as soon as possible to halt or slow its progress, encouraging personal strategies to prevent re-injury or recurrence, and implementing programs to return people to their original health and function to prevent long-term problems.
      • Tertiary prevention: Tertiary prevention aims to soften the impact of an ongoing illness or injury that has lasting effects. This is done by helping people manage long-term, often-complex health problems and injuries (e.g. chronic diseases, permanent impairments) in order to improve as much as possible; their ability to function, their quality of life and their life expectancy.            
    • Safe water supply, vector and animal reservoir control, good living and working condition, nutritional counseling are the example of primary level of prevention.

Question No: 47

All among the following are killed vaccines, except:

Options:

  • 1.

    BCG

  • 2.

    DPT

  • 3.

    Hepatitis B

  • 4.

    Pentavalent

Solution:

    • Killed vaccines are made from viruses or bacteria that have been inactivated or killed, so they cannot cause disease.
    • Live vaccines, on the other hand, contain weakened forms of the virus or bacteria that still trigger an immune response.  
    • BCG (Bacillus Calmette-Guérin): This is a live vaccine that uses a weakened form of Mycobacterium bovis to protect against tuberculosis.
    • DPT (Diphtheria, Pertussis, and Tetanus): This is a killed vaccine. The DPT vaccine contains inactivated toxins or bacteria that cause diphtheria, pertussis, and tetanus.  
    • Hepatitis B: This is a subunit vaccine, not a killed vaccine. It contains a part of the virus (specifically the hepatitis B surface antigen) rather than the whole virus, and it stimulates the immune system without using a live virus.
    • Pentavalent: This is a combination vaccine that includes vaccines for diphtheria, pertussis, tetanus, hepatitis B, and Haemophilus influenzae type b. It includes both killed and subunit components, making it different from a simple killed vaccine.
