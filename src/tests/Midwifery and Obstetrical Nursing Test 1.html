Question No: 2

Which of the following is a function of fallopian tube?

Options:

  • 1. Implantation
  • 2. Fertilization
  • 3. Sperm production
  • 4. Ovum production

Solution:

    • The fallopian tubes are bilateral conduits between the ovaries and the uterus in the female pelvis. They function as channels for oocyte transport and fertilization. Given this role, the fallopian tubes are a common etiology of infertility as well as the target of purposeful surgical sterilization.
    • The implantation normally takes place in the superior and posterior walls of the uterine body (corpus uteri) in the functional layer of the endometrium during the secretory phase of the cycle. Normal implantation zone of the blastocyst is in the superior and posterior wall of the uterine cavity.
    • Sperm production takes place inside the seminiferous tubules, which is a convoluted cluster of tubes located inside the testes. Testosterone production occurs in cells surrounding the seminiferous tubules, called Leydig cells.The ovaries produce the egg cells, called the ova or oocytes. The oocytes are then transported to the fallopian tube where fertilization by a sperm may occur. The fertilized egg then moves to the uterus, where the uterine lining has thickened in response to the normal hormones of the reproductive cycle.

  • 2. The fetal head is one centimeter below the ischial spine
  • 3. The fetal head has not yet entered the pelvic inlet
  • 4. The fetal head is located in the pelvic outlet

Solution:

  • In obstetrics, the station refers to the position of the fetal head in relation to the ischial spines of the pelvis. The ischial spines are considered 0 station.
    • A station of -1 means that the fetal head is 1 centimeter above the ischial spines and has not yet entered the pelvic inlet.

    Thus, a station of -1 indicates that the fetal head is still above the ischial spines, which means it has not yet entered the pelvic inlet.

Question No: 42

Whether the woman had ovulation or not on day 21 of the menstrual cycle will be best determined by:

Options:

  • 1. Serum Inhibin B
  • 2. Serum FSH
  • 3. Progesterone
  • 4. Serum LH

Solution:

    • Minimum levels of LH and FSH are seen in luteal phase (as high concentration of progesterone → has -ve feedback on LH and FSH).
    • Progesterone attains its highest peak about 8 days after the LH peak like day when corpus luteum has maximum function.
    • Progesterone levels are the best indicator of whether ovulation has occurred in the menstrual cycle. After ovulation, the corpus luteum (which forms from the ruptured follicle) secretes progesterone to prepare the endometrium for a potential pregnancy. If ovulation has occurred, progesterone levels rise significantly around day 21 of the menstrual cycle (which is typically in the luteal phase).
    • Here’s an explanation of the other options:
    • Option a. Serum Inhibin B: Inhibin B levels are higher during the follicular phase and drop after ovulation, but it is not as direct an indicator of whether ovulation has occurred as progesterone.
    • Option b. Serum FSH: Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) is involved in the development of ovarian follicles and rises at the beginning of the menstrual cycle. However, its levels are not a direct indicator of ovulation.
    • Option d. Serum LH: Luteinizing hormone (LH) surges just before ovulation (typically around day 14 of a 28-day cycle), but after ovulation, its levels drop. While the LH surge indicates imminent ovulation, progesterone is a more reliable marker for confirming ovulation, especially by day 21.

Solution:

    • Option-d: A reactive non-stress test indicates that the fetus is well oxygenated, but it does not provide information about the likely success or failure of labor induction.
