Question No: 1

Which of the following is the primary function of bacterial pili?

Options:

  • 1.

    Motility

  • 2.

    DNA transfer

  • 3.

    Attachment to host cells

  • 4.

    Synthesis of toxins

Solution:

    • Pili (also called fimbriae) are hair-like structures on the surface of many bacteria that help them attach to host cells, tissues, or surfaces. This attachment is crucial for the initiation of infection and is a key virulence factor.
    • Motility: While pili can assist in motility (e.g., through a process called "twitching"), their primary function is attachment, not motility.
    • DNA transfer: Pili are involved in conjugation, which is a form of horizontal gene transfer, but the primary function is attachment, not DNA transfer.
    • Synthesis of toxins: Pili do not synthesize toxins; toxins are produced by specific proteins or enzymes within the bacterial cell.

Question No: 2

The Gram stain differentiates bacteria based on differences in:

Options:

  • 1.

    Protein composition

  • 2.

    Nucleic acid sequence 

  • 3.

    Cell wall structure

  • 4.

    Ribosomal RNA 

Solution:

    • The Gram stain works by differentiating bacteria based on the structure of their cell walls. Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan layer that retains the crystal violet stain, appearing purple under a microscope. Gram-negative bacteria have a thinner peptidoglycan layer and an outer membrane, which does not retain the stain, appearing pink after counterstaining with safranin.   
    • Protein composition: While proteins can vary between bacteria, the Gram stain is based on cell wall structure, not protein composition.
    • Nucleic acid sequence: Nucleic acid sequence is used for molecular identification methods (like PCR), but not for Gram staining.
    • Ribosomal RNA: Ribosomal RNA is used for genetic analysis, but the Gram stain is not based on rRNA differences.

Question No: 5

Which of the following is a characteristic of viruses?

Options:

  • 1.

    They can reproduce on their own

  • 2.

    They are prokaryotic organisms 

  • 3.

    They lack a cellular structure

  • 4.

    They have a nucleus

Solution:

    • Viruses are not cellular organisms. They consist of a nucleic acid core (either DNA or RNA) surrounded by a protein coat (capsid), and they lack the cellular machinery required for reproduction, which they hijack from host cells.
    • They can reproduce on their own: Viruses cannot reproduce on their own; they need to infect a host cell to replicate and reproduce.
    • They are prokaryotic organisms: Viruses are not prokaryotic or eukaryotic; they are acellular entities.
    • They have a nucleus: Viruses do not have a nucleus or any other cellular organelles. They are much simpler than cellular organisms.

Question No: 11

Which of the following is not a method of horizontal gene transfer in bacteria?

Options:

  • 1.

    Transformation

  • 2.

    Conjugation

  • 3.

    Transduction

  • 4.

    Replication

Solution:

    • Replication is the process by which bacteria duplicate their own genetic material, and it is not a method of horizontal gene transfer. Horizontal gene transfer involves the movement of genetic material between organisms.
    • Transformation: Bacteria can take up free DNA from their environment and incorporate it into their own genome.
    • Conjugation: Bacteria can transfer genetic material directly from one cell to another through physical contact, typically via a plasmid.
    • Transduction: In transduction, bacteriophages (viruses) transfer genetic material between bacteria.

Question No: 15

Which of the following is the primary target of antibiotics in bacterial cells?

Options:

  • 1.

    Nucleic acids

  • 2.

    Cell membrane

  • 3.

    Ribosomes

  • 4.

    Peptidoglycan

Solution:

    • The cell wall, particularly peptidoglycan, is the primary target of many antibiotics, including penicillin. The inhibition of peptidoglycan synthesis prevents bacterial cells from maintaining their structural integrity, leading to cell lysis.
    • Nucleic acids: Some antibiotics target DNA or RNA synthesis, but peptidoglycan is the most common target for many antibiotics.
    • Cell membrane: Some antibiotics target cell membranes, but this is not as common as targeting the peptidoglycan in the bacterial cell wall.
    • Ribosomes: Some antibiotics do target bacterial ribosomes (e.g., tetracyclines), but they do so indirectly by affecting protein synthesis. The most direct and common target is the cell wall.

  • 2.

    The virus is dormant and cannot be detected in the host

  • 3.

    The virus integrates into the host's genome but does not replicate

  • 4.

    The virus causes rapid destruction of host cells

Solution:

    • In the latent phase of a viral infection, the virus remains dormant within the host's cells, often integrating into the host genome. The viral genome does not replicate actively, and there are no symptoms. This phase can be reactivated later.

    • The virus causes rapid destruction of host cells: This is characteristic of the lytic phase of viral infection, not the latent phase.
