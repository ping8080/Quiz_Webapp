Question No: 4

Positive Chvostek is a sign of:

Options:

  • 1. Hyperkalemia
  • 2. Hypocalcemia
  • 3. Hypercalcemia
  • 4. Hypokalemia

Solution:

    • Positive Chvostek is a sign of Hypocalcemia. In the late 1800s, Dr. Chvostek explained that in hypocalcemia, the examiner, mechanically stimulates the facial nerve with the fingertip, it could lead to twitching or tetany of the ipsilateral facial muscles. Hypocalcemia is a clinical condition in which the serum calcium level is going down due to any underlying cause such as disorders of the parathyroid gland or calcium deficiency in the diet .
    • Hyperkalemia (High Potassium Levels): Hyperkalemia affects the heart and muscle function, but it is not associated with the Chvostek sign. Instead, hyperkalemia may lead to changes in heart rhythm, including arrhythmias.
    • Hypercalcemia (High Calcium Levels): High calcium levels tend to decrease neuromuscular excitability, leading to muscle weakness and lethargy. A positive Chvostek sign would not be present in this case, as high calcium typically suppresses muscle contractions and spasms.
    • Hypokalemia (Low Potassium Levels): Like hyperkalemia, hypokalemia can cause muscle weakness and cramps, but it is not linked with the Chvostek sign. Potassium imbalances primarily affect muscle function and heart rhythm, rather than causing the characteristic twitching seen in hypocalcemia.

Solution:

    • Gout is a metabolic disorder caused by an imbalance in uric acid production and excretion. Hyperuricemia leads to the deposition of uric acid crystals in joints, resulting in inflammation and pain.

    Distractors:

Question No: 49

Electrically shockable rhythm is:

Solution:

Sinus Bradycardia is defined as heart rate of:

Options:

  • 1. Less than 40/min
  • 2. Less than 50 /min
  • 3. Less than 60/min
  • 4. Less than 70/min

Solution:

    • Sinus bradycardia is a cardiac rhythm with appropriate cardiac muscular depolarization initiating from the sinus node and a rate of fewer than 60 beats per minute (bpm).
    • The diagnosis requires an ECG showing a normal sinus rhythm at a rate lower than 60 bpm. 
    • It can be a normal physiological condition in athletes or highly conditioned individuals, as their hearts are more efficient.
    • It can also be caused by underlying conditions such as hypothyroidism, electrolyte imbalances, or heart disease.
