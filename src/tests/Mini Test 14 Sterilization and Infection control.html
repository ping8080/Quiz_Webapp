Question No: 1

Bacteriological loops used for culture media inoculation are sterilized by which of the following method?

Options:

  • 1.

    Autoclave

  • 2.

    Placing in flame three times

  • 3.

    Placing over flame until it becomes red hot

  • 4.

    Microwave

Solution:

  • Flaming is a common method used to sterilize bacteriological loops. The loop is held in a flame (usually a Bunsen burner) until it becomes red hot, ensuring that any microorganisms present are killed due to the high temperature. This method provides rapid and effective sterilization, making it suitable for microbiological procedures.

    Option-A: Autoclave:

    • Autoclaving is a method of sterilization that uses pressurized steam at 121°C for 15-20 minutes.
    • It is typically used for sterilizing glassware, surgical instruments, and culture media, not for immediate use of bacteriological loops during inoculation.

    Option-B: Placing in flame three times:

    • This technique is commonly used to disinfect slides and cover slips by passing them through a flame.
    • It is not suitable for sterilizing bacteriological loops, as it may not achieve the necessary temperature for complete sterilization.

    Option-D: Microwave:

    • Microwaves can sterilize some materials using heat generated by microwaves, but this is not a standard or practical method for sterilizing bacteriological loops.
    • Additionally, it lacks the immediate effectiveness and high temperature of direct flaming.

Question No: 2

Cold sterilization methods are all, except: -

Options:

  • 1.

    UV rays

  • 2.

    Autoclave

  • 3.

    Gamma ray

  • 4.

    Plasma Sterilisation

Solution:

    • Autoclaving relies on moist heat and pressure, making it a heat-based sterilization technique, not a cold method.
    • Cold sterilization refers to methods of sterilization that do not involve high temperatures. These methods primarily utilize physical or chemical agents to sterilize without applying heat.

    Autoclave:

    • An autoclave uses moist heat above 100°C at high pressure for sterilization.
    • Principle: High temperature and pressure destroy microbes, including spores.

    Option-A: UV Rays:

    • UV rays are a form of non-ionizing radiation.
    • They cause DNA damage through the formation of thymine-thymine dimers.
    • UV rays are commonly used for surface disinfection in hospitals, operation theatres, and laboratories.
    • Although not sporicidal and limited by low penetrative power, UV rays are considered a form of cold sterilization.

    Option-C: Gamma Rays:

    • Gamma rays are a form of ionizing radiation.
    • They sterilize by causing DNA damage and are highly penetrative.
    • Used commercially to sterilize disposable medical supplies (e.g., gloves, syringes, catheters, IV sets, and implants).
    • Gamma rays are classified as cold sterilization due to their ability to sterilize without the application of heat.

    Plasma Sterilization:

    • Plasma sterilization uses ionized gas (plasma) to sterilize instruments.
    • Plasma contains free radicals and ions that destroy microbes by attacking their cellular components, including DNA.
    • As it operates at low temperatures, it is considered a cold sterilization method.

Question No: 3

Out of the given lab sterilization techniques, which of the following can be used for a disposable pipette tip?

1. Autoclave

2. Hot air oven

3. Microwaving

4. Ethyl Alcohol 70%

Options:

  • 1.

    1 only

  • 2.

    4 only

  • 3.

    1 and 2

  • 4.

    3 and 4

Solution:

    • The most common and effective method to sterilize disposable pipette tips is by using an autoclave.
    • Autoclaving involves subjecting the pipette tips to high-pressure steam at temperatures above 121°C (250°F) for around 20 minutes. This process effectively kills microorganisms and sterilises the pipette tips for reuse. However, it’s important to check the manufacturer’s instructions to ensure that the pipette tips are autoclavable and to follow proper sterilisation protocols to maintain their integrity and functionality.

    Question No: 16

    Which of the following is an example of pre-current disinfection?

    Options:

    • 1.

      Chlorination of a well by village public health authorities

    • 2.

      Disinfection of kidney tray used during delivery of placenta

    • 3.

      Disinfection of bed linen after the death of a patient

    • 4.

      Disinfection of artery forceps following a surgery

    Solution:

      • Chlorination of water in a well is an example of precurrent disinfection.
      • Precurrent disinfection refers to prophylactic disinfection prior to discharge of infectious material. This includes pasteurization of milk, chlorination of water and hand washing.
      • Concurrent disinfection is the application of disinfection methods after discharge of infectious material from a person or soiling. This includes disinfection of urine, faeces, vomit, contaminated linen, clothes, hands.
      • Terminal disinfection is done after the death of a patient or transfer or after cessation of isolation.

    Question No: 17

    Rideal-Walker co-efficient is employed for the assessment of which of the following?

    Options:

    • 1.

      Sufficiency of pasteurization

    • 2.

      Effect of autoclaving

    • 3.

      Effect of dry heat sterilization

    • 4.

      Power of a disinfectant

    Solution:

      • The Rideal-Walker coefficient helps in assessing the power of a disinfectant relative to phenol.
      • It assesses this by determining the phenol coefficient (PC). A PC value of > 1 indicates that it is more effective than phenol. The higher the number, the greater its effectiveness in comparison to phenol and vice versa. Traditional organisms used to test the power of the disinfectant include S. aureus and Salmonella enteric subtype typhi.
      • Phenol coefficient = The dilution of test disinfectant which kills microorganisms in a given time ÷ The dilution of phenol which kills the microorganism in the same time under identical conditions.

      Other tests that are used for disinfectants are as follows:

      • Chick Martin test - modification of the Rideal-Walker test where disinfectants act in the presence of organic matter like feces or yeast to simulate natural conditions.
      • Kelsey-Skyes test (capacity test) - tests the capacity of a disinfectant to retain its activity when used repeatedly.
      • In-use test - determines whether the chosen disinfectant is effective in actual hospital use.

      Joseph Lister first introduced phenol (carbolic acid) as an antiseptic to sterilize surgical instruments and clean wounds. Lister's work led to a reduction in postoperative infections and made surgery safer for patients, distinguishing him as the 'Father of Modern Surgery' and 'Father of Antiseptic Surgery'.

    Options:

    • 1.

      Isopropyl alcohol

    • 2.

      Glutaraldehyde

    • 3.

      Hydrogen peroxide

    • 4.

      Benzalkonium chloride

    Solution:

      • Benzalkonium chloride is a quaternary ammonium compound that is used as a low-level
      • There are 3 types of disinfectants:
        • Low-level disinfectant - kills only vegetative bacteria, fungi and lipid enveloped viruses (e.g. quaternary ammonium compounds).
        • Intermediate level disinfectant: Kills all microbes including mycobacteria and non-enveloped viruses, but not spores (e.g. alcohol, iodophors).
        • High-level disinfectant - This is a chemical that kills all microbial pathogens and some spores if the contact time is increased (e.g. glutaraldehyde, hydrogen peroxide).
