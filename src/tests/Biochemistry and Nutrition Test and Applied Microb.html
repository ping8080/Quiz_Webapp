Question No: 25

What is the place within which microorganisms can thrive and reproduce?

Options:

  • 1.

    Portal of entry

  • 2.

    Reservoir

  • 3.

    Pathogenicity

  • 4.

    Portal of exit

Solution:

    • The reservoir is the place within which microorganisms can thrive and reproduce.
    • There are various links in the infection process:
      • First link – Pathogen, is the disease-causing organism. This can be a bacterium, virus, fungi, etc.
      • Second link - Reservoir: The natural environment that the pathogen requires for survival. Reservoirs can be a person, an animal, or an environmental component, such as soil or water.
      • Third link - Portal of exit: This link is needed for the pathogen to leave the reservoir. If the reservoir is a human, then the portal of exit maybe saliva, mucous membranes, feces, blood or nasal and throat discharges.
      • Fourth link - Means of transmission: The pathogen can be transmitted either directly or indirectly. Direct transmission requires close association with the infected host but not necessarily physical contact. Indirect transmission requires a vector, such as an animal or insect.
      • Fifth link - Portal of entry: The entry of the pathogen can take place in one of three ways: penetration, inhalation, or ingestion. The level and severity of infection may depend on the depth of penetration.
      • Sixth link - New host. Once in the new host, various factors influence the severity of infection including the strength of the immune system and the reproductive rate of the pathogen.

    • Infectivity is a pathogen’s capacity for horizontal transmission that is, how frequently it spreads among hosts that are not in a parent-child relationship.

  • 1. Type 4
  • 2. Type 1
  • 3. Type 2
  • 4. Type 3

Type 1 reactions are also known as IgE mediated hypersensitivity reactions.     • Type 2 or Cytotoxic mediated response is IgG and IgM mediated against cell surface and extracellular matrix proteins. The immunoglobulins involved in this type of reaction damage cells by activating the complement system or by phagocytosis. Type 2 hypersensitivity reactions can be seen in immune thrombocytopenia, autoimmune hemolytic anemia and autoimmune neutropenia.
    • Type 3 or Immunocomplex Reactions are mediated by IgM and IgG antibodies that react with soluble antigens forming antigen - antibody complexes. The complement system gets activated and releases chemotactic agents that attract Neutrophils and cause inflammation and tissue damage as seen in vasculitis and glomerulonephritis. It can be also seen in serum sickness.

The correct order of the phases in the bacterial growth curve is:

Options:

  • 1.

    Lag → Exponential → Stationary → Decline

  • 2.

    Lag → Stationary → Exponential → Decline

  • 3.

    Lag → Exponential → Decline → Stationary

  • 4.

    Lag → Decline → Exponential → Stationary

Solution:

  • Figure: Bacterial growth curve

    • Lag → Exponential → Stationary → Decline: It is the correct order of the phases in the bacterial growth curve.
      • Lag: Bacteria adapt themselves to growth conditions. Bacteria are maturing but not able to divide. This period of little to no cell division is called the lag phase and can last for 1 hour to several days.
      • Exponential: This phase is characterized by cell doubling. If the growth is not limited then doubling of bacteria is continued.
      • Stationary: Stationary phase results from a situation in which growth rate and death rate are equal. Mutation occurs in this phase but DNA damage can change the structure.
      • Decline: Bacteria die and this could cause by lack of nutrition and environmental temperature above or below the tolerance level.
