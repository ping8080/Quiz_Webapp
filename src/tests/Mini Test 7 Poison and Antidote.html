Question No: 1

The nurse is caring for an adult with myasthenia gravis who has been prescribed neostigmine. Which medication should the nurse have readily available because the patient is taking neostigmine? (Drugs used for treatment of poisoning, Miscellaneous, Pharmacology)

Options:

  • 1.

    Atropine

  • 2.

    Vitamin K

  • 3.

    Protamine sulfate

  • 4.

    Calcium gluconate

Solution:

    • Atropine – Correct Answer: Neostigmine is a cholinergic medication that increases the availability of acetylcholine, which can cause excessive cholinergic effects. Atropine is an anticholinergic medication that serves as the antidote for neostigmine in case of overdose or excessive cholinergic effects.
    • Vitamin K – Incorrect: Vitamin K is used to reverse the effects of warfarin (Coumadin), an anticoagulant, and is not related to the treatment of neostigmine or myasthenia gravis.
    • Protamine sulfate – Incorrect: Protamine sulfate is the antidote for heparin, a blood thinner, and does not have any effect in counteracting the actions of neostigmine.
    • Calcium gluconate – Incorrect: Calcium gluconate is used to treat calcium channel blocker overdose or magnesium sulfate toxicity, but it is not related to the management of neostigmine administration.

    Summary:

    Atropine is the appropriate medication to have available when a patient is taking neostigmine, as it can counteract excessive cholinergic effects. Other options like vitamin K, protamine sulfate, and calcium gluconate are used for different purposes unrelated to neostigmine toxicity.

Question No: 2

Which medication should the nurse have on hand for a client receiving heparin therapy? (Drugs used for treatment of poisoning, Miscellaneous, Pharmacology)

Options:

  • 1.

    Vitamin K

  • 2.

    Caffeine

  • 3.

    Calcium gluconate

  • 4.

    Protamine sulfate

Solution:

    • Vitamin K: Vitamin K is used to counteract the effects of warfarin (Coumadin), a medication that reduces blood clotting by inhibiting vitamin K-dependent clotting factors. It is not effective for reversing the anticoagulant effects of heparin.
    • Caffeine: Caffeine is a central nervous system stimulant that enhances alertness and increases heart rate. It is not an antidote for any anticoagulant medications, including heparin.
    • Calcium gluconate: Calcium gluconate is used to reverse the effects of magnesium sulfate toxicity, often in cases of severe preeclampsia or eclampsia. It has no role in reversing heparin's anticoagulant effects.
    • Protamine sulfate: Protamine sulfate is the specific antidote for heparin overdose 

Question No: 3

Activated charcoal is a: (Drugs used for treatment of poisoning, Miscellaneous, Pharmacology)

Options:

  • 1.

    Chemical antidote

  • 2.

    Mechanical antidote

  • 3.

    Chelating agent

  • 4.

    Demulcent

Solution:

  • Activated charcoal acts as a mechanical antidote.

    • Mechanical antidotes- They neutralize poisons by mechanical action or prevention of their absorption.
    • Activated charcoal- It acts mechanically by adsorbing and retaining organic and mineral poisons within its pores and thus delays the absorption from the stomach.
    • Demulcents- Substances that coat the gastric mucous membrane and thus do not permit the poisons to cause any damage, e.g. milk, starch, egg-white, mineral oil, milk of magnesia, aluminium hydroxide gel, etc.
    • Bulky food is the mechanical antidote to glass powder, wherein it acts by imprisoning its particles within its meshes.
    • Chemical antidotes- They neutralize poison by forming harmless or insoluble compounds or by oxidation when it is brought in contact with them.
    • Common salt decomposes silver nitrate, forming the insoluble silver chloride.
    • Potassium permanganate has oxidizing properties. 1 : 5000 solutions is used in opium poisoning.
    • Chelating agents (metal complexing agents) are used in the treatment of heavy metal poisoning. The metal-agent complex is more water-soluble than the metal itself, resulting in higher renal excretion of the complex.

A 40-year-old woman in labor presents with high blood pressure, swelling, and protein in her urine. She is started on magnesium sulfate. Which medication should the nurse ensure is readily available at the bedside? (Drugs used for treatment of poisoning, Miscellaneous, Pharmacology)

Options:

  • 1.

    Calcium gluconate

  • 2.

    Naloxone

  • 3.

    Phenytoin

  • 4.

    Glucose

Solution:

    • Calcium gluconate: Calcium gluconate is the antidote for magnesium sulfate toxicity. It is essential to have on hand because magnesium toxicity can cause severe respiratory depression, which calcium gluconate can counteract effectively.
    • Naloxone: Naloxone is a medication used to reverse the effects of opioid overdose, particularly respiratory depression. It is not effective against magnesium sulfate toxicity.
    • Phenytoin: Phenytoin is an anticonvulsant used to manage seizures, but it is not the preferred medication for women with preeclampsia and does not act as an antidote to magnesium sulfate.
    • Glucose: Glucose is administered to treat low blood sugar (hypoglycemia). It has no role in reversing magnesium sulfate's effects.

Question No: 6

Which of the following drugs can be used as an antidote for fibrinolytic therapy? (Drugs used for treatment of poisoning, Miscellaneous, Pharmacology)

Options:

  • 1.

    Protamine

  • 2.

    Streptokinase

  • 3.

    Heparin

  • 4.

    Ꜫ-Aminocaproic acid

Solution:

    • Ꜫ-Aminocaproic acid can be used as an antidote for fibrinolytic therapy.
    • It is a lysine analog that binds competitively to plasminogen, blocking plasminogen from binding to fibrin and the subsequent conversion to plasmin. This activity subsequently results in the inhibition of fibrinolysis.  
    • It should not be given unless hemorrhage is life-threatening because it inhibits intrinsic fibrinolytic activity and can precipitate DIC. Fresh frozen plasma and cryoprecipitate may also be used to replenish fibrin and clotting factors.

Question No: 7

A child arrives at the emergency department with nausea, vomiting, excessive salivation, muscle weakness, and reduced reflexes, approximately an hour after pesticide exposure. Which medications should the nurse prepare to administer? (Drugs used for treatment of poisoning, Miscellaneous, Pharmacology)

Options:

  • 1.

    Atropine and flumazenil

  • 2.

    Atropine and pralidoxime

  • 3.

    Epinephrine and naloxone

  • 4.

    Epinephrine and digoxin immune Fab

Solution:

    • Atropine and flumazenil: While atropine is an appropriate antidote for pesticide poisoning, flumazenil is used to reverse the effects of benzodiazepines, such as sedation and impaired motor function. It is not effective in treating organophosphate toxicity.
    • Atropine and pralidoxime: Pesticides, particularly organophosphates, inhibit cholinesterase, leading to excessive acetylcholine at neuromuscular junctions. Atropine counters the effects by blocking acetylcholine receptors, while pralidoxime reactivates cholinesterase enzymes, reversing the toxic effects. Together, they are the standard treatment for organophosphate poisoning.
    • Epinephrine and naloxone: Epinephrine is a cardiac stimulant and bronchodilator used for anaphylaxis and cardiac arrest. Naloxone is an antidote for opioid overdose. Neither medication addresses the effects of organophosphate poisoning.
    • Epinephrine and digoxin immune Fab: Digoxin immune Fab (Digibind) is a specific antidote for digoxin toxicity, and epinephrine is used for cardiovascular emergencies. These medications are unrelated to pesticide toxicity management.

A 30-year-old drowsy male was brought to the casualty. His pulse was 130/min, respiratory rate -30/min. His lab reports findings were ABG - metabolic acidosis, Total serum calcium - 6.8 mg/dL Blood Urea - 100 mg/dL, Creatinine - 3.4 mg/dL, Urine analysis - Calcium oxalate crystals. He showed improvement on nasogastric aspiration and administration of sodium bicarbonate, calcium gluconate, and fomipezole. What substance did he most likely consume? (Drugs used for treatment of poisoning, Miscellaneous, Pharmacology)

Options:

  • 1.

    Formaldehyde

  • 2.

    Ethylene glycol

  • 3.

    Paraldehyde

  • 4.

    Methyl alcohol

Solution:

    • The given scenario point towards ethylene glycol poisoning.
    • Ethylene glycol is converted to glyoxyaldehyde by alcohol dehydrogenase. Glyoxyaldehyde causes kidney damage.
    • Methanol is converted to formaldehyde by alcohol dehydrogenase. Formaldehyde causes retinal damage like blurring of vision/loss of vision.
    • The principles of treatment for both these conditions are the same. It predominantly involves using fomipezole or ethanol to inhibit the metabolic conversion of these relatively non-toxic alcohols to their toxic metabolites.

Question No: 10

A nurse is creating a care plan for a patient prescribed haloperidol, a traditional antipsychotic, to manage schizophrenia. Which medication should the nurse prepare to administer if the patient develops extrapyramidal side effects (EPS)? (Drugs used for treatment of poisoning, Miscellaneous, Pharmacology)

Options:

  • 1.

    Olanzapine

  • 2.

    Benztropine

  • 3.

    Chlorpromazine

  • 4.

    Escitalopram oxalate

Solution:

    • Olanzapine: Olanzapine is an atypical antipsychotic used to treat schizophrenia. While it may cause fewer extrapyramidal side effects compared to traditional antipsychotics, it is not used to treat EPS.
    • Benztropine: Benztropine is an anticholinergic medication commonly used to manage extrapyramidal side effects caused by traditional antipsychotic drugs like haloperidol. It helps alleviate symptoms such as muscle rigidity, tremors, and involuntary movements.
    • Chlorpromazine: Chlorpromazine is a traditional antipsychotic, like haloperidol, and may also cause extrapyramidal side effects. It is not used to treat EPS but rather to manage psychotic symptoms.
    • Escitalopram oxalate: Escitalopram oxalate is a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI) prescribed for depression and anxiety disorders. It is not effective in managing extrapyramidal side effects.

Question No: 11

A patient was brought to the emergency room with complaints of headache, vomiting, and blurring of vision after he consumed a bottle of illicit liquor. His ABG shows metabolic acidosis. Which of the following is used for the treatment of his condition? (Drugs used for treatment of poisoning, Miscellaneous, Pharmacology)

Options:

  • 1.

    Fomepizole

  • 2.

    Flumazenil

  • 3.

    N-acetyl cysteine

  • 4.

    Naloxone

Solution:

    • The above clinical scenario and the Arterial blood gas analysis (ABG) showing metabolic acidosis is suggestive of methanol toxicity. Fomepizole is used for the treatment of this condition.
    • Methanol poisoning is caused due to ingestion of illicit liquor which contains ethanol adulterated with methanol. Methanol is metabolized to formaldehyde and formic acid by alcohol and aldehyde dehydrogenases, respectively. The metabolites of methanol are responsible for the toxic effects.
    • Clinically, patients present with headache, vomiting, abdomen pain, uneasiness, confusion, delirium, and coma. Formic acid specifically damages the retina and optic nerve and results in blurring of vision and blindness.
    • Elevated levels of formic acid and other organic acids result in metabolic acidosis on ABG.
    • Treatment of methanol poisoning includes keeping the patient in a quiet, dark room to protect the eyes from the light. Gastric lavage with sodium bicarbonate if the patient is brought within 2 hours of ingesting methanol to combat acidosis. Ethanol infusion since ethanol is preferentially metabolized by alcohol dehydrogenase over methanol and helps by reducing the rate of generation of formaldehyde and formic acid.
    • Fomepizole is a specific inhibitor of alcohol dehydrogenase and the drug of choice for methanol poisoning. Folate therapy with calcium leucovorin reduces blood formate levels by enhancing its oxidation. Hemodialysis is used in severe cases of methanol toxicity.

    Other options:

    • Option B: Flumazenil is the inverse agonist of benzodiazepine receptors and is used for the treatment of benzodiazepine overdose.
    • Option C: N-acetyl cysteine is used as an antidote for the treatment of paracetamol poisoning.
    • Option D: Naloxone is a competitive opioid antagonist used for the treatment of opioid overdos

Question No: 12

A patient with leukemia is prescribed Trimetrexate. After reviewing the patient’s chart, the physician adds Leucovorin calcium to the treatment plan. The purpose of administering Leucovorin calcium in this case is to: (Drugs used for treatment of poisoning, Miscellaneous, Pharmacology)

Options:

  • 1.

    Address anemia caused by chemotherapy

  • 2.

    Enhance the effectiveness of treatment to reduce its duration

  • 3.

    Boost the production of neutrophils in the blood

  • 4.

    Counteract drug toxicity and safeguard tissues

Solution:

    • Address anemia caused by chemotherapy: Incorrect: Leucovorin calcium does not treat anemia, including iron-deficiency anemia, caused by chemotherapy. Its role is to counteract the effects of folic acid antagonists like Methotrexate or Trimetrexate, not to correct anemia.
    • Enhance the effectiveness of treatment to reduce its duration: Incorrect: Leucovorin does not have a synergistic effect with Trimetrexate. Instead, it is used as a protective agent to minimize harmful effects on healthy tissues caused by folic acid antagonists.
    • Boost the production of neutrophils in the blood: Incorrect: Leucovorin does not directly increase neutrophil counts. While it helps protect healthy cells from damage, it is not a hematopoietic agent. Neutrophil recovery in chemotherapy is managed with drugs like G-CSF (e.g., filgrastim).
    • Counteract drug toxicity and safeguard tissues: Correct: Leucovorin calcium is a folinic acid derivative that acts as a "rescue" agent by bypassing the blockade of folic acid metabolism caused by Trimetrexate or Methotrexate. This action prevents toxic effects on normal tissues without reducing the therapeutic effects of the chemotherapy.

Question No: 19

What medication should the nurse anticipate the healthcare provider to prescribe for a patient showing signs of a warfarin (Coumadin) overdose? (Drugs used for treatment of poisoning, Miscellaneous, Pharmacology)

Options:

  • 1. Heparin
  • 2. Vitamin K
  • 3. Iron dextran
  • 4. Protamine sulfate

Solution:

    • Warfarin inhibits the synthesis of clotting factors dependent on vitamin K. Administering vitamin K counteracts this effect by promoting the formation of these clotting factors, effectively reversing warfarin’s action.
    • Heparin: Heparin is an anticoagulant with a mechanism similar to warfarin. It is not used to reverse warfarin overdose and does not act as its antidote.
    • Iron dextran: Iron dextran is used to treat iron deficiency anemia. It has no role in managing anticoagulant overdose or reversing the effects of warfarin.
    • Protamine sulfate: Protamine sulfate is the antidote for heparin overdose, not warfarin. It neutralizes heparin’s anticoagulant effect but does not affect warfarin.
