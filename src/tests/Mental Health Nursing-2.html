Question No: 2

Adaptive mechanism of giving excuses when a person cannot solve a problem is known as:

Options:

  • 1. Projection
  • 2. Substitution
  • 3. Rationalization
  • 4. Compensation

Solution:

    • Rationalization is an adaptive mechanism where an individual justifies or explains their failure or inability to solve a problem by providing reasonable-sounding but often untrue or self-serving excuses. This helps to protect their self-esteem or avoid feelings of guilt.
    • Projection is a defense mechanism where a person attributes their own undesirable feelings, thoughts, or behaviors to someone else. Instead of acknowledging their own failure, they project the issue onto others.
    • Substitution involves replacing a desired but unattainable goal or object with one that is more attainable or less threatening. This mechanism helps individuals to avoid confronting difficult situations directly.
    • Compensation is a mechanism where a person makes up for their deficiencies in one area by excelling in another. It is a way to balance out feelings of inadequacy or failure in one domain by emphasizing success in another.

Question No: 3

The behaviour of a diabetic person who eats sweets regularly is an indication of:

Options:

  • 1. Denial
  • 2. Displacement
  • 3. Dislocation
  • 4. Fixation

Solution:

    • Denial is a defense mechanism where a person refuses to acknowledge the reality of a situation or the consequences of their actions, despite evidence to the contrary. In the case of a diabetic person eating sweets regularly, this behavior could be seen as a form of denial. The individual may be refusing to accept the severity of their medical condition (diabetes) or the impact that consuming sweets has on their health, despite knowing the risks.
    • Displacement is a defense mechanism in which an individual redirects their emotions or impulses from the original object to a safer or more acceptable target. This does not apply to the situation of a diabetic person eating sweets because it doesn’t involve redirecting emotions or impulses but rather ignoring the implications of the behavior.
    • Dislocation is not a psychological defense mechanism. It typically refers to a physical injury in which a bone is displaced from its joint. This term does not apply to the behavior described in the question.
    • Fixation refers to a psychological concept where an individual becomes stuck at a particular stage of development, often due to unresolved conflicts in earlier stages. While someone may display behaviors linked to earlier stages of development (such as childhood), this is not the most accurate explanation for a diabetic person eating sweets regularly.

While anxiety can cause distractibility, it does not inherently involve motor restlessness or agitation. It’s more about cognitive disturbances rather than physical restlessness.

Question No: 12

A strategy commonly used to improve therapeutic communication skills is called:

Options:

  • 1. Validation
  • 2. Active listening
  • 3. Process recording
  • 4. Restating and clarifying

Solution:

    • Active listening is a therapeutic communication strategy where the nurse pays full attention to the patient, understands the message, and responds appropriately. This fosters an effective nurse-patient relationship.
    • Validation: This involves acknowledging and confirming the patient’s feelings, thoughts, or concerns. While validation is important for effective communication, it is not a strategy aimed specifically at improving overall therapeutic communication skills like active listening.

While it helps in improving communication skills through reflection, it’s not a commonly used real-time strategy like active listening for enhancing therapeutic communication during the interaction.

While these are essential tools in communication, they are more specific techniques used to address misunderstandings rather than a broader communication strategy like active listening.
    • Active listening, as the correct answer, focuses on the overall process of listening attentively, understanding, and responding to patients, making it a key strategy to improve therapeutic communication.

Question No: 15

Perception without stimulus is called as:

Options:

  • 1. Delirium
  • 2. Illusion
  • 3. Delusion
  • 4. Hallucination

Solution:

    • Hallucination and illusion are both disorders of perceptions. Hallucination is a perception without some real stimuli/stimulus. For e.g. A person may complain that some insect is crawling on the body but there is no such insect seen in reality (tactile hallucination). A person may see (visual), hear (auditory), smells (olfactory), or tastes (gustatory) things that do not exist outside their mind.
    • On contrary, illusion refers to false perception of existing stimuli/stimulus. For e.g. A person may perceive a rope on the road as a snake. Here, the rope is considered as a stimulus, but the person perceived it as a snake (wrong perception).
    • Delirium is a disorder of consciousness characterized by a state of mental confusion, clouding of consciousness and excitement with disorientation for time and place, hallucinations, incoherent speech and continual state of aimless physical activity.
    • Delusions are disorders of thought characterized by false personal beliefs, not consistent with a person’s intelligence or cultural background.

Question No: 23

The neurotic defense mechanism includes all, except:

Options:

  • 1. Isolation
  • 2. Regression
  • 3. Reaction formation
  • 4. Undoing

Solution:

  • Neurotic defense mechanisms are psychological strategies used unconsciously to manage anxiety and emotional conflicts. Let’s go through the options:
    • Isolation: Involves separating emotions from thoughts or experiences, a common defense mechanism where a person may discuss distressing events without emotional involvement.
    • Regression: This involves reverting to an earlier stage of development or behavior when faced with stress, often seen in children but can occur in adults as well.

Question No: 24

Options:

  • 1. Benzodiazepines
  • 2. TCAs
  • 3. Progesterone
  • 4. SSRIs

Solution:

    • SSRIs are considered as DOC for premenstrual Dysphoric disorder.
      • Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD): Symptoms of mood lability, dysthymia, and anxiety occurring in the week preceding menses and minimal or absent in the week post menses.
      • The first-line treatment for PMDD is SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors), as they are effective in treating mood symptoms and improving emotional symptoms associated with PMDD. SSRIs like fluoxetine, sertraline, or citalopram are commonly prescribed for this condition.

      Thus, SSRIs are the most commonly prescribed and effective treatment for Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD).

Question No: 34

Why is glycopyrrolate given before the administration of Electroconvulsive therapy?

Options:

  • 1. For muscle relaxation
  • 2. Reduces incidence of Bradycardia
  • 3. For relaxing the patient
  • 4. Neuromuscular blockage

Solution:

    • Glycopyrrolate is an anticholinergic medication often given before Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT) to reduce the risk of bradycardia (slow heart rate) that can occur due to the parasympathetic effects of ECT. It works by blocking the action of acetylcholine on the heart, thereby preventing a drop in heart rate and stabilizing the cardiovascular response during the procedure. It is not given for muscle relaxation or neuromuscular blockade, as these effects are typically managed by other medications like muscle relaxants (e.g., succinylcholine).

    • Each electrode site is labeled with a letter and a number. The letter refers to the area of brain underlying the electrode; e.g., F- Frontal lobe and T - Temporal lobe. Even numbers denote the right side of the head and odd numbers denote the left side of the head.
    • The “10” and “20” refer to the fact that the actual distances between adjacent electrodes are either 10% or 20% of the total front–back or right–left distance of the skull.

Question No: 36

Frequency range of theta rhythm on EEG is:

Options:

  • 1. 1-3 Hz
  • 2. 4-7 Hz
  • 3. 10-12 Hz
  • 4. 18-20 Hz

Solution:

    • The electroencephalogram (EEG) is a recording of the electrical activity of the brain from the scalp. The recorded waveforms reflect the cortical electrical activity.
    • Signal intensity: EEG activity is quite small, measured in microvolts (mV).
    • Signal frequency: The main frequencies of the human EEG waves are:
      • Delta: It has a frequency of 3 Hz or below. It tends to be the highest in amplitude and the slowest waves. It is normal as the dominant rhythm in infants up to one year and in stages 3 and 4 of sleep. It may occur focally with subcortical lesions and in general distribution with diffuse lesions, metabolic encephalopathy hydrocephalus or deep midline lesions. It is usually most prominent frontally in adults (e.g., FIRDA - Frontal Intermittent Rhythmic Delta) and posteriorly in children e.g., OIRDA - Occipital Intermittent Rhythmic Delta).
      • Theta: It has a frequency of 3.5 to 7.5 Hz and is classified as “slow” activity. It is perfectly normal in children up to 13 years and in sleep but abnormal in awake adults. It can be seen as a manifestation of focal subcortical lesions; it can also be seen in generalized distribution in diffuse disorders such as metabolic encephalopathy or some instances of hydrocephalus.
      • Alpha: It has a frequency between 7.5 and 13 Hz. It is usually best seen in the posterior regions of the head on each side, being higher in amplitude on the dominant side. It appears when closing the eyes and relaxing, and disappears when opening the eyes or alerting by any mechanism (thinking, calculating). It is the major rhythm seen in normal relaxed adults. It is present during most of life especially after the thirteenth year.
      • Beta: Beta activity is a “fast” activity. It has a frequency of 14 and greater Hz. It is usually seen on both sides in symmetrical distribution and is most evident frontally. It is accentuated by sedative-hypnotic drugs especially the benzodiazepines and the barbiturates. It may be absent or reduced in areas of cortical damage. It is generally regarded as a normal rhythm. It is the dominant rhythm in patients who are alert or anxious or have their eyes open.

Question No: 42

Delirium is not the primary psychiatric manifestation associated with postpartum psychosis.
