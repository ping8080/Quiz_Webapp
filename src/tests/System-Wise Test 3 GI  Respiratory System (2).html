What causes an increase in a patient’s respiratory rate when there is an excess of carbon dioxide (CO2) in the blood ?

Options:

  • 1.

    CO2 reduces oxygen binding to hemoglobin, lowering PaO2.

  • 2.

    CO2 raises the levels of hydrogen ions in the body.

  • 3.

    CO2 reacts with water to form carbonic acid, decreasing the pH of cerebrospinal fluid.

  • 4.

    CO2 directly activates chemoreceptors in the medulla to elevate respiratory rate and volume.

Solution:

  • High levels of CO2 react with water to produce carbonic acid, which lowers the pH of cerebrospinal fluid. This triggers central chemoreceptors to stimulate the respiratory center, increasing the respiratory rate to remove excess CO2 and restore pH balance.

    Explanations of Options:

    • CO2 reduces oxygen binding to hemoglobin, lowering PaO2: Although CO2 can decrease oxygen binding to hemoglobin (Bohr effect), the primary trigger for increased respiratory rate is CO2's effect on blood pH and chemoreceptor activity, not its displacement of oxygen.
    • CO2 raises the levels of hydrogen ions in the body: Excess CO2 combines with water to form carbonic acid, which dissociates into hydrogen ions and bicarbonate. This is an indirect process, and CO2 itself does not directly increase hydrogen ion production in the body.
    • CO2 directly activates chemoreceptors in the medulla to elevate respiratory rate and volume: CO2 does not directly stimulate medullary chemoreceptors. Instead, it lowers cerebrospinal fluid pH, which is detected by chemoreceptors, indirectly leading to an increase in respiratory rate and volume.
