Question No: 1

A 55-year-old male patient presents with hypertension. He has a history of diabetes mellitus and chronic kidney disease (CKD). Which of the following is the most appropriate first-line antihypertensive therapy for this patient?

Options:

  • 1.

    Calcium channel blockers (CCBs)

  • 2.

    ACE inhibitors (ACE-Is)

  • 3.

    Beta blockers

  • 4.

    Thiazide diuretics

Solution:

    • ACE inhibitors are the drug of choice (DOC) in hypertensive patients with diabetes mellitus and CKD due to their ability to:
      • Reduce intraglomerular pressure: ACE-Is dilate the efferent arterioles, thereby decreasing glomerular hypertension and protecting against progression of diabetic nephropathy.
      • Reduce albuminuria: They significantly lower proteinuria, which is a key marker for CKD progression.
      • Cardiovascular benefits: ACE-Is reduce the risk of major cardiovascular events, which is vital in diabetic and CKD populations at high cardiovascular risk.
      • Glycemic neutrality: Unlike some other antihypertensives, ‘ACE-Is’ do not worsen glycemic control, making them safer for diabetic patients.

    Distractors:

    Calcium channel blockers (CCBs):

    • While CCBs effectively lower blood pressure, they do not provide the renal protective benefits of ACE-Is or ARBs.
    • CCBs are preferred in isolated systolic hypertension or when ACE-Is are not tolerated, but they are not the first-line choice in CKD with diabetes.

    Beta blockers:

    • Beta blockers are not first-line agents for hypertension with diabetes or CKD.
    • They can mask hypoglycemia in diabetes and do not have significant effects on reducing proteinuria or preserving renal function.
    • Reserved for specific indications like ischemic heart disease or heart failure in these patients.

    Thiazide diuretics:

    • Thiazides are effective in controlling blood pressure but may exacerbate hyperglycemia and hyperlipidemia in diabetic patients.
    • They lack the renoprotective and proteinuria-reducing effects of ‘ACE-Is’, making them less favorable in CKD.

Question No: 2

A 45-year-old male with a history of hypertension was prescribed clonidine for blood pressure control. He decides to abruptly stop taking the medication. Which of the following adverse effects is most likely to occur?

Options:

  • 1.

    Rebound hypertension

  • 2.

    Orthostatic hypotension

  • 3.

    Bradycardia

  • 4.

    Hyperkalemia

Solution:

    • Clonidine withdrawal hypertension is a classic example of Type E (End of Use) ADR.
    • Clonidine is a centrally acting alpha-2 agonist that suppresses sympathetic outflow from the CNS, leading to reduced blood pressure. Abrupt cessation causes a rebound surge of sympathetic activity, resulting in rebound hypertension.
    • The severity of rebound hypertension depends on the dose and duration of clonidine use. Tapering the medication gradually prevents this complication.

    Distractors:

    • Orthostatic hypotension: Orthostatic hypotension is not associated with clonidine withdrawal but may occur as a side effect during clonidine use due to its blood pressure-lowering properties.
    • Bradycardia: Bradycardia is an expected effect of clonidine due to its ability to reduce sympathetic tone, but it does not occur during withdrawal. Instead, withdrawal leads to tachycardia due to increased sympathetic activity.
    • Hyperkalemia: Hyperkalemia is not a known adverse effect of clonidine withdrawal. It may be associated with other drugs like ACE inhibitors, potassium-sparing diuretics, or aldosterone antagonists.

A 50-year-old male with a history of alcoholic liver disease presents with abdominal distension and lower limb swelling. On examination, he has tense ascites and bilateral pedal edema. Blood tests reveal low serum albumin, elevated liver enzymes, and hyperaldosteronism. Despite being on furosemide therapy, his symptoms persist. Which of the following is the most appropriate addition to his treatment plan?

Options:

  • 1.

    Hydrochlorothiazide

  • 2.

    Spironolactone

  • 3.

    Mannitol

  • 4.

    Eplerenone

Solution:

    • Spironolactone: Cirrhotic edema and ascites in liver disease are driven by secondary hyperaldosteronism, which leads to sodium retention and potassium excretion.
    • Spironolactone, a non-selective aldosterone antagonist, directly inhibits aldosterone activity, reducing sodium and water retention while conserving potassium.
    • It is particularly effective for patients with liver disease, as it addresses the pathophysiological cause of fluid accumulation rather than just symptom control.
    • Studies demonstrate that spironolactone is the first-line therapy for managing ascites and peripheral edema in cirrhotic patients.

    Distractors:

    Hydrochlorothiazide:

    • Thiazide diuretic that acts on the distal convoluted tubule to promote sodium and water excretion.
    • Effective for hypertension and mild edema, but it does not target the hyperaldosteronism seen in cirrhotic patients.
    • Can cause hypokalemia, which is especially undesirable in patients with liver disease.

    Mannitol:

    • Osmotic diuretic used for conditions such as increased intracranial pressure or acute glaucoma.
    • It increases plasma osmolality, pulling water out of tissues into the bloodstream, but it does not address sodium retention or aldosterone-mediated fluid retention.
    • Not suitable for treating ascites or peripheral edema in cirrhosis.

    Eplerenone:

    • A selective aldosterone antagonist that avoids the anti-androgenic and progestogenic side effects of spironolactone.
    • Primarily indicated for hypertension and heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), not as effective in severe ascites due to cirrhosis.
    • It is costlier and less commonly used for cirrhotic edema compared to spironolactone.

Question No: 4

Which of the following best describes the pharmacological action of buprenorphine?

Options:

  • 1.

    Full agonist at mu and partial agonist at kappa receptors

  • 2.

    Partial agonist at mu and antagonist at kappa receptors

  • 3.

    Antagonist at both mu and kappa receptors

  • 4.

    Full agonist at both mu and kappa receptors

Solution:

  • Mu-Opioid Receptor (MOR) as a Partial Agonist:

    • Buprenorphine is a partial agonist at the mu-opioid receptor. This means it activates the receptor to produce analgesic effects (pain relief), but its effect is limited compared to full agonists like morphine or fentanyl.
    • This ceiling effect at the mu receptor means that buprenorphine does not cause the same level of respiratory depression, euphoria, or sedation seen with full agonists, making it safer for long-term use, particularly in the treatment of opioid use disorder (OUD).
    • This partial activation provides effective pain relief with less risk of addiction and overdose compared to other opioids.

    Kappa-Opioid Receptor (KOR) Antagonism:  

    • Buprenorphine acts as an antagonist at the kappa-opioid receptor. The kappa receptors are associated with dysphoric effects, psychotomimetic effects (hallucinations, confusion), and depression.
    • By blocking these receptors, buprenorphine reduces the potential for negative side effects like dysphoria and hallucinations, which are commonly seen with drugs that act on kappa receptors (such as salvinorin A or nalbuphine).
    • This receptor profile enhances buprenorphine's tolerability in patients, especially in opioid dependence treatment, where stabilization without euphoria or intense cravings is a key goal.

    Distractors:

    • Full agonist at mu and partial agonist at kappa receptors: Incorrect: Buprenorphine is not a full agonist at the mu receptor. It is a partial agonist at mu receptors, meaning it has a limited effect on mu receptor activation, providing a ceiling effect and lower risk of respiratory depression. It also blocks kappa receptors, not activating them.
    • Antagonist at both mu and kappa receptors: Incorrect: Buprenorphine is not an antagonist at the mu receptor. Instead, it partially activates the mu receptor, which is key to its analgesic action. It blocks the kappa receptor, but it does not block mu.
    • Full agonist at both mu and kappa receptors: Incorrect: Buprenorphine is not a full agonist at either the mu or kappa It has partial agonist activity at mu receptors and antagonist activity at kappa receptors, which differentiates it from drugs that activate both receptors fully (like morphine).

A 58-year-old male patient with a history of chronic stable angina is admitted to the hospital with increasing chest pain despite taking his usual nitroglycerin tablets. The cardiologist decides to start the patient on a new medication to improve his symptoms and reduce the frequency of angina attacks. The doctor prescribes Nicorandil. Which of the following best explains the mechanism of action of Nicorandil in relieving the patient’s angina?

Options:

  • 1.

    Nicorandil inhibits the sodium-potassium ATPase pump in the myocardial cells, reducing myocardial contractility.

  • 2.

    Nicorandil activates the ATP-sensitive potassium channels, leading to coronary vasodilation and reduced myocardial oxygen demand.

  • 3.

    Nicorandil stimulates the beta-adrenergic receptors in the heart, increasing heart rate and improving myocardial oxygen supply.

  • 4.

    Nicorandil increases the release of nitric oxide from endothelial cells, causing systemic vasoconstriction.

Solution:

    • Nicorandil activates the ATP-sensitive potassium channels, leading to coronary vasodilation and reduced myocardial oxygen demand: Nicorandil is a potassium channel opener that works by activating ATP-sensitive potassium (K_ATP) channels in vascular smooth muscle. This leads to vasodilation, particularly in the coronary arteries, which increases blood flow to the heart muscle. This effect helps reduce the myocardial oxygen demand and improves oxygen delivery to the heart, which is essential for managing chronic stable angina. By reducing the workload of the heart and increasing coronary blood flow, Nicorandil helps prevent angina attacks.
    • Nicorandil inhibits the sodium-potassium ATPase pump in the myocardial cells, reducing myocardial contractility: This description refers to the mechanism of digoxin, not Nicorandil. Digoxin inhibits the Na+/K+ ATPase pump, leading to increased intracellular calcium levels and enhanced myocardial contractility, but this is not how Nicorandil works. Nicorandil’s action is primarily related to potassium channel opening and vasodilation, not directly influencing myocardial contractility.
    • Nicorandil stimulates the beta-adrenergic receptors in the heart, increasing heart rate and improving myocardial oxygen supply: This is the mechanism of action of beta-agonists like isoproterenol or dobutamine, which stimulate beta-adrenergic receptors and increase heart rate and contractility. Nicorandil, however, does not act on adrenergic receptors but on potassium channels to induce vasodilation and reduce myocardial oxygen demand.
    • Nicorandil increases the release of nitric oxide from endothelial cells, causing systemic vasoconstriction: This is incorrect because while nitric oxide plays a role in the vasodilation effects of some drugs (e.g., nitrates), Nicorandil does not induce vasoconstriction. Instead, it induces vasodilation via potassium channel opening and also has nitrate-like effects. The statement about systemic vasoconstriction is contrary to the mechanism of Nicorandil.

A 72-year-old female patient with a history of acute deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is being considered for anticoagulation therapy. The physician is looking for an anticoagulant that inhibits only Factor Xa, and the patient requires subcutaneous administration for both prophylaxis and treatment. After evaluating the patient’s medical history and the desired drug properties, the physician prescribes an anticoagulant. Which of the following anticoagulants inhibits only Factor Xa and is most appropriate for this patient?

Options:

  • 1.

    Unfractionated Heparin (UFH)

  • 2.

    Low Molecular Weight Heparin (LMWH)

  • 3.

    Fondaparinux

  • 4.

    Warfarin

Solution:

    • Fondaparinux:
      • Fondaparinux is a selective Factor Xa inhibitor that works by binding to antithrombin III, which then specifically inhibits Factor Xa. It does not inhibit Factor IIa (thrombin), making it distinct from unfractionated heparin (UFH) and LMWH.
      • Fondaparinux is administered subcutaneously and is used for both prophylaxis and treatment of thromboembolic events like DVT, pulmonary embolism (PE), and in hip and knee surgeries.
      • Fondaparinux has a longer half-life and does not require routine monitoring (like UFH), making it more convenient for outpatient use or for patients who are not easily accessible for frequent lab testing.
    • Unfractionated Heparin (UFH):
      • UFH is a non-selective anticoagulant that inhibits both Factor Xa and Factor IIa (thrombin). It requires frequent monitoring via aPTT (activated partial thromboplastin time) and is typically administered intravenously for treatment or subcutaneously for prophylaxis. Therefore, it does not fit the criteria of selectively inhibiting only Factor Xa.
    • Low Molecular Weight Heparin (LMWH):
      • LMWH inhibits both Factor Xa and to a lesser extent Factor IIa. It is more selective for Factor Xa than UFH, but it still affects Factor IIa, making it less specific than Fondaparinux. It is commonly used for DVT prophylaxis and treatment, but it does not exclusively inhibit Factor Xa.
    • Warfarin:
      • Warfarin is a vitamin K antagonist that inhibits the synthesis of clotting factors II, VII, IX, and X, but it does not directly inhibit Factor Xa. It works through a completely different mechanism than Fondaparinux and is typically used for long-term anticoagulation and not for acute treatment like Fondaparinux. Additionally, warfarin requires monitoring of INR (International Normalized Ratio).

    Glibenclamide

    Metformin

Solution:

    • Metformin is a biguanide and the first-line treatment for Type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM), polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), and metabolic syndrome.

      • Glibenclamide works by stimulating insulin release from pancreatic beta cells, but it does not affect hepatic gluconeogenesis or AMPK.       • Insulin is primarily used in Type 1 diabetes and in advanced Type 2 diabetes when oral agents are ineffective. Insulin does not act on AMPK or hepatic gluconeogenesis and is typically reserved for later stages of diabetes or for patients with insulin deficiency.

      • Acarbose inhibits alpha-glucosidase, reducing the breakdown of carbohydrates in the intestine, which decreases post-meal glucose spikes. However, it does not affect hepatic gluconeogenesis or AMPK, making it less effective as a primary agent in managing PCOS or metabolic syndrome.

Question No: 10

A 65-year-old male with congestive heart failure presents with symptoms of nausea, yellow vision, and palpitations. ECG shows an arrhythmia. Which of the following is the most characteristic arrhythmia caused by digoxin toxicity?

Options:

  • 1.

    Ventricular bigeminy

  • 2.

    Atrial tachycardia with AV block

  • 3.

    Atrial fibrillation

  • 4.

    Ventricular tachycardia

Solution:

    • Digoxin inhibits the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump, leading to an increase in intracellular Na⁺, which promotes Ca²⁺ influx via the Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger. This enhances myocardial contractility (positive inotropy) and slows conduction through the AV node (negative chronotropy). However, toxicity occurs with excessive levels, resulting in:
    • Increased automaticity: Due to calcium overload, ectopic foci in the atria become hyperactive.
    • Decreased AV node conduction: Digoxin promotes vagal activity, leading to AV block.
    • Combination of effects: This unique interplay causes atrial tachycardia with AV block, the most characteristic arrhythmia of digoxin toxicity. The atrial tachycardia arises from ectopic atrial foci, while AV block results from suppressed conduction through the AV node.

    Distractors:

    • Ventricular bigeminy: Most common arrhythmia seen in digoxin toxicity but not the most characteristic. Bigeminy arises due to enhanced ectopic ventricular foci, reflecting increased automaticity. It lacks the hallmark combination of atrial tachycardia with AV block seen in digoxin toxicity.
    • Atrial fibrillation: Digoxin is used to manage atrial fibrillation due to its ability to slow AV node conduction, controlling ventricular response. While digoxin toxicity may exacerbate ectopic atrial activity, it more commonly causes atrial tachycardia with AV block, not atrial fibrillation.
    • Ventricular tachycardia: This is rare in digoxin toxicity and generally occurs in severe cases, especially with coexisting risk factors such as hypokalemia. While digoxin-induced ventricular arrhythmias are dangerous, atrial tachycardia with AV block is more characteristic.

    Important Clinical Points from the Content:  

    Side Effects of Digoxin Toxicity:

    1. Most common and earliest signs:
      • Nausea and vomiting due to stimulation of the chemoreceptor trigger zone.
    2. Specific symptom:
      • Xanthopsia (yellow vision) caused by effects on the retina.
    3. Gynecomastia:                
      • Rarely occurs due to digoxin’s estrogenic effects.

    Arrhythmias in Digoxin Toxicity:

    1. Most common: Ventricular bigeminy.
    2. Most characteristic: Atrial tachycardia with AV block.

    Risk Factors for Digoxin Toxicity:

    (Mnemonic: KMC in Manipal Rocks)

    • K: Hypokalemia - Reduced K⁺ enhances digoxin binding to Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase, increasing its toxic effects.
    • M: Hypomagnesemia - Low Mg²⁺ exacerbates arrhythmias by altering myocardial electrical activity.
    • C in: Hypercalcemia - Increased Ca²⁺ amplifies digoxin-induced calcium overload.
    • Manipal: Myocardial infarction (MI) - Ischemic myocardium is more prone to digoxin-induced arrhythmias.
    • Rocks: Renal failure - Impaired excretion of digoxin raises plasma levels, increasing toxicity risk.

    Contraindications:

    • WPW Syndrome (Wolff-Parkinson-White):
      • Digoxin enhances conduction through the accessory pathway, worsening tachyarrhythmias.
    • HOCM (Hypertrophic Obstructive Cardiomyopathy):
      • Positive inotropic effects can exacerbate obstruction in HOCM, worsening symptoms.

    Management of Digoxin Toxicity:

    • Antidote: Digibind (digoxin-specific antibody fragments) binds free digoxin, neutralizing its effects.
    • Supportive care: Correct hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia to reduce arrhythmogenic risk.
    • Arrhythmias: Manage severe arrhythmias with antiarrhythmic drugs (e.g., lidocaine) as indicated.

      • Use: A loop diuretic sometimes used to manage fluid overload but has no specific protective effects against cisplatin-induced nephrotoxicity. Additionally, it may exacerbate electrolyte imbalances, increasing toxicity risks.

    Furosemide

      • The most ototoxic loop diuretic, with limited use due to its higher risk of hearing damage.
