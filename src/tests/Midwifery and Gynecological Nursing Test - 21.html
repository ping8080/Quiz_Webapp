Question No: 1

Frequency of micturition is an important symptom of pregnancy during first trimester. It occurs due to:

Options:

  • 1.

    Resting of the enlarged uterus on the bladder.

  • 2.

    Increment in blood volume.

  • 3.

    Maternal weight gain.

  • 4.

    All

Solution:

    • During the first trimester of pregnancy, the uterus is still contained within the pelvis and can put pressure on the bladder, leading to an increase in frequency of urination.
    • The other options are not the primary cause of increased frequency of urination during the first trimester.
    • Increment in blood volume: While blood volume does increase during pregnancy, this is more significant in the second and third trimesters.
    • Maternal weight gain: Weight gain is not typically significant enough in the first trimester to cause increased frequency of urination.
    • All: Not all of the options are correct.

Question No: 2

    Cervix becomes soft

  • 2.

    Cervix feels like the lips of the mouth

  • 3.

    Bluish discoloration of the cervix

  • 4.

    Cervix feels like the tip of nose.

Solution:

  • During pregnancy, the cervix undergoes changes, including:

    • Softening of the cervix
    • Cervix feels like the lips of the mouth (due to increased vascularity and softening)

Question No: 5

Total number of antenatal visit by a pregnant lady should be at least:

Options:

  • 1.

    2

  • 2.

    8

  • 3.

    1

  • 4.

    5

Solution:

  • The World Health Organization (WHO) recommends a minimum of 8 antenatal visits for a healthy pregnancy. This is based on research showing that more frequent visits help in early identification of problems and better health outcomes for both the mother and the baby.

    Breakdown of Antenatal Visits:       

    The 8 visits are typically spaced as follows:

    • First Visit (First trimester, up to 12 weeks):
      • Confirmation of pregnancy.
      • Screening for early complications (e.g., ectopic pregnancy, molar pregnancy).
      • Assessing maternal health (blood pressure, weight, medical history).
      • Routine blood tests (blood group, hemoglobin, infections, etc.).
      • Early ultrasound for dating the pregnancy.
      • Counseling on nutrition, lifestyle, and other preventive measures.
    • Second Visit (12-16 weeks):
      • Monitoring of growth and fetal heart rate.
      • Screening for risks of Down's syndrome (e.g., screening tests).
      • Blood pressure and urine tests for any signs of complications (e.g., preeclampsia).
      • Counseling on the importance of folic acid and iron supplements.
    • Third Visit (16-20 weeks):
      • Fetal growth monitoring.
      • Screen for anemia, urine infections, and gestational diabetes.
      • Screening for fetal anomalies (e.g., ultrasound).
      • Discussion of any symptoms or changes in the pregnancy.
    • Fourth Visit (20-24 weeks):
      • Check for fetal movements, heart rate, and growth.
      • Routine check for maternal health, including blood pressure and weight gain.
      • Urine and blood tests.
      • Addressing issues related to gestational diabetes or anemia if present.
    • Fifth Visit (24-28 weeks):
      • Gestational diabetes screening (e.g., glucose tolerance test).
      • Monitoring of fetal heart rate and growth.
      • Evaluation of swelling (edema) or high blood pressure, which could indicate preeclampsia.
    • Sixth Visit (28-32 weeks):
      • Check maternal blood pressure and urine for protein.
      • Measure the baby’s growth and fetal heart rate.
      • Review of any concerns, including swelling or headaches, which could be signs of pregnancy complications.
    • Seventh Visit (32-36 weeks):
      • Monitoring fetal growth, heart rate, and movements.
      • Check for signs of complications like high blood pressure.
      • Position of the baby (to check if the baby is in the head-down position for delivery).
      • Prepare for labor, including a discussion of signs of labor and birth plans.
    • Eighth Visit (36-40 weeks):
      • Checking for fetal position, heart rate, and movements.
      • Monitoring maternal health (e.g., blood pressure, urine, swelling).
      • Final preparations for labor and delivery.
      • Discussion about postnatal care and breastfeeding.

Question No: 6

A pregnant woman at 32 weeks of gestation presents with a sudden onset of severe abdominal pain and vaginal bleeding. What is the most likely diagnosis?

Options:

  • 1.

    Placenta previa

  • 2.

    Placental abruption

  • 3.

    Uterine rupture

  • 4.

    Ectopic pregnancy

Solution:

    • The symptoms of severe abdominal pain and vaginal bleeding at 32 weeks gestation are most consistent with placental abruption, which is a separation of the placenta from the uterus.
    • Placenta previa: This condition involves the placenta covering the cervix, but it typically presents with painless vaginal bleeding.
    • Uterine rupture: This is a life-threatening emergency that can cause severe abdominal pain, but it is less likely to present with vaginal bleeding.
    • Ectopic pregnancy: This condition typically presents earlier in pregnancy (before 20 weeks) and is less likely to present with severe abdominal pain and vaginal bleeding at 32 weeks of gestation.

Solution:

Question No: 12

A woman is diagnosed with a vaginal yeast infection. What is the most appropriate nursing action? 

Options:

  • 1.

    Administer antibiotics as ordered

  • 2.

    Apply topical antifungal cream as ordered

  • 3.

    Encourage the woman to take a warm bath to relieve symptoms

  • 4.

    Recommend that the woman abstain from sexual intercourse for a certain period 

Solution:

  • Topical antifungal creams are effective against vaginal yeast infections.

    • Topical antifungal creams (e.g., clotrimazole, miconazole, or terconazole) are the first-line treatment for localized vaginal yeast infections. These medications work by inhibiting the growth of the Candida fungus, helping to relieve symptoms and clear the infection.
    • The prescribed medication should be applied as ordered, and it’s important to follow the full course of treatment to ensure the infection is completely eradicated.
    • Antibiotics are used to treat bacterial infections, not fungal infections. Since a yeast infection is caused by a fungus, antibiotics would not be effective and could actually worsen the condition by disrupting the balance of normal vaginal flora, potentially leading to more fungal overgrowth.
    • Option C) Encourage the woman to take a warm bath to relieve symptoms: While a warm bath may provide temporary relief from itching or discomfort, it does not address the underlying infection. In fact, prolonged exposure to moisture may make the infection worse or increase the risk of further irritation. Therefore, it's not a treatment for the infection itself.
    • Option D) Recommend that the woman abstain from sexual intercourse for a certain period: While sexual intercourse is not a direct cause of yeast infections, it can sometimes worsen symptoms or cause discomfort due to irritation. It is generally recommended to abstain from intercourse during treatment to avoid potential irritation or reinfection. However, it is not a primary treatment strategy for the infection.

Question No: 13

In calendar month of contraception, first risky day is calculated as:

Options:

  • 1.

    Longest cycle – 10

  • 2.

    Shortest cycle – 10

  • 3.

    Longest cycle – 18

  • 4.

    Shortest cycle – 10

Solution:

    • The calendar method of contraception (also called the rhythm method) involves tracking a woman's menstrual cycle to predict the days of fertility. The fertile period is calculated by identifying the first risky day (the first day when intercourse could potentially lead to pregnancy) and the last risky day.
    • To calculate the first risky day, you use the longest cycle (the longest number of days in the cycle over the past 6–12 months) and subtract 18. This gives the first day of the fertile period.

    For example:

    • If the longest cycle in the past months was 30 days, then: 30 (longest cycle) – 18 = 12. This means that day 12 is the first potentially fertile (risky) day.

    This helps in predicting the fertile window, and couples trying to avoid pregnancy would typically avoid intercourse during this period.

    Why the other options are incorrect:

    • Longest cycle – 10: This is not a correct formula for calculating the first risky day.
    • Shortest cycle – 10: This method is used to calculate the last risky day, not the first.
    • Shortest cycle – 10: This formula is also used for calculating the last risky day, not the first.

Question No: 14

A woman presents to the clinic with complaints of pelvic pain and irregular menstrual bleeding. What is the most likely diagnosis?

Options:

  • 1.

    Endometriosis

  • 2.

    Uterine fibroids

  • 3.

    Ovarian cyst

  • 4.

    Pelvic inflammatory disease

Solution:

  • Endometriosis is a common cause of pelvic pain and irregular menstrual bleeding.

    Endometriosis is a condition where tissue similar to the endometrium (the lining of the uterus) grows outside the uterus. It can cause pelvic pain, painful periods (dysmenorrhea), and irregular menstrual bleeding, which aligns with the woman's symptoms of pelvic pain and irregular bleeding.

    • Pelvic Pain: This is the hallmark symptom, often described as a deep, crampy pain that occurs during menstruation, but can also be present outside of the menstrual cycle.  
    • Irregular Menstrual Bleeding: Women with endometriosis may experience heavy menstrual bleeding (menorrhagia), spotting between periods, or prolonged periods.
    • Other Symptoms: Endometriosis can also cause infertility, painful intercourse (dyspareunia), and bowel or urinary symptoms in some cases.
    • Pathophysiology: The endometrial-like tissue responds to hormonal changes (estrogen) during the menstrual cycle, leading to inflammation, pain, and the formation of adhesions (scar tissue) in the pelvic cavity.
    • Uterine Fibroids: Fibroids (Leiomyoma) are benign growths in the muscular wall of the uterus. While they can cause pelvic pain and heavy menstrual bleeding, the pain is usually more associated with a bulk effect (pressure) or degeneration of the fibroid, rather than the chronic pain seen in endometriosis. Fibroids often don't cause the specific pattern of pain associated with endometriosis.   
    • Ovarian cysts may cause pelvic pain, especially if they rupture or become torsed (twisted). However, the pain is typically more acute and localized. While some ovarian cysts can cause irregular bleeding, they don't usually cause the chronic pelvic pain and the cyclical nature of pain is seen in endometriosis. Additionally, cysts are less likely to cause the widespread symptoms (like infertility) that can be seen with endometriosis.
    • PID is an infection of the reproductive organs, often caused by untreated sexually transmitted infections. It leads to pain and fever, and the pain is usually more acute, with a more recent onset. PID often causes fever, vaginal discharge, and tenderness during a pelvic exam, which is not a typical presentation for endometriosis. PID typically presents with acute symptoms, whereas endometriosis symptoms are more chronic and cyclical.

Question No: 17

The feeling of fetus movement by mother is known as:

Options:

  • 1.

    Ballottement

  • 2.

    Palmar Sign

  • 3.

    Hegar Sign

  • 4.

    Quickening

Solution:

  • Quickening refers to the first movements of the fetus that the mother can feel, typically occurring between 18 weeks of pregnancy. These movements can be described as flutters, and it is an important milestone in pregnancy.

    • Ballottement: This is a clinical sign that involves a technique where the examiner taps or pushes on the cervix or uterus to feel the fetus move or float, but it refers to the examiner feeling fetal movement, not the mother.
    • Palmar Sign: This refers to a clinical sign of pregnancy where a woman's palm can feel a certain way when placed on her abdomen, indicating the presence of a pregnant uterus, but it is not related to fetal movement.
    • Hegar Sign: This is a clinical sign of pregnancy where there is softening of the lower part of the uterus (isthmus), detectable during a pelvic exam, but it does not refer to fetal movement.

    So, Quickening is the term used for the sensation of fetal movement felt by the mother.
