Question No: 1

A patient's arterial blood gas (ABG) results are as follows:

  • pH: 7.3
  • HCO₃⁻: 14 mEq/L
  • PaCO₂: 30 mmHg
  • PaO₂: 95 mmHg

What is the most likely acid-base disorder? 

Options:

  • 1.

    Metabolic alkalosis with respiratory compensation

  • 2.

    Metabolic acidosis with respiratory compensation

  • 3.

    Metabolic alkalosis 

  • 4.

    Respiratory acidosis 

Solution:

    • What is the "-osis"?
      • The pH is low (7.3), indicating acidosis. This rules out alkalosis-related options (a and c).
    • What is the cause of the acidosis?
      • The HCO₃⁻ (bicarbonate) is low (14 mEq/L).
      • A low HCO₃⁻ suggests metabolic acidosis.
    • Is there compensation?
      • The body compensates for metabolic acidosis by increasing respiratory rate to eliminate CO₂ (which is acidic).
      • Expected PaCO₂ compensation can be estimated using Winter’s formula: Predicted PaCO₂=(1.5×HCO₃⁻) + 8±2

          = (1.5×14)+8 ± 2=29 ±2

    • The actual PaCO₂ is 30 mmHg, which falls within the predicted range (27–31 mmHg), indicating appropriate respiratory compensation.

    Final Diagnosis: Metabolic acidosis with respiratory compensation.

Question No: 2

A patient's arterial blood gas (ABG) results are as follows:

  • pH: 7.6
  • HCO₃⁻: 20 mEq/L
  • PaCO₂: 20 mmHg
  • PaO₂: 95 mmHg

What is the most likely acid-base disorder?

Options:

  • 1.

    Metabolic acidosis

  • 2.

    Metabolic alkalosis

  • 3.

    Respiratory acidosis

  • 4.

    Respiratory alkalosis

Solution:

    • What is the "-osis"?
      • The pH is high (7.6), indicating alkalosis.
      • This rules out acidosis-related options (a and c).
    • What is the cause of the alkalosis?
      • The HCO₃⁻ is low (20 mEq/L), but it is not high enough to be the primary cause of alkalosis.
      • The PaCO₂ is low (20 mmHg), which suggests excessive CO₂ loss (commonly due to hyperventilation).
      • Since the primary abnormality is a decrease in PaCO₂, this confirms respiratory alkalosis.
    • Is there compensation?
      • In respiratory alkalosis, compensation occurs via renal excretion of HCO₃⁻, lowering its level.
      • To determine if the condition is acute or chronic, we apply expected HCO₃⁻ compensation:
        • Acute: HCO₃⁻ decreases by 2 mEq/L per 1 mmHg drop in PaCO₂. Predicted HCO₃⁻=24− (0.2× (40−20)) = 24−4=20
        • Chronic: HCO₃⁻ decreases by 5 mEq/L per 1 mmHg drop in PaCO₂. Predicted HCO₃⁻=24−(0.5× (40−20)) = 24−10=14
      • The measured HCO₃⁻ is 20 mEq/L, which matches the predicted value for acute respiratory alkalosis.

    Final Diagnosis:

    Acute Respiratory Alkalosis (due to uncompensated CO₂ loss).

Question No: 3

A patient's arterial blood gas (ABG) results are as follows:

  • pH: 7.2
  • HCO₃⁻: 30 mEq/L
  • PaCO₂: 80 mmHg
  • PaO₂: 70 mmHg

What is the most likely acid-base disorder?

Options:

  • 1.

    Metabolic acidosis with respiratory compensation

  • 2.

    Acute metabolic acidosis

  • 3.

    Metabolic alkalosis

  • 4.

    Acute respiratory acidosis

Solution:

    • What is the "-osis"?
      • The pH is low (7.2), indicating acidosis.
      • This rules out alkalosis-related options (c).
    • What is the cause of the acidosis?
      • The HCO₃⁻ is elevated (30 mEq/L) rather than decreased, meaning the acidosis is not metabolic.
      • The PaCO₂ is significantly elevated (80 mmHg), suggesting CO₂ retention, which is characteristic of respiratory acidosis.
    • Is there compensation?
      • The kidneys compensate for respiratory acidosis by retaining HCO₃⁻.
      • To determine if this is acute or chronic, we use expected HCO₃⁻ compensation:
        • Acute: HCO₃⁻ increases by 1 mEq/L per 1 mmHg rise in PaCO₂. Predicted HCO₃⁻=24+(0.1× (80−40)) =24+4=28
        • Chronic: HCO₃⁻ increases by 35 mEq/L per 1 mmHg rise in PaCO₂. Predicted HCO₃⁻=24+(0.35× (80−40)) =24+14=38
      • The measured HCO₃⁻ is 30 mEq/L, which is much closer to the acute prediction (28) than the chronic prediction (38).
      • This confirms acute respiratory acidosis rather than chronic compensation.

    Final Diagnosis:

    Acute Respiratory Acidosis (due to acute CO₂ retention).

Question No: 4

A patient's arterial blood gas (ABG) results are as follows:

  • pH: 7.6
  • HCO₃⁻: 44 mEq/L
  • PaCO₂: 52 mmHg
  • PaO₂: 70 mmHg

What is the most likely acid-base disorder?

Options:

  • 1.

    Metabolic acidosis with respiratory compensation

  • 2.

    Metabolic alkalosis with respiratory compensation

  • 3.

    Respiratory alkalosis with metabolic compensation

  • 4.

    Respiratory acidosis with metabolic compensation

Solution:

    • What is the "-osis"?
      • The pH is high (7.6), indicating alkalosis.
      • This rules out acidosis-related options (a and d).
    • What is the cause of the alkalosis?
      • The HCO₃⁻ is significantly elevated (44 mEq/L), suggesting metabolic alkalosis as the primary disturbance.
      • The PaCO₂ is also elevated (52 mmHg), which suggests respiratory compensation rather than a primary respiratory disorder.
    • Is there appropriate compensation?
      • The body compensates for metabolic alkalosis by hypoventilating, which raises PaCO₂.
      • Expected PaCO₂ compensation can be calculated as:
        • For metabolic alkalosis, PaCO₂ should increase by 7 mmHg per 1 mEq/L increase in HCO₃⁻.
        • The increase in HCO₃⁻ is: 44−24= 20
        • Expected increase in PaCO₂: 20 × 0.7=14
        • Predicted PaCO₂: 40+ 14=54 mmHg
        • The patient’s actual PaCO₂ is 52 mmHg, which is within the expected range (±2 mmHg), confirming appropriate respiratory compensation.

    Final Diagnosis: Metabolic Alkalosis with Respiratory Compensation

Question No: 5

A patient's arterial blood gas (ABG) results are as follows:

  • HCO₃⁻: 20 mEq/L
  • PaCO₂: 55 mmHg

What is the most likely acid-base disorder?

Options:

  • 1.

    Metabolic acidosis with respiratory acidosis

  • 2.

    Metabolic alkalosis with respiratory acidosis

  • 3.

    Respiratory alkalosis with metabolic acidosis

  • 4.

    Respiratory acidosis

Solution:

    • What do the bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) and carbon dioxide (PaCO₂) levels suggest?
      • HCO₃⁻ is low (20 mEq/L), which suggests either metabolic acidosis or respiratory alkalosis.
      • PaCO₂ is elevated (55 mmHg), which suggests respiratory acidosis rather than alkalosis.
      • Since PaCO₂ is high, respiratory alkalosis is ruled out.
    • Identifying a Mixed Disorder:
      • Normally, metabolic acidosis is compensated by respiratory alkalosis (low PaCO₂ due to hyperventilation).
      • In this case, PaCO₂ is actually elevated, indicating that respiratory compensation is not occurring.
      • This means the patient has both metabolic acidosis (low HCO₃⁻) and respiratory acidosis (high PaCO₂), confirming a mixed acid-base disorder.
    • Key Finding: Opposing Directions
      • If this was only respiratory acidosis, the kidneys would compensate by increasing HCO₃⁻, not decreasing it.
      • Since HCO₃⁻ is decreased while PaCO₂ is increased, this confirms the presence of two simultaneous acidotic processes—metabolic acidosis and respiratory acidosis.

    Final Diagnosis: Mixed Metabolic Acidosis and Respiratory Acidosis

pCO2- 60 mmHg

pO2- 52 mmHg

HCO3- - 27 mEg/L

Options:

  • 1.

    Metabolic acidosis

  • 2.

    Metabolic alkalosis

  • 3.

    Respiratory acidosis

  • 4.

    Respiratory alkalosis

Solution:

  • The underlying acid-base disorder is respiratory acidosis.

    Interpretation of the ABG:

    i. Step 1: A pH of 7.24 signifies acidosis.

    ii. Step 2: A plasma HCO3- is 27 mEq/L is within normal limits. This is very likely to be due to compensation as it does not reflect the direction of the pH change.

    • Antacids- Milk alkali syndrome

    • Loop or thiazide-type diuretics
    • Sweat losses in cystic fibrosis
    • Gastric losses in achlorhydria Miscellaneous:
    • Bartter's syndrome
    • Gitelman's syndrome

Question No: 10

A patient arrives in the emergency department with diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA). Which nursing diagnosis should be prioritized?

Options:

  • 1.

    Disrupted sleep pattern

  • 2.

    Difficulty maintaining health

  • 3.

    Imbalanced nutrition: Less than body requirements

  • 4.

    Deficient fluid volume

Solution:

    • In DKA, insulin deficiency leads to hyperglycemia, which triggers excessive breakdown of glycogen and increased glucose production. This results in glucose spilling into the urine (glycosuria), causing osmotic diuresis and dehydration. As the condition worsens, nausea and vomiting exacerbate fluid loss, leading to severe dehydration. If untreated, this can cause circulatory collapse and shock, making it the most critical and life-threatening issue to address immediately.
    • Disrupted sleep pattern: While discomfort or symptoms related to DKA may disrupt sleep, this is not a life-threatening concern and is secondary to the immediate need to address fluid volume deficits.
    • Difficulty maintaining health: Challenges with health maintenance may be a contributing factor to the development of DKA, but this is not an immediate concern during the acute phase and would be addressed during long-term management.
    • Imbalanced nutrition: less than body requirements: Nutritional imbalances, often present in DKA due to nausea and vomiting, are important but not as urgent as correcting fluid and electrolyte imbalances to prevent shock and organ failure.

    Key Takeaway: In the acute management of DKA, prioritizing deficient fluid volume is critical to stabilize the patient, prevent circulatory collapse, and manage life-threatening dehydration.

    • The normal anion gap is 8 - 12 mEq/L (optimally <10 mEq/L).

Question No: 16

A 40-year-old man presented with nausea, dizziness, and hyperventilation. His arterial blood gas analysis and serum electrolyte levels revealed metabolic acidosis with a normal anion gap. Which of the following is the possible reason for this condition?

Options:

  • 1.

    Lactic acidosis

  • 2.

    Chronic renal failure

  • 3.

    Renal tubular acidosis  

  • 4.

    Methanol toxicity  

Solution:

    • Metabolic acidosis with a normal anion gap is a feature of renal tubular acidosis.
    • Renal tubular acidosis is a form of hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis which occurs when the renal damage primarily affects tubular function without much effect on glomerular function. The bicarbonate loss leading to acidosis is mirrored by an increase in chloride concentration. Hence the unmeasured anions are unaffected and thus, the anion gap remains within normal limits.
    • The result is a decrease in H+ excretion which is greater than that can be explained by any change in the Glomerular filtration rate (GFR). However, if the GFR is significantly depressed e.g., renal failure, the retention of fixed acids leads to a high anion gap metabolic acidosis.

Question No: 17

A patient with chronic kidney disease came with complaints of vomiting and diarrhea. His blood gas reports show pH = 7.40, HC03- = 23 mEq/L, Na+ = 145 mEq/L, CI- = 100 mEq/L. What is your probable diagnosis?

Options:

  • 1.

    No acid base abnormality

  • 2.

    Respiratory acidosis  

  • 3.

    Metabolic alkalosis  

  • 4.

    High anion gap metabolic acidosis with metabolic alkalosis  

Solution:

  • The probable diagnosis is a high anion gap metabolic acidosis (HAGMA) with metabolic alkalosis.

    Interpretation of the ABG:

    i. Step 1: The pH being 7.4 is within normal limits.

    ii. Step 2: Plasma HCO3- is 23 mEq/L and again is within normal limits.

    At this juncture, we have ruled out options B and C as this particular clinical scenario is not suggestive of a simple acid-base disorder.

    iii. Step 3: Calculating the Anion gap:

          Anion gap = [Na+] - ([CI-] + [HCO3-]) = 145-(100+23) = 22 mEq/L

    • The presence of a high anion gap indicates that there's indeed an acid-base disorder, thus ruling out option A.
    • A high anion gap indicates metabolic acidosis. However, HCO3- levels within normal limits suggest that there is also coexisting metabolic alkalosis in this patient.
    • This can be confirmed by calculating the delta gap.

    Delta gap = Δ AG – ΔНСОз-

    • In this scenario: Delta gap = (22-10) - (24-23) = 11 mEq/L
    • Delta gap is > +6 suggesting the presence of metabolic alkalosis along with HAGMA.
    • If the delta gap is significantly positive (>+6), metabolic alkalosis is present because the rise in the anion gap is more than the fall in bicarbonate. If the delta gap is significantly negative (<6) then hyperchloremic acidosis is usually present because a rise in the anion gap is less than a fall in bicarbonate.
    • Alternatively, this can be confirmed by calculating the delta ratio.
    • Delta ratio = the increase in Anion Gap / the decrease in HCO3-
    • It is used in the presence of a high anion gap metabolic acidosis (HAGMA), to check it is a 'pure' HAGMA or if there is coexistent normal anion gap metabolic acidosis (NAGMA) or metabolic alkalosis.

    Delta ratio values:

    • 1-2 indicates HAGMA
    • < 1 indicates NAGMA + HAGMA
    • > 2 indicates HAGMA + Metabolic alkalosis

    In this scenario,

    Delta ratio = 12/1 = 12

    Thus, the acid-base disorder for the above scenario is HAGMA with metabolic alkalosis.

Question No: 18

A 30-year-old man presents to the emergency department with dyspnea, and altered sensorium. These symptoms are preceded by an asthma attack. What is the probable reason for the patient's presenting symptoms?

    Metabolic acidosis

  • 2.

    Respiratory acidosis

  • 4.

    Respiratory alkalosis

Solution:

    • Asthma attack leads to the narrowing of respiratory passages. This leads to reduced O2 inhalation and reduced CO2 exhalation contributing to the rise of pCO2 in the blood, thereby causing respiratory acidosis.

    • Drugs (anesthetics, morphine, sedatives)
    • Stroke
    • Infection

    Airway:

    • Obstruction
    • Asthma

    Lung parenchyma:

    • Emphysema
    • Pneumoconiosis
    • Bronchitis
    • Adult respiratory distress syndrome
    • Barotrauma

    Neuromuscular:

    • Poliomyelitis
    • Kyphoscoliosis
    • Myasthenia
    • Muscular dystrophies

    Miscellaneous:

    • Obesity
    • Hypoventilation
    • Permissive hypercapnia.

    A case of respiratory acidosis should always be treated with caution and the hypercapnia should be corrected gradually. Rapid correction can result in metabolic alkalemia and alkalization of the CSF can result in seizures.

Question No: 20

A patient is diagnosed with acute renal failure. Which lab finding would the nurse most likely observe?

Options:

  • 1.

    Metabolic acidosis

  • 2.

    Metabolic alkalosis

  • 3.

    Respiratory acidosis

  • 4.

    Respiratory alkalosis 

Solution:

    • In acute renal failure, the kidneys lose their ability to excrete acid-type substances generated by normal metabolism. This results in the accumulation of hydrogen ions, leading to metabolic acidosis. Additionally, impaired renal function disrupts normal buffering mechanisms, further contributing to acid build-up in the body.
    • Metabolic alkalosis: Metabolic alkalosis occurs when there is a loss of hydrogen ions, often due to vomiting, gastric suctioning, or certain electrolyte imbalances (e.g., hypokalemia). These conditions are unrelated to the primary issues seen in acute renal failure.
    • Respiratory acidosis: Respiratory acidosis results from inadequate ventilation, causing carbon dioxide retention and increased acidity. It is associated with lung or respiratory issues, not kidney dysfunction.
    • Respiratory alkalosis: Respiratory alkalosis occurs when there is excessive ventilation, leading to low carbon dioxide levels in the blood (e.g., in hyperventilation). This condition is not linked to acute renal failure.

    Key Takeaway: Metabolic acidosis is a hallmark of acute renal failure due to the kidneys' inability to eliminate acid waste products, making it the expected lab finding in such cases.
